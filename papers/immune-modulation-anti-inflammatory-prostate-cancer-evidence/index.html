    Immune Modulation and Anti-inflammatory Agents in Prostate Cancer: Evidence Map

    Retrieved: 2026-07-04

    Category: Therapies; advanced & recurrent disease; localized treatment; side effects & quality of life
    Scope: Patient-facing review of immune modulation and anti-inflammatory approaches in prostate-cancer management, from approved immunotherapy to investigational vaccines, checkpoint inhibitors, mycobacterial immune modulators, NSAIDs/COX-2 inhibitors, vitamin D, low-dose naltrexone, bromelain, corticosteroids, and inflammation-related trial strategies.

    Not medical advice

    This page summarizes evidence for discussion with clinicians. It does not recommend starting aspirin, NSAIDs, celecoxib, low-dose naltrexone, bromelain, vitamin D, immunotherapy, supplements, or off-label immune-modulating regimens for prostate cancer. These agents can have serious risks, drug interactions, bleeding/cardiovascular effects, immune toxicities, or may delay proven cancer therapy if used outside a supervised plan.

    Patient-facing bottom line

    Question State of the evidence Clinician discussion question
    Is prostate cancer an immunotherapy-responsive cancer? Usually less responsive than melanoma, lung cancer, kidney cancer, and some mismatch-repair-deficient tumors. Most unselected prostate cancers are relatively "immune cold." "Has my tumor been tested for MSI-H/dMMR, TMB-high, CDK12, HRR alterations, PD-L1, or other markers that might affect immunotherapy relevance?"
    Is any immunotherapy approved with survival evidence? Sipuleucel-T improved overall survival by about 4 months in asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC), without clearly delaying radiographic/clinical progression. Availability varies by country. "If I have mCRPC and few symptoms, is sipuleucel-T available and appropriate in my care system?"
    Do checkpoint inhibitors work for most prostate cancer? Not for most unselected patients. Pembrolizumab has low response rates in unselected mCRPC, but can produce meaningful responses in tumors with MSI-H/dMMR or selected high-TMB biology. "Do my genomic results suggest a tumor-agnostic indication for pembrolizumab or a clinical trial?"
    Do combination checkpoint strategies solve the problem? Mixed and mostly investigational. Several phase 3 combinations have not improved overall survival in biomarker-unselected mCRPC, although some combinations show progression-free-survival or subgroup signals. "Is this combination guideline-supported for my biomarker group, or is it mainly a clinical-trial question?"
    Do anti-inflammatory drugs such as aspirin/NSAIDs improve prostate-cancer outcomes? Observational studies are inconsistent; randomized evidence does not support routine use as prostate-cancer treatment. Aspirin/NSAIDs may be indicated for cardiovascular or pain reasons, but cancer-control benefit is unproven. "Do I have a cardiovascular, bleeding, kidney, ulcer, anticoagulant, or perioperative reason to use or avoid aspirin/NSAIDs?"
    What about celecoxib/COX-2 inhibition? STAMPEDE and perioperative biomarker studies do not show clear survival or biomarker benefit sufficient for routine prostate-cancer treatment. Celecoxib has cardiovascular and renal risks. "Is there any cancer-specific reason for celecoxib in my case, or would it only be used for pain/inflammation under usual safety rules?"
    What about vitamin D, bromelain, or low-dose naltrexone? Vitamin D deficiency should be corrected for general health, but high-dose vitamin D has not shown reliable prostate-cancer treatment benefit. Bromelain and low-dose naltrexone are hypothesis-generating, not standard-of-care cancer therapy. "Should my vitamin D be checked/corrected, and could any supplement interact with cancer treatment, anticoagulants, surgery, or pain medications?"
    How should the Dalgleish/Liu IMM-101 case report be interpreted? It is interesting and hypothesis-generating, but it is a six-patient uncontrolled case report with major confounding from standard therapies. It cannot prove that IMM-101, LDN, bromelain, aspirin, or vitamin D controlled cancer. "Is there a registered clinical trial or controlled evidence for this regimen, and would it interfere with proven therapy?"

    1. Why immune modulation is biologically plausible but clinically difficult

    Prostate cancer is shaped by androgen signaling, DNA-repair biology, bone microenvironment, myeloid immune suppression, chronic inflammation, tumor-associated macrophages, regulatory T-cell activity, and therapy-induced immune changes. This makes immune modulation scientifically plausible. However, most prostate cancers have relatively low tumor mutational burden, limited T-cell infiltration, low PD-L1 expression, and an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment compared with cancers where checkpoint inhibitors are broadly effective.

    Practical interpretation: immune modulation is not one idea. It includes:

    • therapeutic vaccines and cellular immunotherapy;
    • checkpoint inhibitors such as PD-1/PD-L1 and CTLA-4 blockade;
    • combinations with androgen-receptor pathway inhibitors, PARP inhibitors, radiotherapy, chemotherapy, or tyrosine-kinase inhibitors;
    • macrophage/myeloid/TGF-β/IL-8 and other microenvironment-directed strategies;
    • anti-inflammatory and repurposed agents such as aspirin, NSAIDs, COX-2 inhibitors, corticosteroids, vitamin D, low-dose naltrexone, and supplements.

    These have very different evidence levels.

    2. Approved or guideline-recognized immunotherapy

    Sipuleucel-T: proven survival signal in a selected mCRPC setting

    Sipuleucel-T is an autologous cellular immunotherapy studied in asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic mCRPC. In the pivotal IMPACT trial, sipuleucel-T reduced risk of death compared with placebo and improved median overall survival:

    • median overall survival: 25.8 vs 21.7 months;
    • hazard ratio for death: 0.78;
    • 36-month survival: 31.7% vs 23.0%;
    • no clear effect on time to objective disease progression.[^sipuleucel]

    Patient interpretation: sipuleucel-T is the clearest example that immune modulation can help selected prostate-cancer patients, but it is not a rapid tumor-shrinking therapy and is most relevant before heavy symptoms or very rapidly progressive disease. Access differs substantially by country.

    Pembrolizumab: biomarker-selected role, not broad unselected use

    In the KEYNOTE-199 phase 2 study of pembrolizumab in treatment-refractory mCRPC, objective response rates were low in unselected cohorts:

    • 5% in PD-L1-positive measurable disease;
    • 3% in PD-L1-negative measurable disease;
    • some responses were durable.[^keynote199]

    The strongest clinical rationale for pembrolizumab is not "prostate cancer" by itself, but tumor biology such as MSI-H/dMMR and, in some settings, high tumor mutational burden. A 2024 analysis reported that among patients with MSI-H/dMMR prostate cancer treated with immune checkpoint blockade, about 45% had RECIST responses and 65% had PSA50 responses, while TMB-high/microsatellite-stable disease looked less reliably responsive.[^msi-tmb]

    Patient interpretation: if considering immunotherapy, the important step is tumor genomic profiling and pathology review, not assuming checkpoint inhibitors work for prostate cancer generally.

    3. Checkpoint-inhibitor combinations: active research, mixed results

    CTLA-4 blockade with ipilimumab

    A phase 3 trial of ipilimumab after radiotherapy in post-docetaxel mCRPC did not meet its primary overall-survival endpoint:

    • median overall survival 11.2 vs 10.0 months;
    • HR 0.85, p=0.053;
    • grade 3–4 immune-related adverse events occurred in 26% with ipilimumab vs 3% with placebo.[^ipilimumab]

    Nivolumab plus ipilimumab has shown activity in CheckMate 650, with objective response rates reported around 25% pre-chemotherapy and 10% post-chemotherapy in an early analysis, but grade 3–4 treatment-related adverse events were common and treatment-related deaths occurred.[^checkmate650]

    Patient interpretation: dual checkpoint blockade is biologically interesting but toxicity is real, and use should generally be trial- or biomarker-driven.

    PD-L1 blockade plus androgen-receptor pathway inhibition

    The IMbassador250 phase 3 trial tested atezolizumab plus enzalutamide versus enzalutamide alone after abiraterone in mCRPC. It did not improve overall survival in an unselected population:

    • OS HR 1.12;
    • p=0.28.[^imbassador250]

    Biomarker analyses suggested immune-active subgroups may exist, but this is not the same as a standard indication for all patients.

    PD-1 plus PARP inhibition

    KEYLYNK-010 tested pembrolizumab plus olaparib in biomarker-unselected, previously treated mCRPC. It did not significantly improve radiographic progression-free survival or overall survival compared with a next-generation hormonal agent:

    • rPFS 4.4 vs 4.2 months;
    • OS 15.8 vs 14.6 months;
    • grade ≥3 treatment-related adverse events 34.6% vs 9.0%.[^keylynk010]

    Patient interpretation: combinations that sound mechanistically plausible still need randomized evidence and biomarker selection.

    Cabozantinib plus atezolizumab

    CONTACT-02 tested cabozantinib plus atezolizumab versus ARPI switch in mCRPC after prior ARPI. Final analysis showed improved progression-free survival but not overall survival:

    • PFS 6.3 vs 4.2 months, HR 0.65;
    • OS 14.8 vs 15.0 months, HR 0.89, p=0.30;
    • grade 3–4 adverse events 56% vs 26%.[^contact02]

    Patient interpretation: a PFS signal may be clinically relevant in some contexts, but lack of OS benefit and higher toxicity require careful discussion.

    4. Therapeutic vaccines and immune adjuvants beyond sipuleucel-T

    PROSTVAC, a viral-vector immunotherapy, had promising phase 2 survival results but failed in a phase 3 trial of asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic mCRPC:

    • median OS 34.4 months with PROSTVAC, 33.2 months with PROSTVAC plus GM-CSF, and 34.3 months with placebo;
    • no OS benefit.[^prostvac]

    This pattern—promising immune signal, then neutral phase 3 result—is common in prostate-cancer immunotherapy.

    Patient interpretation: vaccines may still matter in future combinations or selected disease states, but most non-approved vaccine/adjuvant regimens remain investigational.

    5. The supplied case report: IMM-101 plus anti-inflammatory/immune-modulatory agents

    The paper Terry highlighted is:

    Dalgleish AG, Liu WM. The role of immune modulation and anti-inflammatory agents in the management of prostate cancer: A case report of six patients. Oncology Letters. 2022. DOI 10.3892/ol.2022.13367; PMID 35761946.[^dalgleish-liu]

    What the authors reported

    The paper describes six prostate-cancer patients treated in a named-patient context with IMM-101, a heat-killed whole-cell mycobacterial preparation, alongside conventional treatments and various partner agents:

    • vitamin D3 correction;
    • aspirin;
    • bromelain;
    • low-dose naltrexone (LDN);
    • zoledronic acid;
    • standard prostate-cancer therapies such as hormone therapy, radiotherapy, docetaxel, abiraterone, enzalutamide, radium-223, and cabazitaxel in some cases.

    The authors report PSA declines or slowed PSA doubling time, stable imaging for periods of time, and few side effects attributed to IMM-101/LDN. Their table summarizes six cases and partner agents: zoledronic acid, aspirin, bromelain, LDN, or combinations.

    Why it is interesting

    • It connects a plausible biologic idea—mycobacterial immune activation plus anti-inflammatory modulation—with prostate-cancer disease control.
    • It highlights a research question: whether innate immune stimulation, myeloid modulation, or inflammatory-pathway modulation could make prostate cancer more immune-responsive.
    • It is relevant to patients who are asking about non-standard adjunctive therapies.

    Why it should not be treated as proof of efficacy

    Limitation Why it matters
    Only six patients Case reports can generate hypotheses but cannot estimate treatment effect.
    No control group The natural course of PSA kinetics and disease biology cannot be separated from treatment effect.
    Major confounding by standard therapies Patients also received ADT, radiotherapy, docetaxel, abiraterone, enzalutamide, radium-223, cabazitaxel, and zoledronic acid at different times.
    Multiple simultaneous adjuncts IMM-101, vitamin D, aspirin, bromelain, LDN, and other agents were not isolated from each other.
    Selection and reporting bias Named-patient experiences may overrepresent unusual responders or highly motivated patients.
    Surrogate outcomes PSA changes and scan stability are useful signals, but they do not prove improved survival or quality of life.
    Competing interests The paper discloses patents related to low-dose naltrexone as a cancer therapy.

    Repository interpretation: this case report belongs in the dashboard as a hypothesis-generating signal, not as a treatment recommendation. It supports asking whether controlled trials exist; it does not justify substituting IMM-101/LDN/bromelain for proven prostate-cancer therapy.

    6. Anti-inflammatory and repurposed agents

    Aspirin and non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)

    Epidemiologic studies have explored whether aspirin or NSAIDs reduce prostate-cancer incidence, recurrence, or mortality. Results are inconsistent. Meta-analyses of aspirin exposure after diagnosis have not shown a reliable prostate-cancer-specific mortality benefit.[^aspirin-mortality] Earlier NSAID-risk meta-analyses described evidence as suggestive but not conclusive.[^nsaid-risk]

    Safety issue: aspirin and NSAIDs can cause gastrointestinal bleeding, kidney injury, perioperative bleeding, interactions with anticoagulants/antiplatelets, and cardiovascular risks depending on agent and patient context.

    Patient interpretation: aspirin should be discussed primarily as a cardiovascular/vascular-risk medication unless a clinician is using it for another clear indication. It should not be started solely for prostate-cancer control based on current evidence.

    Celecoxib and COX-2 inhibition

    COX-2 is biologically relevant to inflammation, angiogenesis, and tumor signaling, but randomized clinical evidence has not established celecoxib as routine prostate-cancer therapy.

    In STAMPEDE, adding celecoxib to standard hormone therapy did not improve overall survival in the overall trial population. Long-term survival results reported:

    • SOC + celecoxib HR for death 0.98, p=0.847;
    • SOC + zoledronic acid + celecoxib HR 0.86, p=0.130;
    • conclusion: no overall evidence of improved survival with celecoxib.[^stampedecel]

    Perioperative randomized celecoxib biomarker trials in localized prostate cancer also failed to show convincing biomarker benefit, despite measurable prostate tissue levels.[^celecoxib-preop]

    Patient interpretation: celecoxib is not a standard anti-cancer therapy for prostate cancer. If used for pain/inflammation, cardiovascular, renal, gastrointestinal, and perioperative risks matter.

    Corticosteroids: supportive and treatment-partner role, not simple immune enhancement

    Prednisone/prednisolone is routinely paired with abiraterone to reduce mineralocorticoid excess and is also used with docetaxel/cabazitaxel regimens and for symptom control in some advanced settings. In LATITUDE, abiraterone acetate plus prednisone with ADT improved overall survival in newly diagnosed high-risk metastatic castration-sensitive prostate cancer.[^latitude]

    But prednisone is not being used there as an anti-inflammatory cancer cure. It is part of a regimen that modifies androgen synthesis and manages toxicity.

    Patient interpretation: corticosteroids can be necessary and beneficial in standard regimens, but long-term risks include hyperglycemia, infection risk, bone loss, muscle weakness, mood/sleep effects, and adrenal suppression.

    Vitamin D

    Correcting deficiency is reasonable for bone health and general health, particularly in men receiving ADT who face bone-density and metabolic risks. However, vitamin D supplementation has not shown reliable prostate-cancer treatment benefit in controlled trials. A systematic review/meta-analysis of clinical trials found no significant improvement in PSA change, PSA response proportion, or mortality in controlled trials and concluded high-dose vitamin D should not be recommended as prostate-cancer treatment.[^vitd]

    Patient interpretation: ask whether vitamin D should be measured and corrected to a safe target, but do not treat vitamin D as a proven anti-cancer therapy.

    Low-dose naltrexone and bromelain

    LDN and bromelain appear in the Dalgleish/Liu case report as anti-inflammatory or immune-modulatory adjuncts. LDN has preclinical and mechanistic literature, and the authors discuss TLR-9 and cytokine-modulating hypotheses. Bromelain is also discussed as an anti-inflammatory agent. However, controlled prostate-cancer outcome evidence is insufficient.

    Safety issue: LDN can interfere with opioid pain medicines and perioperative analgesia. Bromelain may have bleeding or drug-interaction concerns, especially around anticoagulants/antiplatelets or surgery.

    Patient interpretation: these are not standard prostate-cancer treatments. They should only be discussed as experimental adjuncts or trial questions, with medication-interaction review.

    7. How to use this evidence in clinician discussion

    Questions for the oncology team

    • Biomarkers: Has my tumor been tested for MSI-H/dMMR, TMB, CDK12, HRR genes such as BRCA1/2, PTEN/PI3K pathway changes, PD-L1, or other immune-relevant markers?
    • Disease state: Am I hormone-sensitive, non-metastatic castration-resistant, metastatic castration-resistant, post-docetaxel, post-ARPI, or biomarker-selected?
    • Approved therapy: Is sipuleucel-T available and appropriate for my disease state and symptom burden?
    • Checkpoint inhibitors: Is pembrolizumab relevant because of a tumor-agnostic biomarker indication, or would it be off-label/clinical-trial only?
    • Trials: Are there trials combining immunotherapy with radiotherapy, PARP inhibition, PSMA radioligand therapy, vaccines, myeloid modulation, or androgen-receptor pathway therapy?
    • Anti-inflammatory drugs: Do I have a separate cardiovascular, pain, arthritis, or post-radiation urinary indication for aspirin/NSAIDs/celecoxib, and what are the bleeding/kidney/cardiac risks?
    • Supplements and LDN: Could vitamin D, bromelain, LDN, or other supplements interfere with surgery, radiation, chemotherapy, anticoagulants, opioids, or clinical-trial eligibility?
    • Evidence threshold: Is the proposed immune/anti-inflammatory strategy supported by randomized prostate-cancer data, a biomarker indication, or only case reports/preclinical rationale?

    Red flags for patient safety

    • Starting aspirin/NSAIDs before biopsy, surgery, or while on anticoagulants without clinician approval.
    • Using high-dose supplements or LDN without disclosing them to the oncology team.
    • Treating PSA improvement from a multi-agent regimen as proof that an unproven adjunct worked.
    • Delaying ADT, radiotherapy, surgery, chemotherapy, radioligand therapy, PARP inhibitor therapy, or other proven treatments to try unproven immune-modulatory regimens.
    • Assuming "anti-inflammatory" means harmless.

    Repository interpretation

    The state of the art is best summarized as:

    1. Proven but narrow: sipuleucel-T has randomized overall-survival evidence in selected mCRPC.
    2. Biomarker-selected: pembrolizumab and checkpoint inhibitors are most credible for MSI-H/dMMR and select high-TMB biology, not for most unselected prostate cancers.
    3. Combination research: checkpoint combinations are active but have produced mixed results; many phase 3 unselected strategies have not improved overall survival.
    4. Anti-inflammatory adjuncts: aspirin/NSAIDs/celecoxib/vitamin D/LDN/bromelain are not established prostate-cancer control therapies. Some may be appropriate for non-cancer indications or deficiency correction.
    5. Case-report signal: IMM-101 plus anti-inflammatory/immune-modulatory agents is hypothesis-generating and worth tracking for trials, but not practice-changing without controlled evidence.

    Selected evidence notes

    Kantoff et al. 2010 — sipuleucel-T IMPACT trial

    • Citation: Kantoff PW, et al. New England Journal of Medicine. 2010.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa1001294; PMID 20818862
    • Evidence type: Phase 3 randomized trial.
    • Population: Asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic mCRPC.
    • Main finding: Overall survival improved by 4.1 months; no clear effect on objective disease progression.
    • Patient relevance: Most established immunotherapy survival signal in prostate cancer.

    Antonarakis et al. 2020 — KEYNOTE-199 pembrolizumab

    • Citation: Antonarakis ES, et al. Journal of Clinical Oncology. 2020.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1200/JCO.19.01638; PMID 31774688
    • Evidence type: Phase 2 multicohort study.
    • Population: Treatment-refractory mCRPC.
    • Main finding: Low objective response rates in unselected/PD-L1-defined cohorts; some durable responses.
    • Patient relevance: Supports biomarker testing rather than broad checkpoint-inhibitor use.

    Kwon et al. 2014 — ipilimumab after radiotherapy

    • Citation: Kwon ED, et al. Lancet Oncology. 2014.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1016/S1470-2045(14)70189-5; PMID 24831977
    • Evidence type: Phase 3 randomized trial.
    • Population: mCRPC after docetaxel.
    • Main finding: Primary OS endpoint not met; immune-related grade 3–4 toxicity increased.
    • Patient relevance: Checkpoint activation can be toxic and is not automatically beneficial.

    Fizazi et al. 2022 — IMbassador250 atezolizumab plus enzalutamide

    • Citation: Fizazi K, et al. Nature Medicine. 2022.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1038/s41591-021-01600-6; PMID 35013615
    • Evidence type: Phase 3 randomized trial.
    • Population: mCRPC after abiraterone.
    • Main finding: No OS improvement in the unselected population; biomarker subgroup signals require further study.
    • Patient relevance: Combination immunotherapy requires evidence and selection.

    Yu et al. 2023 — KEYLYNK-010 pembrolizumab plus olaparib

    • Citation: Yu EY, et al. Journal of Clinical Oncology. 2023.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1200/JCO.23.00233; PMID 37290035
    • Evidence type: Phase 3 randomized trial.
    • Population: Biomarker-unselected, previously treated mCRPC.
    • Main finding: No significant rPFS or OS improvement; higher grade ≥3 treatment-related adverse events.
    • Patient relevance: PARP/immunotherapy combinations are not automatically useful without biomarker context.

    Gulley et al. 2019 — PROSTVAC phase 3

    • Citation: Gulley JL, et al. Journal of Clinical Oncology. 2019.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1200/JCO.18.02031; PMID 30817251
    • Evidence type: Phase 3 randomized vaccine trial.
    • Population: Asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic mCRPC.
    • Main finding: No OS benefit compared with placebo.
    • Patient relevance: Promising vaccine concepts still need phase 3 proof.

    Dalgleish and Liu 2022 — IMM-101 case report

    • Citation: Dalgleish AG, Liu WM. Oncology Letters. 2022.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.3892/ol.2022.13367; PMID 35761946
    • Evidence type: Six-patient case report.
    • Population: Prostate-cancer patients receiving IMM-101 plus conventional and adjunctive therapies.
    • Main finding: Reported PSA/imaging stability signals and few side effects.
    • Limitations: No control group; heavy confounding; multiple simultaneous interventions; patent conflicts disclosed.
    • Patient relevance: Useful for questions and trial tracking, not for proving efficacy.

    James et al. 2017 — STAMPEDE celecoxib survival results

    • Citation: James ND, et al. Journal of Clinical Oncology. 2017.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1200/JCO.2016.69.0677; PMID 28300506
    • Evidence type: Adaptive multiarm randomized platform trial.
    • Population: Hormone-naive advanced/high-risk prostate cancer starting long-term hormone therapy.
    • Main finding: No overall evidence of improved survival with celecoxib.
    • Patient relevance: Strong caution against using celecoxib as routine prostate-cancer therapy.

    Harrison et al. 2017 — pre-prostatectomy celecoxib biomarker trial

    • Citation: Harrison DJ, et al. BJU International. 2017.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1111/bju.13612; PMID 27480340
    • Evidence type: Double-blind randomized pre-prostatectomy study.
    • Population: Localized prostate cancer.
    • Main finding: No effect on apoptosis, prostaglandins, androgen receptor levels, or PSA; one postoperative MI in celecoxib arm.
    • Patient relevance: Biomarker and safety caution for COX-2 inhibition.

    Liu et al. 2019 — aspirin mortality meta-analysis

    • Citation: Liu Y, et al. BioMed Research International. 2019.
    • DOI/PMID: PMID 31073532
    • Evidence type: Systematic review/meta-analysis.
    • Population: Prostate-cancer patients exposed to aspirin before or after diagnosis.
    • Main finding: No significant association between aspirin exposure and prostate-cancer-specific or all-cause mortality in highest-exposure analyses.
    • Patient relevance: Aspirin should not be assumed to improve cancer outcomes.

    Shahvazi et al. 2019 — vitamin D supplementation meta-analysis

    • Citation: Shahvazi S, et al. Hormone and Metabolic Research. 2019.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1055/a-0774-8809; PMID 30522147
    • Evidence type: Systematic review/meta-analysis of clinical trials.
    • Population: Prostate-cancer patients receiving vitamin D supplementation.
    • Main finding: Controlled trials did not show significant benefit in PSA change, PSA response, or mortality.
    • Patient relevance: Correct deficiency for general health; do not treat vitamin D as proven cancer therapy.
