    Nutrition and Prostate Cancer: Evidence Map

    Retrieved: 2026-07-04
    Category: Nutrition
    Scope: diet, dietary patterns, body composition, metabolic health, and selected supplements before diagnosis, after diagnosis, during treatment, and after treatment/survivorship.

    Not medical advice

    This page summarizes evidence for education and clinician discussion. It does not recommend any diet, supplement, fasting program, or treatment choice. Nutrition changes should be discussed with qualified clinicians, especially during active treatment, weight loss, diabetes management, anticoagulant use, kidney disease, frailty, or when supplements may interact with surgery, radiation, ADT, chemotherapy, or other medicines.

    Patient-facing bottom line

    Phase Best-supported conclusion Strength of evidence Practical question for clinician
    Before diagnosis / incidence The strongest evidence is about overall dietary patterns rather than single foods. Western/inflammatory patterns and high dairy/calcium intake are associated with higher risk in some observational syntheses; healthy/plant-forward patterns may be associated with lower risk, but causality is uncertain. Moderate observational evidence; limited RCT evidence for prevention Should I focus on a heart-healthy, plant-forward diet rather than prostate-specific “anti-cancer” claims?
    After diagnosis / active surveillance Post-diagnosis plant-based, Mediterranean, and lower-inflammatory patterns are associated with lower progression risk in observational cohorts. RCTs show mixed results: increased vegetables alone did not reduce progression in MEAL, while an omega-3/low omega-6 intervention changed a biopsy proliferation biomarker in CAPFISH-3. Moderate observational evidence; limited and mixed RCT evidence Are there diet changes that support active surveillance without delaying appropriate treatment if risk changes?
    During treatment / ADT Diet and weight-management interventions may help cardiometabolic risk, weight, insulin resistance, and function during ADT, but evidence is smaller and not primarily about cancer control. Low-to-moderate interventional evidence for metabolic/supportive outcomes Should I have dietitian support for ADT-related weight, glucose, lipids, muscle, and cardiovascular risk?
    After treatment / survivorship Better diet quality and Mediterranean/plant-forward patterns are linked to better outcomes in some survivorship studies, but evidence remains mostly observational. The safest interpretation is general survivorship and cardiometabolic health support. Moderate observational evidence; limited prostate-specific intervention evidence What dietary pattern best supports recovery, cardiovascular health, weight, bowel function, and long-term survivorship?

    Nutrition topics covered

    • Dietary patterns: Mediterranean, plant-based, healthy diet indices, Western/inflammatory patterns.
    • Single foods/nutrients: dairy/calcium, tomato sauce/lycopene, fish/omega-3 and omega-6 balance.
    • Active surveillance interventions: vegetable-rich diet counseling, omega-3/low omega-6 diet plus fish oil, weight-loss diet/exercise programs.
    • ADT/metabolic support: low-carbohydrate diet plus walking, diet/exercise behavior programs.
    • Survivorship diet quality: diet after diagnosis, progression, recurrence, cancer-specific mortality, and all-cause mortality.

    Evidence synthesis by patient journey

    1. Before diagnosis: incidence and risk of developing prostate cancer

    The most patient-relevant message is that diet evidence is less definitive than claims often suggest. A 2025 systematic review of prospective cohorts and randomized trials found that among men without prostate cancer at baseline, plant-based and healthy dietary patterns were generally associated with lower total prostate cancer risk, while results varied across studies. A 2024 umbrella review emphasized that few modifiable risk factors are firmly established.

    For specific dietary exposures, Western dietary patterns were associated with increased prostate cancer risk in a 2016 meta-analysis. Dairy and calcium showed modest positive associations with total prostate cancer risk in a 2015 cohort meta-analysis. Lycopene has long been studied, but a Cochrane review concluded evidence was insufficient for recommending lycopene supplementation for prostate cancer prevention.

    Interpretation: a plant-forward, minimally processed, cardiometabolic-health diet is reasonable to discuss, but single-food or supplement claims should be treated cautiously.

    2. After diagnosis: progression, recurrence, and mortality

    Post-diagnosis evidence increasingly favors overall dietary patterns. A 2024 JAMA Network Open cohort found higher plant-based diet scores after diagnosis were associated with lower risk of disease progression among men with nonmetastatic prostate cancer. A 2021 active surveillance cohort found higher Mediterranean diet adherence was associated with lower risk of grade group progression.

    Older Health Professionals Follow-Up Study data suggested post-diagnosis fish and tomato sauce intake were inversely associated with progression. More recent systematic reviews of post-diagnosis diet and lifestyle in prostate cancer survivors report that plant foods, healthier patterns, and minimizing inflammatory/hyperinsulinemic patterns are promising, but findings are not perfectly consistent.

    Key caution: many post-diagnosis diet findings are observational. Men who eat healthier diets may also differ in healthcare access, exercise, weight, smoking, comorbidities, screening intensity, and treatment choices.

    3. Active surveillance and treatment period

    The best-known active surveillance RCT, MEAL, successfully increased vegetable intake but did not significantly reduce cancer progression over 24 months. This is important because it pushes against simplistic “eat more vegetables to stop progression” claims.

    CAPFISH-3 tested a high omega-3, low omega-6 diet with fish oil in men on active surveillance and found a favorable change in Ki-67, a biopsy proliferation biomarker, over one year. This is promising but should not be overinterpreted as proof of better long-term cancer outcomes.

    In overweight/obese men on active surveillance, PALS tested a structured diet and exercise weight-loss program and primarily targeted obesity/metabolic biomarkers rather than definitive prostate cancer outcomes. In men starting ADT, CAPS1 and IDEA-P suggest diet/lifestyle interventions can improve or support metabolic and functional outcomes, but these are supportive-care findings, not proven anti-cancer effects.

    4. After treatment has ended: survivorship

    Survivorship reviews suggest better overall diet quality and Mediterranean-style patterns may be associated with improved prognosis in cancer survivors, including prostate cancer, but prostate-specific intervention evidence remains limited. Nutrition after treatment should be framed around recovery, cardiometabolic health, bowel health, muscle preservation, weight stability or safe weight loss, and supporting tolerance of ongoing therapies if any.

    Interpretation: after treatment, nutrition is best treated as survivorship support and risk-factor management rather than as a substitute for surveillance or clinician-directed treatment.

    Selected papers

    Incidence / before diagnosis

    Dietary Patterns in Prostate Cancer Prevention and Management

    • Citation: Systematic review. European Urology. 2025.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1016/j.eururo.2025.07.017; PMID 40835500
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40835500/
    • Date: 2025 Dec
    • Category: Nutrition; Screening & Early Detection; Active Surveillance
    • Evidence type: Systematic review of prospective cohort studies and randomized clinical trials.
    • Population: 63 studies: 49 cohort studies and 14 RCT reports.
    • Patient relevance: Broadest recent evidence map of diet patterns across prevention and management.
    • Main finding: Healthy, plant-based, Mediterranean, and lower-inflammatory patterns were generally more favorable; RCT evidence was more limited and less consistent.
    • Limitations: Dietary measurement heterogeneity, observational confounding, variable endpoints, and limited long-term RCT outcome data.
    • Questions for clinician: Which dietary pattern supports overall health and prostate cancer surveillance without overclaiming cancer-control benefit?

    Risk factors for prostate cancer: umbrella review of prospective observational studies and Mendelian randomization analyses

    • Citation: PLOS Medicine. 2024.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1371/journal.pmed.1004362; PMID 38489391
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38489391/
    • Date: 2024 Mar
    • Category: Nutrition; Screening & Early Detection
    • Evidence type: Umbrella review of prospective observational meta-analyses and Mendelian randomization analyses.
    • Population: Meta-analyses and MR studies of prostate cancer incidence risk factors.
    • Patient relevance: Helps separate plausible modifiable factors from less-certain associations.
    • Main finding: Few modifiable risk factors are firmly established; diet findings should be interpreted cautiously.
    • Limitations: Umbrella review depends on quality of underlying meta-analyses and MR assumptions.
    • Questions for clinician: Which diet claims are strong enough to affect personal decisions, and which are only hypothesis-generating?

    A Western Dietary Pattern Increases Prostate Cancer Risk

    • Citation: Fabiani R, Minelli L, Bertarelli G, Bacci S. Nutrients. 2016.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.3390/nu8100626; PMID 27754328
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27754328/
    • Date: 2016 Oct 12
    • Category: Nutrition; Screening & Early Detection
    • Evidence type: Systematic review and meta-analysis of observational dietary-pattern studies.
    • Population: 12 observational studies.
    • Patient relevance: Addresses broad diet pattern rather than isolated nutrients.
    • Main finding: Western dietary pattern was associated with increased prostate cancer risk; “healthy pattern” was not clearly associated with lower risk.
    • Limitations: Observational studies, pattern definitions vary, residual confounding possible.
    • Questions for clinician: Should reducing Western-pattern foods be prioritized for general and prostate health?

    Dairy products, calcium, and prostate cancer risk

    • Citation: Aune D, Navarro Rosenblatt DA, Chan DSM, et al. American Journal of Clinical Nutrition. 2015.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.3945/ajcn.113.067157; PMID 25527754
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25527754/
    • Date: 2015 Jan
    • Category: Nutrition; Screening & Early Detection
    • Evidence type: Systematic review and meta-analysis of cohort studies.
    • Population: 32 prospective studies.
    • Patient relevance: Common question about dairy/calcium intake and prostate cancer risk.
    • Main finding: Higher total dairy, milk, low-fat milk, cheese, and dietary calcium intakes were modestly associated with increased total prostate cancer risk.
    • Limitations: Observational data, small effect sizes, confounding, and uncertainty about causality and individual dietary needs.
    • Questions for clinician: How should calcium, dairy, bone health, kidney stone risk, and prostate-cancer concerns be balanced?

    Lycopene for the prevention of prostate cancer

    • Citation: Ilic D, Forbes KM, Hassed C. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews. 2011.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1002/14651858.CD008007.pub2; PMID 22071840
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22071840/
    • Date: 2011 Nov 9
    • Category: Nutrition; Screening & Early Detection
    • Evidence type: Cochrane systematic review.
    • Population: Lycopene prevention studies.
    • Patient relevance: Evaluates a common tomato/lycopene supplement claim.
    • Main finding: Evidence was insufficient to support lycopene supplementation for prostate cancer prevention.
    • Limitations: Limited eligible trials, uncertainty about supplement vs food sources, older evidence base.
    • Questions for clinician: Is tomato-rich food reasonable as part of a healthy diet, while avoiding high-dose supplement claims?

    After diagnosis: progression, recurrence, and mortality

    Diet after diagnosis and the risk of prostate cancer progression, recurrence, and death

    • Citation: Chan JM, Holick CN, Leitzmann MF, et al. Cancer Causes & Control. 2006.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1007/s10552-005-0413-4; PMID 16425098
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16425098/
    • Date: 2006 Mar
    • Category: Nutrition; Active Surveillance; Side Effects & Quality of Life
    • Evidence type: Prospective cohort study after diagnosis.
    • Population: 1,202 men with localized/regional prostate cancer in the Health Professionals Follow-Up Study.
    • Patient relevance: Directly studies diet after diagnosis and progression outcomes.
    • Main finding: Higher fish and tomato sauce consumption were inversely associated with prostate cancer progression in this cohort.
    • Limitations: Observational, older treatment/surveillance context, self-reported diet, possible confounding.
    • Questions for clinician: Are fish and tomato-rich foods reasonable within a broader cardiometabolic diet plan?

    Plant-Based Diets and Disease Progression in Men With Prostate Cancer

    • Citation: Loeb S, et al. JAMA Network Open. 2024.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.9053; PMID 38691361
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38691361/
    • Date: 2024 May 1
    • Category: Nutrition; Active Surveillance; Side Effects & Quality of Life
    • Evidence type: Longitudinal observational cohort study.
    • Population: Men with nonmetastatic prostate cancer in the CaPSURE diet and lifestyle substudy.
    • Patient relevance: Modern postdiagnosis evidence on plant-based dietary patterns and progression.
    • Main finding: Higher postdiagnostic plant-based diet scores were associated with lower risk of prostate cancer progression.
    • Limitations: Observational, diet self-report, healthy-user confounding possible, not proof that diet prevents progression.
    • Questions for clinician: Would a plant-forward diet be appropriate given weight, protein needs, diabetes, bowel function, and treatment plan?

    Adherence to the Mediterranean diet and grade group progression in localized prostate cancer

    • Citation: Gregg JR, et al. Cancer. 2021.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1002/cncr.33182; PMID 33411364
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33411364/
    • Date: 2021 Mar 1
    • Category: Nutrition; Active Surveillance
    • Evidence type: Prospective active-surveillance cohort.
    • Population: 410 men with newly diagnosed prostate cancer on active surveillance.
    • Patient relevance: Directly relevant to men choosing active surveillance.
    • Main finding: Higher Mediterranean diet adherence was associated with lower risk of grade group progression.
    • Limitations: Observational, single cohort, progression definitions and follow-up duration matter.
    • Questions for clinician: Is a Mediterranean-style diet appropriate during active surveillance, and what monitoring remains essential?

    Effect of a behavioral intervention to increase vegetable consumption: MEAL randomized clinical trial

    • Citation: Parsons JK, et al. JAMA. 2020.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1001/jama.2019.20207; PMID 31935026
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31935026/
    • Date: 2020 Jan 14
    • Category: Nutrition; Active Surveillance
    • Evidence type: Randomized clinical trial.
    • Population: 478 men aged 50–80 with early-stage prostate cancer on active surveillance.
    • Patient relevance: High-quality test of a vegetable-rich diet intervention in early-stage prostate cancer.
    • Main finding: The intervention increased vegetable intake but did not significantly reduce cancer progression over 24 months.
    • Limitations: Single dietary target, 24-month duration, early-stage population, may not test all healthy dietary patterns.
    • Questions for clinician: What should be concluded from a negative RCT, and should diet goals focus on overall health rather than cancer control alone?

    High omega-3, low omega-6 diet with fish oil for men on active surveillance: CAPFISH-3

    • Citation: Aronson WJ, et al. Journal of Clinical Oncology. 2025.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1200/JCO.24.00608; PMID 39671538
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39671538/
    • Date: 2025 Mar
    • Category: Nutrition; Active Surveillance
    • Evidence type: Phase II randomized clinical trial.
    • Population: 100 men with Grade Group 1 or 2 prostate cancer on active surveillance.
    • Patient relevance: Tests a specific diet plus fish oil strategy in active surveillance.
    • Main finding: The intervention produced a favorable change in Ki-67 proliferation index over one year compared with control.
    • Limitations: Biomarker endpoint, relatively small trial, not proof of lower metastasis, mortality, or long-term progression.
    • Questions for clinician: Are fish oil capsules appropriate or risky given bleeding risk, medications, cardiovascular history, and treatment plans?

    Treatment period, ADT, and metabolic support

    PALS: diet and exercise in overweight and obese men on active surveillance

    • Citation: Prostate Cancer Active Lifestyle Study. Cancer. 2024.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1002/cncr.35241; PMID 38353455
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38353455/
    • Date: 2024 Jun 15
    • Category: Nutrition; Exercise; Active Surveillance
    • Evidence type: Randomized controlled trial.
    • Population: Overweight/obese men with prostate cancer on active surveillance.
    • Patient relevance: Tests a practical lifestyle program targeting weight and metabolic biomarkers.
    • Main finding: Structured diet and exercise achieved weight loss and improved selected obesity/metabolic biomarkers; the main focus was not definitive cancer-control outcomes.
    • Limitations: Combined diet/exercise intervention, short duration, biomarker-focused endpoints.
    • Questions for clinician: Would safe weight loss improve metabolic health without risking muscle loss or treatment readiness?

    Low-carbohydrate diet plus walking during ADT: CAPS1 randomized controlled trial

    • Citation: Freedland SJ, et al. Prostate Cancer and Prostatic Diseases. 2019.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1038/s41391-019-0126-5; PMID 30664736
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30664736/
    • Date: 2019 Sep
    • Category: Nutrition; Exercise; Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Side Effects & Quality of Life
    • Evidence type: Randomized controlled trial.
    • Population: Men initiating ADT.
    • Patient relevance: ADT can worsen insulin resistance, weight, lipids, and body composition.
    • Main finding: Low-carbohydrate diet plus walking improved weight and some metabolic markers, but trial stopped early and primary endpoint was not definitively met at 6 months.
    • Limitations: Small sample due to early stopping, combined diet/exercise intervention, metabolic rather than cancer-control endpoints.
    • Questions for clinician: Is carbohydrate restriction safe and appropriate given diabetes medications, weight, kidney function, and cardiovascular risk?

    Group-mediated exercise and dietary intervention during ADT: IDEA-P

    • Citation: Focht BC, et al. Annals of Behavioral Medicine. 2018.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1093/abm/kax002; PMID 29684136
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29684136/
    • Date: 2018 Apr 19
    • Category: Nutrition; Exercise; Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Side Effects & Quality of Life
    • Evidence type: Pilot randomized trial.
    • Population: 32 men undergoing ADT.
    • Patient relevance: Tests a behavioral diet/exercise support model during ADT.
    • Main finding: Feasible with favorable adherence and no serious intervention-related adverse events; suggested benefits for mobility, body composition, and strength.
    • Limitations: Pilot size, short follow-up, combined diet and exercise, not powered for cancer outcomes.
    • Questions for clinician: Would a structured group or coaching program improve adherence during ADT?

    After treatment / survivorship

    Effect of postdiagnosis diet and lifestyle on clinical outcomes in prostate cancer survivors

    • Citation: Systematic review. Journal of Urology. 2026.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1097/JU.0000000000004855; PMID 41270186
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41270186/
    • Date: 2026 Apr
    • Category: Nutrition; Side Effects & Quality of Life; Active Surveillance
    • Evidence type: Systematic review of clinical trials and cohort studies.
    • Population: Men diagnosed with prostate cancer; studies of progression, recurrence, prostate-cancer-specific mortality, PSA kinetics, and related outcomes.
    • Patient relevance: Most directly aligned with survivorship and secondary prevention decisions.
    • Main finding: Several studies suggest plant foods, healthier dietary/lifestyle patterns, and minimizing inflammatory or hyperinsulinemic foods may be associated with better outcomes, but not all studies agree.
    • Limitations: Heterogeneous exposures and outcomes; mixture of observational and trial evidence; conclusions are cautious.
    • Questions for clinician: Which diet and lifestyle targets are sensible for survivorship without implying guaranteed recurrence prevention?

    The Role of Diet in Prognosis among Cancer Survivors

    • Citation: Castro-Espin C, Agudo A. Nutrients. 2022.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.3390/nu14020348; PMID 35057525
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35057525/
    • Date: 2022 Jan 14
    • Category: Nutrition; Side Effects & Quality of Life
    • Evidence type: Systematic review and meta-analysis of dietary patterns and diet interventions in cancer survivors.
    • Population: 35 prospective cohort studies and 14 RCTs across cancer survivor populations, including prostate cancer.
    • Patient relevance: Places prostate cancer survivorship in broader cancer nutrition evidence.
    • Main finding: Better overall diet quality was associated with improved survival in several survivor groups; Mediterranean diet adherence was associated with lower mortality risk in colorectal and prostate cancer survivors.
    • Limitations: Multi-cancer evidence, observational dominance, heterogeneity, and limited prostate-specific RCTs.
    • Questions for clinician: Should survivorship nutrition follow a Mediterranean-style, high-quality diet pattern unless contraindicated?

    The Facts about Food after Cancer Diagnosis

    • Citation: Schwedhelm C, et al. Nutrients. 2020.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.3390/nu12082345; PMID 32764484
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32764484/
    • Date: 2020 Aug 5
    • Category: Nutrition; Side Effects & Quality of Life
    • Evidence type: Systematic review of prospective cohort studies after cancer diagnosis.
    • Population: 29 prospective studies, including 8 prostate cancer studies.
    • Patient relevance: Addresses common post-diagnosis diet questions across survivorship.
    • Main finding: Healthier diet quality, vegetables/fiber, and high-quality protein appeared beneficial; Western diet and high saturated fat were associated with worse outcomes in some studies.
    • Limitations: Observational evidence, multi-cancer synthesis, risk of self-made/web-based diet misuse.
    • Questions for clinician: How can diet support adequate protein, muscle, bowel health, and cardiometabolic risk after treatment?

    Clinician discussion checklist

    • Which phase am I in: screening/risk reduction, active surveillance, surgery/radiation, ADT/systemic therapy, or survivorship?
    • Do I need referral to an oncology dietitian, especially if losing weight, gaining weight on ADT, diabetic, frail, or using supplements?
    • Are there foods/supplements that could interact with anticoagulants, surgery, radiation side effects, ADT, chemotherapy, kidney disease, or diabetes medications?
    • Should the focus be Mediterranean-style, plant-forward, high-fiber diet quality rather than single supplements?
    • How should calcium and dairy decisions balance prostate-cancer uncertainty with bone health, especially if on ADT?
    • If considering fish oil or omega-3 supplements, what are the bleeding, medication, and cardiovascular considerations?
    • What outcomes should be tracked: weight, waist, muscle/strength, lipids, glucose/HbA1c, blood pressure, bowel symptoms, fatigue, and treatment tolerance?

    Repository interpretation

    This evidence strengthens the Nutrition category by adding a structured distinction between:

    1. Incidence prevention claims — diet pattern evidence is suggestive but not definitive; avoid single-food/supplement certainty.
    2. Post-diagnosis associations — plant-forward, Mediterranean, and low-inflammatory patterns look promising but are mostly observational.
    3. Active surveillance trials — increased vegetables alone did not reduce progression in MEAL; omega-3/low omega-6 diet changed a biomarker in CAPFISH-3 but needs longer-term clinical outcomes.
    4. Treatment/survivorship support — nutrition is strongest as cardiometabolic, weight, muscle, bowel, and quality-of-life support, especially during ADT and survivorship.
