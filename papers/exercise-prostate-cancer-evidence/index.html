    Exercise and Prostate Cancer: Evidence Map

    Retrieved: 2026-07-04
    Category: Exercise
    Scope: exercise/physical activity before diagnosis, after diagnosis, during treatment, and after treatment.

    Not medical advice

    This page summarizes evidence for education and clinician discussion. It does not recommend any exercise program, cancer screening decision, or treatment choice. Exercise plans should be discussed with qualified clinicians, especially after surgery, during radiation or systemic therapy, with cardiovascular disease, severe fatigue, anemia, pain, falls risk, or possible bone metastases.

    Patient-facing bottom line

    Phase Best-supported conclusion Strength of evidence Practical question for clinician
    Before diagnosis / incidence Physical activity is clearly beneficial for general health, but prostate-cancer-specific incidence findings are mixed. Meta-analyses suggest little or no association with total prostate cancer incidence; some studies suggest lower risk of advanced/aggressive or fatal disease. Moderate for "mixed/uncertain total incidence"; lower-to-moderate for advanced/fatal subtypes Does my overall health profile make exercise especially important regardless of uncertain prostate-cancer incidence effects?
    After diagnosis Observational cohorts consistently associate higher post-diagnosis activity, especially brisk walking/vigorous activity, with lower all-cause mortality and possibly lower prostate-cancer-specific mortality/progression. Moderate observational evidence; not proof of causation What activity level is safe for my risk group and treatment plan?
    During active treatment Randomized trials and reviews support aerobic and resistance exercise for fatigue, physical fitness, strength, body composition, and quality of life during radiation and ADT. These trials generally evaluate symptoms/function rather than cancer control. Moderate interventional evidence for function/QoL; limited for survival/cancer-control endpoints Should I be referred to physiotherapy or an exercise physiologist during treatment?
    After treatment / survivorship Exercise rehabilitation and pelvic-floor muscle training can help selected post-treatment problems, especially urinary incontinence after prostatectomy and fatigue/function after treatment. Moderate for pelvic-floor training; moderate/variable for broader rehabilitation outcomes What post-treatment exercise or pelvic-floor program is appropriate, supervised, and timed safely?

    Exercise types covered

    • Walking / brisk walking: most clinically approachable activity in cohort evidence after diagnosis.
    • Vigorous activity: associated with better survival/progression outcomes in some cohorts, but subject to healthy-user and reverse-causation issues.
    • Aerobic training: RCT evidence during radiation/ADT supports fitness and fatigue/QoL outcomes.
    • Resistance training: RCT evidence supports muscle mass, strength, and function, especially relevant during ADT.
    • Combined aerobic + resistance programs: common in ADT/survivorship trials and exercise oncology guidance.
    • Impact/loading exercise: potentially relevant to ADT-related bone loss, but only when safely prescribed and supervised; not appropriate for unsupervised use with osteoporosis, falls risk, pain, or possible bone metastases.
    • High-intensity interval training (HIIT): studied in active surveillance and advanced-disease mechanistic settings; should be individualized rather than copied from generic fitness programs.
    • Isometric or modified loading: may be considered by specialist teams for selected patients with bone metastases, based on imaging and fracture-risk assessment; not a do-it-yourself strategy.
    • Pelvic-floor muscle exercise: specific post-prostatectomy urinary incontinence evidence; supervised programs appear more effective than unsupervised.

    Evidence synthesis by patient journey

    1. Before diagnosis: incidence and risk of developing prostate cancer

    The most prostate-specific systematic reviews do not support a simple claim that exercise prevents all prostate cancer. Benke et al. (2018) found the highest vs lowest overall physical activity had a summary relative risk close to null for total prostate cancer incidence, while estimates for advanced disease trended lower but were not definitive. Liu et al. (2011) found a modest inverse association when combining observational studies, but results varied by design and measurement.

    Large general-population cohort syntheses (Moore et al. 2016; Garcia et al. 2023) show strong benefits of physical activity for many chronic diseases and several cancers, but prostate cancer is not among the most consistently reduced cancer sites. Orsini et al. (2009) is a useful cohort suggesting lifetime activity, occupational activity, and walking/bicycling may be associated with lower prostate cancer incidence and mortality, but observational confounding remains important.

    Interpretation: exercise should be viewed as strongly beneficial for overall health and treatment resilience, but not as a reliable prostate-cancer prevention strategy.

    2. After diagnosis: progression, prostate-cancer mortality, and overall survival

    The most directly relevant patient evidence is observational. Kenfield et al. (2011), Richman et al. (2011), Friedenreich et al. (2016), Wang et al. (2017), and the 2026 post-diagnosis meta-analysis all point in the same broad direction: men who are more physically active after diagnosis tend to have better overall survival, and in several analyses lower prostate-cancer-specific mortality or progression.

    Key caution: these are not exercise-prescription trials powered for mortality. Men who feel well enough to exercise may differ in disease severity, comorbidity, treatment response, and health behaviors. Still, the consistency makes physical activity a reasonable topic for clinician discussion after diagnosis.

    3. During active treatment: radiation and ADT

    RCTs and systematic reviews support exercise as supportive care. Segal et al. (2009) tested resistance or aerobic exercise during radiation. Horgan and O'Donovan (2018) summarized RCTs during radiotherapy. For ADT, resistance and combined programs are especially relevant because ADT can worsen body composition, muscle, strength, metabolic risk, fatigue, and bone health. Houben et al. (2023), Alibhai et al. (2024), and Toohey et al. (2022) support feasibility and functional benefits of exercise programs in men on ADT.

    Interpretation: exercise during treatment is best framed as symptom/function/risk-factor support, not as a proven anti-cancer treatment.

    4. After treatment has ended: recovery and survivorship

    Post-treatment evidence is strongest for specific functional outcomes. Pelvic-floor muscle exercise is relevant after radical prostatectomy; Baumann et al. (2022) found supervised pelvic-floor muscle exercise more effective than unsupervised exercise for urinary incontinence recovery. Broader exercise oncology guidance (Campbell et al. 2019) supports exercise to improve fatigue, physical function, and quality of life across cancer survivorship, with tailoring to cancer type, treatment effects, and safety risks.

    Interpretation: after treatment, exercise is mainly a recovery, function, quality-of-life, and long-term health strategy. Evidence for recurrence/survival benefit is promising but mostly observational.

    Selected papers

    Incidence / before diagnosis

    Physical activity in relation to risk of prostate cancer: a systematic review and meta-analysis

    • Citation: Benke IN, Leitzmann MF, Behrens G, Schmid D. Annals of Oncology. 2018.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1093/annonc/mdy073; PMID 29788165
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29788165/
    • Date: 2018 May 1
    • Category: Exercise; Screening & Early Detection
    • Evidence type: Systematic review and meta-analysis of observational studies.
    • Population: 48 cohort and 24 case-control studies; 151,748 prostate cancer cases.
    • Patient relevance: Best prostate-specific synthesis for whether physical activity affects prostate cancer incidence.
    • Main finding: Overall physical activity was close to null for total prostate cancer incidence; estimates for advanced disease trended lower but were not definitive.
    • Limitations: Observational evidence, heterogeneous activity measures, detection/screening bias, and residual confounding.
    • Questions for clinician: Should exercise be emphasized for general health and treatment resilience rather than as prostate cancer prevention?

    Does physical activity reduce the risk of prostate cancer? A systematic review and meta-analysis

    • Citation: Liu Y, Hu F, Li D, Wang F, Zhu L, Chen W. European Urology. 2011.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1016/j.eururo.2011.07.007; PMID 21802197
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21802197/
    • Date: 2011 Nov
    • Category: Exercise; Screening & Early Detection
    • Evidence type: Systematic review and meta-analysis of cohort and case-control studies.
    • Population: 19 cohort and 24 case-control studies; 88,294 prostate cancer cases.
    • Patient relevance: Earlier broad synthesis suggesting possible modest risk reduction.
    • Main finding: Combined observational data suggested total physical activity was associated with lower prostate cancer risk, but findings were inconsistent.
    • Limitations: Older evidence base, variable definitions of activity, observational design, possible publication and detection biases.
    • Questions for clinician: How should older positive evidence be balanced against newer meta-analyses showing weaker prostate-specific incidence associations?

    Non-occupational physical activity and risk of cardiovascular disease, cancer and mortality outcomes

    • Citation: Garcia L, Pearce M, Abbas A, et al. British Journal of Sports Medicine. 2023.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1136/bjsports-2022-105669; PMID 36854652
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36854652/
    • Date: 2023 Aug
    • Category: Exercise; Nutrition/metabolic health context
    • Evidence type: Dose-response meta-analysis of large prospective studies.
    • Population: 196 articles; 94 cohorts; more than 30 million participants across multiple outcomes.
    • Patient relevance: Places prostate cancer in the broader context of exercise benefits for mortality, cardiovascular disease, and cancer.
    • Main finding: Non-occupational physical activity was associated with lower risks for many major outcomes; prostate-cancer-specific incidence benefit is less certain than for several other cancers.
    • Limitations: General-population evidence, not prostate-cancer-patient-specific; pooled studies vary in measurement and adjustment.
    • Questions for clinician: What level of activity is appropriate for cardiovascular and cancer survivorship health even if prostate cancer incidence effects are uncertain?

    Association of Leisure-Time Physical Activity With Risk of 26 Types of Cancer in 1.44 Million Adults

    • Citation: Moore SC, Lee IM, Weiderpass E, et al. JAMA Internal Medicine. 2016.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1001/jamainternmed.2016.1548; PMID 27183032
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27183032/
    • Date: 2016 Jun 1
    • Category: Exercise; Screening & Early Detection
    • Evidence type: Pooled prospective cohort analysis.
    • Population: 1.44 million adults from 12 US and European cohorts.
    • Patient relevance: Large-scale context for cancer prevention claims.
    • Main finding: Leisure-time physical activity was associated with lower risk of several cancers; prostate cancer was not one of the clearest protective associations.
    • Limitations: Self-reported activity, general population, screening/detection bias may differ by activity level.
    • Questions for clinician: Should exercise be discussed as broad cancer/cardiometabolic prevention rather than prostate-specific prevention?

    A prospective study of lifetime physical activity and prostate cancer incidence and mortality

    • Citation: Orsini N, Bellocco R, Bottai M, et al. British Journal of Cancer. 2009.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1038/sj.bjc.6605404; PMID 19861965
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19861965/
    • Date: 2009 Dec 1
    • Category: Exercise; Screening & Early Detection
    • Evidence type: Prospective cohort study.
    • Population: 45,887 Swedish men aged 45–79 years.
    • Patient relevance: Useful cohort on lifetime activity, walking/bicycling, occupational activity, incidence, and mortality.
    • Main finding: Higher lifetime total activity was associated with lower prostate cancer incidence; walking/bicycling and occupational activity showed inverse associations.
    • Limitations: Observational design, activity self-report, confounding, older cohort and screening context.
    • Questions for clinician: Does a history of activity change risk discussion, or mainly support general health advice?

    After diagnosis: progression and survival

    Physical activity and survival after prostate cancer diagnosis in the Health Professionals Follow-Up Study

    • Citation: Kenfield SA, Stampfer MJ, Giovannucci E, Chan JM. Journal of Clinical Oncology. 2011.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1200/JCO.2010.31.5226; PMID 21205749
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21205749/
    • Date: 2011 Feb 20
    • Category: Exercise; Side Effects & Quality of Life; Active Surveillance
    • Evidence type: Prospective cohort study after diagnosis.
    • Population: 2,705 men with nonmetastatic prostate cancer in the Health Professionals Follow-Up Study.
    • Patient relevance: Directly addresses activity after diagnosis and survival.
    • Main finding: Higher post-diagnosis physical activity was associated with lower all-cause mortality and lower prostate-cancer mortality; brisk walking and vigorous activity were notable.
    • Limitations: Observational; healthier men may be more active; not a randomized exercise prescription.
    • Questions for clinician: What level and type of walking or vigorous activity is safe after diagnosis and during the chosen management plan?

    Physical activity after diagnosis and risk of prostate cancer progression: CaPSURE

    • Citation: Richman EL, Kenfield SA, Stampfer MJ, et al. Cancer Research. 2011.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1158/0008-5472.CAN-10-3932; PMID 21610110
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21610110/
    • Date: 2011 Jun 1
    • Category: Exercise; Active Surveillance; Risk Stratification & Staging
    • Evidence type: Prospective cohort analysis.
    • Population: 1,455 men with clinically localized prostate cancer.
    • Patient relevance: Focuses on progression, an outcome relevant after diagnosis and during surveillance/treatment decision-making.
    • Main finding: Brisk walking after diagnosis was associated with lower risk of prostate cancer progression; walking pace appeared important.
    • Limitations: Observational, relatively few progression events, activity self-report, possible residual confounding.
    • Questions for clinician: Is brisk walking appropriate during active surveillance or after local treatment, and are there safety limitations?

    Physical Activity and Survival After Prostate Cancer

    • Citation: Friedenreich CM, Wang Q, Neilson HK, et al. European Urology. 2016.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1016/j.eururo.2015.12.032; PMID 26774959
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26774959/
    • Date: 2016 Oct
    • Category: Exercise; Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Side Effects & Quality of Life
    • Evidence type: Prospective cohort study.
    • Population: 830 stage II–IV incident prostate cancer cases in Alberta, Canada, followed up to 17 years.
    • Patient relevance: Evaluates activity type, intensity, timing, survival, and recurrence/progression.
    • Main finding: Higher post-diagnosis activity was associated with better survival outcomes; activity timing and intensity mattered.
    • Limitations: Observational, self-reported activity, heterogeneous stages and treatments.
    • Questions for clinician: How should activity be adapted by stage, treatment, symptoms, and recurrence risk?

    Recreational Physical Activity in Relation to Prostate Cancer-specific Mortality Among Men with Nonmetastatic Prostate Cancer

    • Citation: Wang Y, Jacobs EJ, Gapstur SM, et al. European Urology. 2017.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1016/j.eururo.2017.06.037; PMID 28711382
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28711382/
    • Date: 2017 Dec
    • Category: Exercise; Side Effects & Quality of Life
    • Evidence type: Prospective cohort study.
    • Population: Men with nonmetastatic prostate cancer in a US prospective cohort.
    • Patient relevance: Examines recreational activity, including walking, before and after diagnosis in relation to prostate-cancer-specific mortality.
    • Main finding: Higher recreational physical activity before and after diagnosis was associated with lower prostate-cancer-specific mortality in some analyses.
    • Limitations: Observational design, self-reported activity, potential reverse causation and confounding.
    • Questions for clinician: Is a walking-based plan a safe baseline while more individualized exercise advice is arranged?

    Post-diagnosis physical activity in relation to mortality among prostate cancer survivors: a systematic review and meta-analysis

    • Citation: Benker P, Hamann A, Leitzmann MF, Stein MJ. Cancer Causes & Control. 2026.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1007/s10552-026-02197-2; PMID 42348026
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42348026/
    • Date: 2026 Jun 25
    • Category: Exercise; Side Effects & Quality of Life
    • Evidence type: Systematic review and meta-analysis.
    • Population: 10 studies, more than 50,144 participants, 28,044 deaths.
    • Patient relevance: Current synthesis of post-diagnosis activity and mortality in prostate cancer survivors.
    • Main finding: Higher post-diagnosis physical activity was associated with lower all-cause mortality and lower prostate-cancer-specific mortality.
    • Limitations: Based on observational studies; activity measures and timing vary; causality not established.
    • Questions for clinician: What individualized, sustainable exercise target is reasonable after diagnosis?

    During treatment and ADT

    Randomized controlled trial of resistance or aerobic exercise in men receiving radiation therapy for prostate cancer

    • Citation: Segal RJ, Reid RD, Courneya KS, et al. Journal of Clinical Oncology. 2009.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1200/JCO.2007.15.4963; PMID 19064985
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19064985/
    • Date: 2009 Jan 20
    • Category: Exercise; Localized Treatment; Side Effects & Quality of Life
    • Evidence type: Randomized controlled trial.
    • Population: 121 men receiving radiotherapy for prostate cancer, with or without ADT.
    • Patient relevance: Direct trial of aerobic vs resistance exercise during radiation.
    • Main finding: Exercise improved selected outcomes such as fatigue, fitness, strength, and quality of life measures compared with usual care; resistance and aerobic exercise had different strengths.
    • Limitations: Moderate sample size; endpoints are supportive-care outcomes, not survival or cancer control.
    • Questions for clinician: During radiation, should exercise emphasize aerobic training, resistance training, or both?

    The Impact of Exercise during Radiation Therapy for Prostate Cancer on Fatigue and Quality of Life

    • Citation: Horgan S, O'Donovan A. Journal of Medical Imaging and Radiation Sciences. 2018.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1016/j.jmir.2018.02.056; PMID 32074040
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32074040/
    • Date: 2018 Jun
    • Category: Exercise; Localized Treatment; Side Effects & Quality of Life
    • Evidence type: Systematic review and meta-analysis of RCTs.
    • Population: Men with prostate cancer undergoing radiation therapy.
    • Patient relevance: Synthesizes exercise effects on radiation-related fatigue and quality of life.
    • Main finding: Exercise during radiation can help fatigue and quality of life, although effects vary by program and study.
    • Limitations: Limited number of trials, heterogeneous exercise prescriptions, supportive-care endpoints.
    • Questions for clinician: What supervised or home exercise plan is safe during radiotherapy?

    Resistance Exercise Training Increases Muscle Mass and Strength in Prostate Cancer Patients on ADT

    • Citation: Houben LHP, Overkamp M, van Kraaij P, et al. Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise. 2023.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1249/MSS.0000000000003095; PMID 36534950
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36534950/
    • Date: 2023 Apr 1
    • Category: Exercise; Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Side Effects & Quality of Life
    • Evidence type: Randomized exercise trial.
    • Population: Men with prostate cancer receiving ADT.
    • Patient relevance: ADT commonly worsens muscle, strength, body composition, and metabolic health.
    • Main finding: Resistance exercise training increased muscle mass and strength in men on ADT; protein supplementation did not appear to be the main driver compared with exercise.
    • Limitations: Functional/supportive endpoints rather than cancer-control endpoints; program supervision and adherence affect applicability.
    • Questions for clinician: Should resistance training be part of an ADT supportive-care plan, and is referral to an exercise professional appropriate?

    Home-based versus supervised group exercise in men with prostate cancer on ADT

    • Citation: Alibhai SMH, Papadopoulos E, Mina DS, et al. Journal of Geriatric Oncology. 2024.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1016/j.jgo.2023.101646; PMID 37976654
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37976654/
    • Date: 2024 Jan
    • Category: Exercise; Side Effects & Quality of Life; Advanced & Recurrent Disease
    • Evidence type: Multicentre randomized controlled trial and economic analysis.
    • Population: Men with prostate cancer on ADT.
    • Patient relevance: Compares practical delivery models: home-based vs supervised group exercise.
    • Main finding: Both home and group models were used for aerobic and resistance training; the study informs feasibility, adherence, outcomes, and costs.
    • Limitations: Delivery model and local resources may affect generalizability; not powered for cancer survival endpoints.
    • Questions for clinician: Is supervised group exercise necessary, or could a structured home program be sufficient and safe?

    Exercise Adherence in Men with Prostate Cancer Undergoing ADT: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

    • Citation: Toohey K, Hunter M, Paterson C, Mortazavi R, Singh B. Cancers. 2022.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.3390/cancers14102452; PMID 35626058
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35626058/
    • Date: 2022 May 16
    • Category: Exercise; Side Effects & Quality of Life; Advanced & Recurrent Disease
    • Evidence type: Systematic review and meta-analysis.
    • Population: Men with prostate cancer undergoing ADT in exercise intervention studies.
    • Patient relevance: Helps assess whether ADT exercise programs are realistic and beneficial.
    • Main finding: Exercise interventions in men on ADT are feasible and can improve physiological outcomes; adherence and patient experience are central.
    • Limitations: Heterogeneity in programs, supervision, adherence measurement, and outcomes.
    • Questions for clinician: What program design would maximize adherence for my circumstances?

    After treatment / survivorship

    Clinical exercise interventions in prostate cancer patients — systematic review of randomized controlled trials

    • Citation: Baumann FT, Zopf EM, Bloch W. Supportive Care in Cancer. 2012.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1007/s00520-011-1271-0; PMID 21989678
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21989678/
    • Date: 2012 Feb
    • Category: Exercise; Side Effects & Quality of Life
    • Evidence type: Systematic review of randomized controlled trials.
    • Population: Prostate cancer patients during treatment and aftercare.
    • Patient relevance: Broad review of exercise interventions addressing common problems such as fatigue, incontinence, function, and quality of life.
    • Main finding: RCTs generally show positive physiological and psychological effects of exercise interventions in prostate cancer care.
    • Limitations: Older review; trials vary widely in intervention type, treatment phase, and outcome measures.
    • Questions for clinician: Which exercise goals matter most now: fatigue, continence, strength, aerobic fitness, mood, or return to activities?

    Supervised pelvic floor muscle exercise after radical prostatectomy — systematic review and meta-analysis

    • Citation: Baumann FT, Reimer N, Gockeln T, et al. Disability and Rehabilitation. 2022.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1080/09638288.2021.1937717; PMID 34550846
    • URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34550846/
    • Date: 2022 Sep
    • Category: Exercise; Side Effects & Quality of Life; Localized Treatment
    • Evidence type: Systematic review and meta-analysis.
    • Population: Men after radical prostatectomy.
    • Patient relevance: Directly relevant to post-surgical urinary incontinence recovery.
    • Main finding: Supervised pelvic-floor muscle exercise was more effective than unsupervised exercise for improving urinary incontinence after radical prostatectomy.
    • Limitations: Program details and timing vary; outcomes depend on adherence and baseline severity.
    • Questions for clinician: Should pelvic-floor physiotherapy start before or after surgery, and should it be supervised?

    Practical patient resource: Five exercises to strengthen your pelvic floor, UT Health San Antonio MD Anderson Cancer Center. This is a practical exercise-description page rather than a research paper; use it as a discussion aid with a treating clinician or pelvic-floor physiotherapist, especially after prostate surgery.

    • Date: 2019 Nov
    • Category: Exercise; Guidelines & Care Pathways; Side Effects & Quality of Life

    • Population: Cancer survivors broadly, with relevance to prostate cancer survivorship and treatment side effects.
    • Patient relevance: Provides practical exercise-oncology framing and safety principles after diagnosis and treatment.
    • Main finding: Exercise is generally safe for cancer survivors when appropriately tailored and can improve fatigue, quality of life, physical function, anxiety, and depressive symptoms.
    • Limitations: Not prostate-only; individual contraindications and treatment-specific risks require clinician input.
    • Questions for clinician: Should I be referred to an accredited exercise physiologist or physiotherapist with oncology experience?

    Mechanistic Q&A: myokines and tumour-suppressive serum after exercise

    Q: Has a larger version of the 12-week myokine/serum tumour-suppression trial been done?

    Short answer: not yet in the same form. The 2022 study by Dieli-Conwright/colleagues was a very small mechanistic study in 10 men with prostate cancer on ADT. It tested whether a 12-week exercise-based intervention changed resting serum myokines and whether post-intervention serum suppressed growth of the DU145 prostate-cancer cell line. It is best treated as hypothesis-generating rather than clinical proof that exercise slows prostate cancer through this mechanism.

    The closest published expansion is an INTERVAL-GAP4 mechanistic substudy in 25 men with metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer. It used a randomized exercise-versus-self-directed-control context, measured myokines after 6 months, and found increased SPARC/OSM signals and reduced DU145 growth with post-intervention serum. This is larger and randomized, but still a small mechanistic substudy.

    A separate acute high-intensity interval exercise study in 9 trained men with advanced prostate cancer found short-term rises in myokines and suppression of DU145 growth when exposed to post-exercise serum. The MYEX protocol plans mechanistic comparisons of aerobic versus resistance exercise effects on myokines, extracellular vesicles, and prostate cancer cell lines in 32 patients, but it is not a large outcomes trial.

    The much larger related trial is INTERVAL-GAP4 — a multicentre phase III trial of intense exercise in metastatic prostate cancer with estimated enrolment of 866 and overall survival as the primary endpoint. However, it is not simply a larger version of the serum/cell-line myokine experiment, and definitive clinical outcome results have not yet been posted.

    Patient-facing interpretation: exercise can change circulating factors in men with prostate cancer, and small lab studies suggest post-exercise serum can suppress prostate cancer cell-line growth. But this mechanism has not yet been proven in a large clinical trial to slow prostate cancer progression or improve survival.

    • Original mechanistic study: Myokine Expression and Tumor-Suppressive Effect of Serum after 12 wk of Exercise in Prostate Cancer Patients on ADT. PMID 34559721; DOI 10.1249/MSS.0000000000002783.
    • Closest published mechanistic expansion: Exercise in advanced prostate cancer elevates myokine levels and suppresses in-vitro cell growth. PMID 35152272.
    • Acute HIIT mechanistic study: PMID 36446945.
    • MYEX protocol: PMID 38951803.
    • INTERVAL-GAP4 protocol: PMID 29764892; ClinicalTrials.gov NCT02730338.
    • INTERVAL-GAP4 feasibility/implementation report: PMID 41561488.

    Adjacent cancer evidence: structured exercise after chemotherapy

    Structured Exercise after Adjuvant Chemotherapy for Colon Cancer

    • Citation: Courneya KS, Vardy JL, O'Callaghan CJ, et al.; CHALLENGE Investigators. New England Journal of Medicine. 2025.
    • DOI/PMID: DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa2502760; PMID 40450658
    • URL: PubMed abstract
    • Date: 2025 Jul 3
    • Category: Exercise; Survivorship; Adjacent cancer evidence
    • Evidence type: Phase III multicentre randomized controlled trial.
    • Population: Colon cancer survivors after adjuvant chemotherapy, not prostate cancer patients.

    PCRI / Rob Newton video notes: precision exercise medicine in prostate cancer

    Source discussed by Terry: Robert Newton, PhD, PCRI 2025 talk, The Power Of Exercise For Prostate Cancer.
    Repository framing: the talk is useful because it shifts the patient question from “Should I be active?” to “What kind of exercise medicine should be prescribed for this stage, treatment, risk profile, and goal?”

    What “precision exercise medicine” means

    Newton’s central message is that exercise is not one medicine. Different forms and doses can have different goals:

    Exercise target Possible clinical goal Patient caution
    General movement / walking reduce sedentary time; improve overall health and treatment resilience any movement is usually better than none, but symptoms and safety limits still matter
    Resistance training maintain or rebuild muscle, strength, function, and treatment tolerance, especially during ADT needs correct technique and progression; frailty, pain, surgery recovery, or metastases may require supervision
    Aerobic / interval training cardiorespiratory fitness, fatigue, metabolic health, possible immune/perfusion effects intensity should be individualized; not all patients can start with vigorous exercise
    Impact/loading exercise bone-density stimulus, particularly relevant to ADT-related bone loss not for unsupervised use in osteoporosis, falls risk, bone pain, or suspected/known bone metastases
    Pelvic-floor training urinary continence recovery after prostatectomy timing and supervision should be discussed with the urologist/physiotherapist
    Isometric/modified loading selected advanced/metastatic cases under specialist supervision requires imaging-informed fracture-risk assessment

    The practical implication is that a generic target such as “150 minutes per week plus resistance training” is a starting point, not a precision prescription. A patient on ADT with low muscle mass, a patient preparing for prostatectomy, a patient with bone metastases, and a patient receiving radiation may need very different programs.

    Prehabilitation before prostatectomy

    Newton emphasizes exercise before primary treatment — especially before prostatectomy — as a way to improve physical and mental resilience. This is often called prehabilitation or neoadjuvant exercise. The proposed goals include better fitness for anesthesia, improved muscle and body composition, reduced systemic inflammation, and potentially smoother recovery.

    Patient-facing interpretation: this is not a reason to delay indicated treatment, but if surgery is scheduled weeks away, the waiting period may be an opportunity to ask about supervised prehabilitation, pelvic-floor preparation, strength, cardiovascular fitness, weight/body composition, and recovery planning.

    Questions for clinician:

    • Is there enough time before surgery to start a safe prehabilitation program?
    • Should pelvic-floor physiotherapy start before prostatectomy?
    • Are there lifting, catheter, wound, hernia, cardiovascular, or continence-related limits?
    • What outcomes should be tracked: walking capacity, strength, fatigue, continence preparation, body composition, or anxiety?

    ADT: muscle, bone, fat, metabolic risk, and intermittent recovery

    ADT can worsen muscle mass, strength, fat mass, bone mineral density, fatigue, metabolic health, sexual function, mood, and cardiovascular risk. Newton’s talk places special emphasis on low muscle mass / sarcopenia as a practical risk marker because it can affect frailty, treatment tolerance, diabetes/cardiovascular risk, and overall resilience.

    A key assessment issue is that body weight or BMI can miss the problem: a man can look “normal weight” or overweight but still have very low muscle mass. Newton specifically highlights DEXA/body-composition assessment as one way to measure muscle, fat, and bone health. This should be interpreted clinically, not as a stand-alone consumer fitness metric.

    Evidence examples:

    • Resistance training in men on ADT can improve muscle mass and strength; see Houben et al. above and related ADT exercise trials.
    • Timing of exercise may matter when men start ADT: PMID 32020032.
    • Exercise mode may matter for bone: resistance training plus impact/loading stimulus may be different from resistance training alone. See Exercise Mode Specificity for Preserving Spine and Hip Bone Mineral Density in Prostate Cancer Patients, PMID 30395051.

    Patient-facing interpretation: for men on ADT, exercise should not be framed only as “cardio.” A resistance-focused plan may be central, especially when muscle loss, frailty, falls risk, bone density, fatigue, or metabolic health are concerns. Protein/energy adequacy may matter when the goal is muscle gain or preservation, but higher protein intake should be discussed with a clinician or dietitian, especially with kidney disease or other medical restrictions.

    Radiation exercise priming: promising but not routine everywhere

    Newton discusses short aerobic exercise immediately before radiation as a possible way to increase tumour blood flow/oxygenation, reduce hypoxia, activate immune factors, and potentially improve radiation biology. This is a promising exercise-priming concept, not a universal standard of care.

    Evidence examples:

    • Exercise modulation of tumour perfusion and hypoxia to improve radiotherapy response in prostate cancer, PMID 32632128.
    • ERADICATE protocol: Exercise medicine as adjunct therapy during radiation for cancer of the prostate to improve treatment efficacy, PMID 39875858.

    Patient-facing interpretation: do not independently exercise immediately before a radiation fraction without the radiation oncology team’s agreement. Bladder-filling protocols, fatigue, cardiovascular risk, falls risk, urinary symptoms, and treatment logistics all matter.

    Questions for radiation oncology:

    • Is any exercise before radiation appropriate in my centre’s workflow?
    • Would it interfere with bladder/rectal preparation or positioning?
    • Is the goal fatigue reduction, fitness, treatment tolerance, or tumour oxygenation/perfusion research?
    • Are there trials or exercise-oncology services linked to my treatment centre?

    Bone metastases: avoid both extremes

    The older advice for men with bone metastases was often “avoid activity.” Newton argues that blanket rest can worsen weakness, frailty, and quality of life. The opposite extreme — unsupervised loading of painful or unstable bone lesions — is also unsafe.

    Balanced interpretation: metastatic bone disease should trigger imaging-informed, supervised, modified exercise, not automatic inactivity and not do-it-yourself heavy loading. Depending on lesion location and stability, programs may exercise unaffected regions, avoid high-risk movements, use machines or supported positions, and monitor pain and function.

    Newton also describes specialist-supervised isometric loading around selected bone lesions. This should be treated as an emerging/specialist practice, not a general patient instruction.

    Questions for clinician/exercise professional:

    • Do my scans show bone metastases, osteoporosis, or fracture-risk areas that change exercise choices?
    • Which bones or movements should be protected?
    • Can I safely train unaffected limbs or muscle groups?
    • Should I see an oncology physiotherapist or accredited exercise physiologist experienced with metastatic disease?
    • What symptoms — new focal bone pain, night pain, neurological symptoms, falls, weakness — should stop exercise and trigger urgent review?

    Mechanisms Newton highlights

    The current evidence is strongest for function, fatigue, strength, body composition, and quality of life. Mechanistic evidence is promising but should not be overstated as proven survival benefit. Newton’s proposed mechanisms include:

    • myokines released from muscle, including IL-6, irisin, decorin, OSM, SPARC, IL-15, and IL-10;
    • adipokines/fat signalling, where higher fat and lower muscle may shift circulating signals in an unfavourable direction;
    • immune activation, including natural killer cells and memory T-cell signalling;
    • reduced systemic inflammation and oxidative stress;
    • androgen-receptor and hormone-related signalling changes;
    • tumour vascularization/perfusion and hypoxia changes;
    • insulin, growth factor, and sex-steroid signalling;
    • possible epigenetic, genetic, and telomere-related effects.

    Key evidence anchor: Exercise-induced myokines and their effect on prostate cancer, PMID 34158658.

    Active surveillance, HIIT, and mental health

    Newton and Moyad discuss active surveillance and a Canadian high-intensity interval training study. The important patient point is not just possible cancer biology; it is also anxiety, fear of progression, stress, self-esteem, fatigue, and quality of life.

    Evidence example: A Randomized Trial of the Effects of Exercise on Anxiety, Fear of Cancer Progression and Quality of Life in Prostate Cancer Patients on Active Surveillance, PMID 35179044.

    Patient-facing interpretation: exercise may help men tolerate the uncertainty of active surveillance, but it does not replace PSA testing, MRI, biopsy schedules, or treatment escalation when clinically indicated.

    Sexual function and patient-priority outcomes

    Newton emphasizes that patients often care deeply about urinary continence and sexual function, sometimes more than clinicians expect. The current page already covers pelvic-floor/continence evidence; it should also flag sexual function evidence.

    Evidence example: Exercise and Psychosexual Education to Improve Sexual Function in Men With Prostate Cancer: A Randomized Clinical Trial, JAMA Network Open 2025, DOI 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2025.0413, PMID 40072437.

    Patient-facing interpretation: sexual function, intercourse satisfaction, fatigue, mood, continence, strength, and treatment tolerance are legitimate exercise outcomes to discuss — not secondary or trivial concerns.

    Practical prescription issues from the Q&A

    The Q&A section includes practical advice about lifting frequency, whole-body resistance training, machines/free weights/bands, breathing, stretching, protein, creatine, sleep, and cold plunges. These are useful discussion prompts, but the website should avoid turning them into one-size-fits-all instructions.

    Cautious takeaways:

    • resistance training generally needs sufficient stimulus to maintain or grow muscle;
    • beginners or medically complex patients may be safer starting with supervised machines rather than free weights;
    • exhaling during exertion and avoiding breath-holding may matter for blood pressure/hernia risk;
    • sleep is central to recovery;
    • protein adequacy matters when trying to preserve or build muscle, but kidney disease and other conditions change what is safe; this should be balanced against Valter Longo's FMD/protein-IGF-1/mTOR hypothesis, which argues for caution with high animal-protein intake in some contexts but does not remove the need to prevent sarcopenia during ADT or older age;
    • creatine may be reasonable for some people but should be discussed with clinicians, especially with kidney issues or medication complexity;
    • cold-water immersion after lifting may not enhance muscle adaptation and may blunt some exercise signals, but this is a lower-priority issue than doing the exercise safely and consistently.

    Expanded clinician discussion checklist

    • Which phase am I in: active surveillance, pre-surgery preparation, surgery recovery, radiation, ADT/systemic therapy, metastatic disease, or survivorship?
    • Is my main exercise goal muscle, bone, fatigue, continence, sexual function, mood, metabolic health, treatment tolerance, cardiovascular health, or possible cancer-control endpoints?
    • Should I be referred to an accredited exercise physiologist, oncology physiotherapist, pelvic-floor physiotherapist, dietitian, or psychologist?
    • Do I need DEXA or another body-composition/bone-density assessment because of ADT, frailty, weight loss, GLP-1 medication, falls risk, or low muscle mass?
    • If on ADT, should resistance training be prioritized over aerobic exercise because of muscle loss, sarcopenia, or frailty risk?
    • Is supervised impact/loading exercise appropriate for bone density, or unsafe because of osteoporosis, pain, falls risk, or metastases?
    • If I have bone metastases, which lesions are stable or unstable, and which movements should be avoided or modified?
    • During radiation, is exercise priming being studied or used at this centre, and would it interfere with bladder/rectal preparation?
    • During active surveillance, could exercise help anxiety, fear of progression, fatigue, and health markers while I continue standard monitoring?
    • How should nutrition, protein intake, sleep, and recovery support the exercise goal?
    • What symptoms should prompt stopping exercise and contacting the clinical team?

    Repository interpretation

    This evidence strengthens the Exercise category by adding a structured distinction between:

    1. Incidence prevention claims — mixed and should be stated cautiously.
    2. Post-diagnosis prognosis associations — promising but mainly observational.
    3. Supportive-care exercise during treatment — stronger randomized evidence for fatigue, fitness, strength, function, and quality of life.
    4. Precision exercise medicine — emerging framework: exercise type, timing, intensity, supervision, body-composition assessment, bone safety, and treatment phase matter.
    5. Post-treatment rehabilitation — strongest for targeted problems such as urinary incontinence after prostatectomy and general survivorship function.
