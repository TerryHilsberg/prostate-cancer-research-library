    Clinician Roles in Prostate Cancer Care

    Educational research support — not medical advice

    This page is a patient-perspective guide to who may be involved in prostate cancer diagnosis and treatment. Roles vary by country, hospital, public/private system, cancer stage, and individual circumstances. It is not a substitute for advice from clinicians who know the details of a case.

    Prostate cancer care is often multidisciplinary: different clinicians contribute different expertise at different points in the pathway. A practical goal for a patient is to understand who is responsible for each decision, when a second opinion or multidisciplinary review is useful, and how information flows between clinicians.

    At-a-glance role map

    Clinician / professional Main role Common points in the pathway Patient-facing questions
    General practitioner (GP) / primary care doctor First assessment, PSA testing context, referrals, long-term care coordination Before diagnosis; after treatment; survivorship What does my PSA trend mean in context? Who should I be referred to and how urgently? Who coordinates follow-up?
    Urologist / urologic surgeon Prostate-focused specialist; evaluates PSA/MRI/biopsy need; performs or arranges biopsy; discusses surgery and surveillance Suspicion/diagnosis; biopsy; localized disease treatment choices; active surveillance Do I need MRI or biopsy? What biopsy route is recommended? Is active surveillance reasonable? What are the surgery trade-offs?
    Radiation oncologist Specialist in radiotherapy options such as external beam radiation, stereotactic radiotherapy, and brachytherapy Localized, locally advanced, recurrent, and sometimes metastatic settings What radiation options fit this risk group? How do urinary, bowel, sexual, and fatigue side effects compare? Is hormone therapy combined with radiation?
    Medical oncologist Specialist in systemic drug treatment, especially hormone therapy, chemotherapy, androgen-receptor pathway inhibitors, radioligand therapy, immunotherapy, and clinical trials Advanced, metastatic, recurrent, castration-resistant, or complex high-risk disease Is systemic therapy needed now? What is the goal of treatment? What sequencing and side-effect monitoring matter? Are trials relevant?
    Radiologist / nuclear medicine physician Interprets MRI, CT, bone scan, PSMA PET/CT, and other imaging; nuclear medicine may deliver radioligand therapies in some systems Diagnostic workup; staging; recurrence detection; treatment monitoring What does the scan show and what are its limits? Would PSMA PET/CT change management?
    Pathologist Examines biopsy or surgical tissue; reports cancer type, Grade Group/Gleason pattern, tumor extent, margins, and other features After biopsy; after prostatectomy What exactly did the pathology show? Is a second pathology review useful? Were margins, grade, or adverse features reported?
    Prostate cancer specialist nurse / cancer nurse coordinator Education, navigation, symptom triage, treatment preparation, side-effect support, connecting services Across the pathway Who do I contact with symptoms or treatment questions? What practical preparation is needed? What support services are available?
    Radiation therapist / dosimetrist / medical physicist Plans and delivers radiation treatment safely and accurately under the radiation oncologist's prescription During radiotherapy How is treatment planned and checked? What should I report during treatment?
    Physiotherapist / pelvic-floor physiotherapist Pelvic-floor training, continence rehabilitation, exercise support, fatigue and functional recovery Before/after surgery; during/after radiation or systemic therapy Should pelvic-floor training start before treatment? What recovery plan is realistic?
    Dietitian / exercise physiologist Nutrition, weight, muscle, metabolic health, treatment side-effect support Before, during, and after treatment; especially with hormone therapy or frailty risk How can I maintain strength and nutrition? Are supplements safe with treatment?
    Psychologist / counsellor / social worker Distress, uncertainty, relationship/sexual concerns, financial/work supports, family communication Any stage, especially diagnosis and treatment decisions What support is available for anxiety, decision stress, sexual/relationship issues, or practical burdens?
    Palliative care specialist Symptom control, quality of life, advance care planning, support alongside active cancer treatment Advanced disease; complex pain/symptoms; high supportive-care needs Can symptom control be improved while cancer treatment continues? What supports exist at home?

    How roles change across the pathway

    1) Before diagnosis: deciding whether further testing is needed

    Often involved:

    • GP / primary care doctor — places PSA in context: age, prior PSA values, urinary symptoms, infection/prostatitis possibilities, recent procedures/ejaculation/exercise, medications, family history, and comorbidities.
    • Urologist — assesses whether MRI, repeat PSA, risk calculator use, or biopsy is appropriate.
    • Radiologist — interprets prostate MRI when used before biopsy.

    Useful discussion points:

    • Is PSA being repeated before referral or biopsy?
    • Are there reversible PSA confounders to address first?
    • Would MRI meaningfully change the biopsy decision?
    • If biopsy is suggested, why now rather than monitoring or repeating tests?

    2) Biopsy and diagnosis

    Often involved:

    • Urologist — performs or arranges biopsy, explains transperineal vs transrectal route, infection prevention, bleeding/urinary-retention risk, anaesthesia or sedation, and follow-up.
    • Radiologist — supports MRI-targeted biopsy planning when MRI lesions are present.
    • Pathologist — determines whether cancer is present and reports Grade Group/Gleason pattern, cancer volume in cores, and other features.
    • Specialist nurse / coordinator — often helps with preparation instructions, post-biopsy symptoms, and results navigation.

    Useful discussion points:

    • Is the biopsy systematic, MRI-targeted, or both?
    • What route is being used and why?
    • What symptoms after biopsy require urgent contact?
    • Will the pathology be reviewed in a multidisciplinary meeting?

    3) If cancer is found: risk grouping and staging

    Often involved:

    • Urologist — explains grade, PSA, stage, number of positive cores, cancer volume, and initial management options.
    • Radiation oncologist — provides an independent view of radiotherapy options and side-effect trade-offs.
    • Radiologist / nuclear medicine physician — interprets staging imaging such as MRI, CT, bone scan, or PSMA PET/CT where appropriate.
    • Pathologist — provides tissue findings; occasionally a second pathology review is useful when results are borderline or decisions are high-stakes.
    • Multidisciplinary team (MDT) — in many systems, complex or higher-risk cases are discussed by several specialists together.

    Useful discussion points:

    • What risk group is this: very-low, low, intermediate, high, locally advanced, recurrent, or metastatic?
    • Is more imaging needed before choosing treatment?
    • Is active surveillance, surgery, radiation, or systemic treatment being considered?
    • Has the case been reviewed by both surgical and radiation specialists?

    4) Localized treatment decisions

    For cancer apparently confined to the prostate or nearby tissues, a patient may need input from more than one specialty because different specialists offer different treatment modalities.

    Often involved:

    • Urologist / urologic surgeon — surgery, active surveillance, biopsy follow-up, urinary function issues.
    • Radiation oncologist — external beam radiation, stereotactic body radiotherapy, brachytherapy, radiation plus short- or long-course hormone therapy where indicated.
    • Medical oncologist — sometimes involved for very high-risk or complex cases, especially where systemic intensification or trials are relevant.
    • Pelvic-floor physiotherapist — continence and sexual-function rehabilitation planning, especially around prostatectomy.
    • Specialist nurse, psychologist, sexual health clinician, dietitian, exercise physiologist — preparation and side-effect support.

    Useful discussion points:

    • What are the realistic cancer-control outcomes for each option in this risk group?
    • What are the urinary, sexual, bowel, fatigue, and hormonal side-effect profiles?
    • How experienced is the treating team with the proposed technique?
    • What follow-up schedule and trigger points would apply?

    5) Advanced, recurrent, or metastatic disease

    Often involved:

    • Medical oncologist — systemic therapy sequencing, drug side effects, clinical trials, monitoring response and resistance.
    • Radiation oncologist — radiotherapy for local control, symptom relief, oligometastatic disease in selected cases, or recurrence after surgery.
    • Urologist — urinary obstruction, local complications, surgical history, and some ongoing surveillance issues.
    • Nuclear medicine physician — PSMA PET/CT interpretation and, in some centres, radioligand therapy such as lutetium-PSMA.
    • Palliative care specialist — symptom control, quality of life, pain management, and planning; this can occur alongside active cancer treatment.

    Useful discussion points:

    • What is the goal: cure, durable control, delaying progression, symptom relief, or quality-of-life support?
    • What treatment sequence is being proposed, and what would trigger a change?
    • Are genomic testing, PSMA PET/CT, or clinical trials relevant?
    • Who manages urgent symptoms such as bone pain, urinary obstruction, or treatment toxicity?

    Multidisciplinary review: when it may matter

    A multidisciplinary review can be particularly useful when:

    • biopsy results, MRI findings, or risk grouping are uncertain;
    • active surveillance, surgery, and radiation are all plausible options;
    • disease is high-risk, locally advanced, recurrent, or metastatic;
    • PSMA PET/CT or genomic testing might change management;
    • side-effect priorities are central to the decision;
    • a patient wants to understand whether specialist recommendations differ.

    A practical question is:

    “Has my case been reviewed in a multidisciplinary meeting, or should I seek input from both a urologist and a radiation oncologist before deciding?”

    Care coordination questions

    These questions help clarify accountability:

    • Who is the lead clinician right now?
    • Who explains results from PSA, MRI, biopsy, pathology, and imaging?
    • Who decides whether a scan or biopsy is needed?
    • Who compares active surveillance, surgery, radiation, and systemic therapy options?
    • Who monitors PSA and side effects after treatment?
    • Who should be contacted after hours or for urgent symptoms?
    • Who sends reports back to the GP and other clinicians?
    • Is there a nurse coordinator, prostate cancer specialist nurse, or navigator?
