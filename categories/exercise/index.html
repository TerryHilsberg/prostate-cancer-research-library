    Exercise

    Scope

    Research notes and filed articles about exercise, physical function, resistance training, aerobic fitness, fatigue, treatment tolerance, and quality of life in prostate cancer diagnosis, treatment, and survivorship.

    This category is for evidence that helps patients discuss safe, appropriate activity plans with clinicians and exercise professionals.

    Key patient questions

    • What kinds of exercise improve fatigue, function, strength, mood, or quality of life during or after prostate cancer treatment?
    • What is known about exercise during active surveillance, after prostatectomy, during radiation, or during ADT?
    • Can resistance training help reduce muscle and bone loss associated with ADT?
    • What safety limits apply after surgery, during radiation, or with bone metastases?
    • What should be discussed with a clinician, physiotherapist, or accredited exercise physiologist?

    Evidence to prioritize

    • Randomized trials and meta-analyses of exercise interventions in prostate cancer populations
    • Studies measuring fatigue, strength, cardiorespiratory fitness, falls, bone density, sexual/urinary function, mood, and quality of life
    • Exercise oncology guidelines and consensus statements
    • Safety studies for patients on ADT or with advanced/metastatic disease

    Cautions

    Not medical advice

    Exercise notes are educational. People undergoing diagnosis or treatment should discuss exercise plans with a qualified clinician, especially after surgery, during systemic therapy, or if bone metastases, severe fatigue, anemia, cardiovascular disease, falls risk, or pain are present.

    Be especially cautious with:

    • Heavy lifting soon after surgery
    • High-impact exercise with bone metastases or fracture risk
    • Intense exercise during acute illness, severe fatigue, or treatment complications
    • Generic fitness advice not studied in prostate cancer populations

    Filed items

    • Exercise and Prostate Cancer: Evidence Map — curated evidence summary covering exercise/physical activity before diagnosis, after diagnosis, during treatment, and after treatment/survivorship. Includes incidence evidence, post-diagnosis survival/progression cohorts, radiation/ADT trials, pelvic-floor rehabilitation evidence, and exercise-oncology guidance.

    Current evidence summary

    Patient phase Summary
    Before diagnosis / incidence Exercise is highly beneficial for general health, but prostate-cancer-specific incidence prevention evidence is mixed. Best meta-analyses show little or no association with total prostate cancer incidence, with possible but uncertain reductions in advanced/aggressive/fatal disease.
    After diagnosis Higher post-diagnosis activity, especially brisk walking and vigorous activity, is consistently associated with better survival/progression outcomes in observational cohorts, but causality is not proven.
    During treatment Randomized trials and systematic reviews support aerobic and resistance exercise during radiation and ADT for fatigue, fitness, strength, function, body composition, and quality of life.
    After treatment Survivorship exercise supports function and quality of life. Pelvic-floor muscle exercise, especially supervised programs, has specific evidence for urinary incontinence after radical prostatectomy.

    Practical patient resources

    • Five exercises to strengthen your pelvic floor — UT Health San Antonio MD Anderson Cancer Center patient resource. Useful as a practical discussion aid for pelvic-floor strengthening, especially around urinary-control recovery, but it should be checked with the treating surgeon, urologist, physiotherapist, or pelvic-floor clinician before use after prostate surgery or during active treatment.
