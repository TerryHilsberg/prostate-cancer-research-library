### 1) EAU 2026 update — relapsing and metastatic prostate cancer treatment

- **Citation:** Tilki D, van den Bergh RCN, Briers E, Chiu P, et al. **EAU-EANM-ESTRO-ESUR-ISUP-SIOG Guidelines on Prostate Cancer. Part II—2026 Update: Treatment of Relapsing and Metastatic Prostate Cancer.** *European Urology.* Published July 2026 / PubMed indexed 2026-07-15. DOI: [10.1016/j.eururo.2026.04.014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eururo.2026.04.014). PMID: [42457456](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42457456/)
- **Evidence type and population:** Major multidisciplinary guideline update; men with biochemical relapse, metastatic hormone-sensitive prostate cancer, and metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer.
- **Category/categories:** Guidelines & Care Pathways; Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Imaging & Biomarkers.
- **Selection score:** 9.5/10.
- **Summary:** The 2026 EAU-associated guideline update summarizes new evidence from 2023–2025 and updates recommendations for relapse risk stratification, salvage-treatment decisions, metastatic hormone-sensitive therapy, castration-resistant therapy, AR pathway inhibitors, PARP inhibitors and combinations, metastasis-directed therapy, and PSMA-based therapy.
- **Main finding:** The advanced/recurrent treatment map continues to broaden: treatment decisions increasingly depend on relapse risk, disease volume/timing, prior local therapy, PSMA or other next-generation imaging, genomic eligibility, symptoms, comorbidities, and access/reimbursement.
- **Patient relevance:** For a patient with recurrence or metastatic disease, this is a high-priority clinician-discussion item because it affects what specialists may consider standard, optional, trial-based, or sequencing-dependent.
- **Limitations:** Guideline summaries do not substitute for local multidisciplinary review; recommendations may not account for individual frailty, comorbidities, cost, drug availability, reimbursement, or patient values.
- **Safety/clinical cautions:** Systemic intensification can add fatigue, cardiovascular/metabolic effects, cytopenias, falls/fracture risk, drug interactions, and quality-of-life trade-offs. Discuss goals of care, expected benefit, monitoring, alternatives, and clinical-trial eligibility with oncology/urology specialists.
- **Changes to decision map/category page:** **Yes.** Filed under Guidelines & Care Pathways and Advanced & Recurrent Disease as a 2026 update anchor.

### 2) ProBIOPSY international consensus — prostate biopsy standards

- **Citation:** Chernysheva D, Di Bello F, Avesani G, Baboudjian M, et al. **ProBIOPSY: A Multidisciplinary International Consensus on Standards for Prostate Biopsy.** *European Urology.* Published July 2026 / PubMed indexed 2026-07-08. DOI: [10.1016/j.eururo.2026.06.012](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eururo.2026.06.012). PMID: [42420069](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42420069/)
- **Evidence type and population:** Systematic-review-informed modified Delphi consensus with 34 international experts; men being considered for prostate biopsy and local-treatment planning.
- **Category/categories:** Diagnostic Workup; Localized Treatment; Imaging & Biomarkers; Guidelines & Care Pathways.
- **Selection score:** 8.8/10.
- **Summary:** The consensus addresses standardization across patient selection, imaging pathway, biopsy tools, biopsy technique, targeted/perilesional sampling, and biopsy strategy for local treatment planning.
- **Main finding:** The panel endorsed MRI-based pathways when imaging quality is adequate and harmonized key elements of targeted plus perilesional biopsy. For many local-treatment-planning decisions, targeted ± perilesional biopsy may be sufficient, but contralateral sampling may still be needed in selected situations.
- **Patient relevance:** Patients facing first biopsy, repeat biopsy, active surveillance entry, focal therapy evaluation, or radiotherapy/surgery planning can ask how their team ensures biopsy quality, whether targeted plus systematic/perilesional sampling is planned, and whether the biopsy approach fits the treatment decision being made.
- **Limitations:** Delphi consensus is not the same as a randomized trial; local expertise, MRI quality, lesion visibility, biopsy route, anesthesia/tolerability, and infection-prevention protocols vary.
- **Safety/clinical cautions:** Biopsy decisions involve bleeding, urinary retention, pain, infection/sepsis risk, false-negative risk, overdiagnosis, and downstream overtreatment. Patients should discuss transperineal versus transrectal route, antibiotic strategy, anticoagulants, and what a negative or limited biopsy would mean.
- **Changes to decision map/category page:** **Yes.** Filed under Diagnostic Workup and Localized Treatment.

### 3) SPPORT quality-of-life analysis — salvage radiotherapy intensification after prostatectomy

- **Citation:** Bruner DW, Karrison TG, Pollack A, Michalski JM, et al. **Quality of life results of addition of androgen deprivation therapy and pelvic lymph node treatment to prostate bed salvage radiotherapy: NRG Oncology/RTOG 0534 SPPORT.** *International Journal of Radiation Oncology Biology Physics.* Published July 2026 / PubMed indexed 2026-07-14. DOI verified by Crossref: [10.1016/j.ijrobp.2026.06.3085](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijrobp.2026.06.3085). PMID: [42448001](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42448001/)
- **Evidence type and population:** Randomized trial quality-of-life analysis; 644 men with detectable PSA after prostatectomy enrolled in the SPPORT salvage-radiotherapy trial.
- **Category/categories:** Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Localized Treatment; Side Effects & Quality of Life; Shared Decision-Making.
- **Selection score:** 9.0/10.
- **Summary:** The parent SPPORT trial showed biochemical-progression-free-survival benefit from adding short-term ADT and pelvic lymph-node radiotherapy to prostate-bed salvage radiotherapy. This analysis focuses on EPIC, mental-health, EuroQol, and quality-adjusted survival outcomes through years 1 and 5.
- **Main finding:** Urinary and bowel scores worsened during radiotherapy and then improved by years 1 and 5, though not always to baseline. Sexual and hormonal domains showed clinically relevant trade-offs, especially with short-term ADT.
- **Patient relevance:** For a patient considering salvage radiotherapy after prostatectomy, the paper helps frame the benefit–harm discussion: intensification may improve cancer-control endpoints for selected men, but quality-of-life effects should be discussed before treatment.
- **Limitations:** Results apply to trial-eligible men and group averages; individual urinary, bowel, sexual, fatigue, mood, and hormonal effects depend on baseline function, prior surgery recovery, radiation fields/dose, ADT use, age, comorbidities, and clinician technique.
- **Safety/clinical cautions:** Salvage treatment choices should include discussion with radiation oncology/urology/medical oncology about PSA kinetics, pathology, imaging, ADT duration, pelvic nodal field rationale, bowel/bladder constraints, sexual rehabilitation, pelvic-floor support, fatigue/exercise, and cardiovascular/metabolic risk during ADT.
- **Changes to decision map/category page:** **Yes.** Filed as a recurrent/localized-treatment quality-of-life decision item.

### 4) Localized-treatment quality of life in an Asian prospective cohort

- **Citation:** Lee YHA, Chan KY, Kwok MC, Hui HL, et al. **Impact of different treatment modalities for localized prostate cancer on patient-reported quality of life outcomes in an Asian Cohort.** *Scientific Reports.* Published 2026-07-13. DOI: [10.1038/s41598-026-62051-y](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-026-62051-y). PMID: [42443472](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42443472/)
- **Evidence type and population:** Prospective cohort; 866 men with localized prostate cancer receiving active surveillance, radical prostatectomy, radiation therapy, or focal therapy, followed with EPIC-26 patient-reported outcomes up to 5 years.
- **Category/categories:** Localized Treatment; Side Effects & Quality of Life; Shared Decision-Making; Active Surveillance.
- **Selection score:** 8.2/10.
- **Summary:** The study compares longer-term urinary, bowel, sexual, and hormonal/quality-of-life domains across common localized management strategies in an Asian clinical cohort.
- **Main finding:** Radical prostatectomy was associated with worse urinary continence and sexual-function scores in the first year compared with other approaches; the study provides patient-reported outcome trajectories rather than only clinician-recorded complications.
- **Patient relevance:** This supports asking each treatment team for expected urinary pad/leakage, erectile-function, bowel, hormone/fatigue, and recovery trajectories in patients similar to oneself, not just cancer-control rates.
- **Limitations:** Nonrandomized treatment selection can bias comparisons; focal therapy group size was smaller; outcomes may reflect local expertise and population characteristics and may not generalize to all health systems.
- **Safety/clinical cautions:** Do not infer that one treatment is “best” from quality-of-life averages alone. Cancer risk group, tumour features, life expectancy, patient priorities, salvage options, and clinician/team outcomes matter.
- **Changes to decision map/category page:** **Yes.** Filed under Localized Treatment and Side Effects & Quality of Life.

### 5) Lutetium-177–PSMA radioligand therapy in metastatic hormone-sensitive disease

- **Citation:** McGrath J, Willis R, Balaji S, Ishiyama Y, et al. **Lutetium-177–PSMA radioligand therapy in metastatic hormone-sensitive prostate cancer: A systematic review of efficacy, safety, and combination strategies.** *Urologic Oncology: Seminars and Original Investigations.* Published/Indexed 2026-07-18. DOI: [10.1016/j.urolonc.2026.06.028](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.urolonc.2026.06.028). PMID: [42471290](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42471290/)
- **Evidence type and population:** Systematic review; 14 studies of 177Lu-PSMA radioligand therapy as monotherapy or combined with ADT, chemotherapy, stereotactic radiotherapy, or tandem alpha-emitter approaches in metastatic hormone-sensitive prostate cancer.
- **Category/categories:** Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Imaging & Biomarkers; Therapies; Side Effects & Quality of Life.
- **Selection score:** 8.0/10.
- **Summary:** The review synthesizes early evidence for moving PSMA-targeted radioligand therapy earlier than its established metastatic castration-resistant setting.
- **Main finding:** PSA50 response rates were reported across studies, with high responses in some treatment-naïve de novo metastatic cohorts, but the evidence base remains heterogeneous and still actively investigational for hormone-sensitive disease.
- **Patient relevance:** Patients with newly diagnosed metastatic disease may hear about PSMA radioligand therapy and can use this review to ask whether it is standard, trial-based, or appropriate only after later disease states in their setting.
- **Limitations:** Included studies vary in design, maturity, combinations, endpoints, and follow-up; conference abstracts and nonrandomized data can overestimate benefit or underestimate late harms.
- **Safety/clinical cautions:** Radioligand therapy requires specialist nuclear-medicine/oncology assessment, PSMA-avid disease confirmation, marrow/renal/salivary toxicity monitoring, radiation-safety procedures, and careful sequencing with ADT, AR pathway inhibitors, chemotherapy, PARP inhibitors, and trials.
- **Changes to decision map/category page:** **Yes.** Filed as an advanced-disease/PSMA-therapy watch item, but does not make early 177Lu-PSMA a patient-facing recommendation.

### 6) Primary G-CSF prophylaxis during darolutamide triplet therapy for metastatic castration-sensitive disease

- **Citation:** Kubo M, Urabe F, Tashiro K, Kagawa H, et al. **Real-world evaluation of primary prophylactic use of granulocyte colony-stimulating factor in patients with metastatic castration-sensitive prostate cancer treated with triplet therapy.** *International Journal of Clinical Oncology.* Published 2026-07-15. DOI verified by Crossref: [10.1007/s10147-026-03137-5](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10147-026-03137-5). PMID: [42455461](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42455461/)
- **Evidence type and population:** Retrospective multicenter real-world cohort; 121 patients with metastatic castration-sensitive prostate cancer treated with ADT + docetaxel + darolutamide across eight institutions.
- **Category/categories:** Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Therapies; Side Effects & Quality of Life.
- **Selection score:** 7.7/10, included for treatment-safety relevance.
- **Summary:** The study evaluated whether primary prophylactic granulocyte colony-stimulating factor reduced hematologic toxicity during docetaxel-containing triplet therapy.
- **Main finding:** Primary G-CSF was independently associated with lower grade ≥3 neutropenia and febrile neutropenia; without prophylaxis, severe neutropenia and febrile neutropenia were common, especially early in treatment.
- **Patient relevance:** For patients offered triplet therapy, this supports asking about neutropenia/febrile-neutropenia risk, baseline blood counts, infection precautions, whether G-CSF is indicated, and how early-cycle monitoring will work.
- **Limitations:** Retrospective and physician-discretion prophylaxis can introduce selection bias; results may differ by age, comorbidity, chemotherapy dose, local G-CSF guidelines, and health-system practice.
- **Safety/clinical cautions:** G-CSF itself can cause bone pain and rare serious adverse effects; chemotherapy fever, chills, infection symptoms, or feeling acutely unwell during neutropenia may require urgent medical review. Patients should follow their oncology team's fever/infection action plan.
- **Changes to decision map/category page:** **Yes.** Filed under Advanced & Recurrent Disease and Side Effects & Quality of Life as a monitoring/safety discussion point.

### 7) Biochemical recurrence consensus scenarios after surgery or radiation

- **Citation:** López Campos F, Couñago F, Vallejo JA, Vilaseca A, et al. **Expert consensus recommendations for the management of biochemical recurrence in prostate cancer: a Delphi study across clinical scenarios.** *Clinical & Translational Oncology.* Published/Indexed 2026-07-18. DOI in PubMed appears metadata-inconsistent and should be treated cautiously until publisher metadata stabilizes. PMID: [42471494](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42471494/)
- **Evidence type and population:** Modified Delphi consensus; 49 Spanish specialists across urology, radiation oncology, and nuclear medicine; men with biochemical recurrence after radical prostatectomy, radiotherapy, or prostatectomy followed by salvage radiotherapy.
- **Category/categories:** Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Imaging & Biomarkers; Guidelines & Care Pathways; Shared Decision-Making.
- **Selection score:** 7.6/10, included as guideline-watchlist/consensus evidence.
- **Summary:** The panel reached consensus on most statements, including PSA-based recurrence definitions, risk stratification, next-generation imaging use, and salvage radiotherapy ± ADT depending on clinical scenario.
- **Main finding:** The consensus reinforces that biochemical recurrence is not a single situation: prior treatment, PSA level and kinetics, risk features, symptoms, imaging, and prior salvage therapy should guide next steps.
- **Patient relevance:** Patients with a rising PSA after local therapy can ask what definition of recurrence is being used, whether PSMA PET or other next-generation imaging is appropriate, and how clinicians weigh salvage radiotherapy, ADT, observation, or systemic therapy.
- **Limitations:** Consensus recommendations are region-specific and expert-opinion-influenced; they do not replace formal international guidelines or individualized multidisciplinary review.
- **Safety/clinical cautions:** Rising PSA should be evaluated promptly but not reflexively treated without context. Imaging can find equivocal lesions; salvage treatments carry urinary, bowel, sexual, fatigue, hormonal, and systemic risks.
- **Changes to decision map/category page:** **Yes.** Filed as recurrent-disease pathway context.

### 8) PI-RADS-stratified MRI-based PSA density for adverse pathology prediction

- **Citation:** Fu H, Millan B, Michael P, Blachman-Braun R, et al. **PI-RADS-stratified MRI-based PSA Density for Predicting Adverse Pathology after Robotic-assisted Laparoscopic Radical Prostatectomy.** *European Urology Focus.* Published July 2026 / PubMed indexed 2026-07-17. DOI: [10.1016/j.euf.2026.04.017](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.euf.2026.04.017). PMID: [42469100](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42469100/)
- **Evidence type and population:** MRI-era cohort from a prospectively maintained database; 788 men undergoing robotic radical prostatectomy with preoperative mpMRI, pathology, and long-term follow-up.
- **Category/categories:** Risk Stratification & Staging; Diagnostic Workup; Imaging & Biomarkers; Localized Treatment.
- **Selection score:** 7.8/10.
- **Summary:** The study evaluated MRI-derived PSA density, stratified by PI-RADS category, for predicting adverse prostatectomy pathology and oncologic outcomes.
- **Main finding:** PSA density remains a clinically accessible risk-stratification variable, but optimal thresholds may depend on MRI/PI-RADS context rather than a single universal cutoff.
- **Patient relevance:** Patients comparing active surveillance, surgery, radiation, or additional biopsy/imaging can ask how PSA density, MRI lesion score, prostate volume, biopsy grade/volume, and other biomarkers are being combined.
- **Limitations:** Surgical-cohort design means results may not apply to all men considering biopsy or active surveillance; prostatectomy pathology is unavailable for men managed non-surgically; thresholds need external validation.
- **Safety/clinical cautions:** PSA density can help risk discussions but does not diagnose or rule out cancer by itself. Decisions should integrate MRI quality, biopsy adequacy, family history/genetics, DRE, PSA kinetics, comorbidities, and preferences.
- **Changes to decision map/category page:** **Yes.** Filed as risk-stratification context; no immediate recommendation change.

## Overall interpretation for patients

This week’s strongest patient-facing updates are guideline/consensus and decision-quality items: the EAU 2026 recurrent/metastatic treatment update, ProBIOPSY biopsy standardization consensus, and SPPORT quality-of-life analysis. Together they reinforce that prostate-cancer decisions increasingly depend on risk group, MRI/biopsy quality, PSA kinetics, genomic/imaging eligibility, expected side effects, and patient priorities. These updates should be used as clinician-discussion prompts, not as personal medical advice.
