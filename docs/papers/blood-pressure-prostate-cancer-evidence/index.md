# Blood Pressure, Hypertension, and Prostate Cancer: Evidence Map

Retrieved: **2026-07-04**  
Categories: **Side Effects & Quality of Life; Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Screening & Early Detection**  
Scope: blood pressure, hypertension, antihypertensive medication, prostate cancer incidence/fatal risk, recurrence/progression, and treatment-related hypertension/cardiovascular risk.

!!! warning "Not medical advice"
    This page summarizes research for education and clinician discussion. It does not diagnose hypertension, recommend a blood pressure target, or advise starting/stopping blood pressure or prostate cancer medication. Blood pressure goals and medication decisions should be individualized with qualified clinicians, especially in people with frailty, dizziness/falls risk, kidney disease, heart disease, diabetes, or prostate cancer therapies that can raise blood pressure.

## Evidence synthesis by patient journey

### 1. Before diagnosis: incidence and fatal prostate cancer risk

The literature does **not** support a simple statement that hypertension definitely causes prostate cancer. Metabolic syndrome meta-analyses suggest the broader cardiometabolic cluster may be associated with prostate cancer risk or aggressive features, but individual components vary and confounding is substantial.

The ARIC cohort adds a more specific signal: untreated stage 1 hypertension was associated with higher fatal prostate cancer risk, particularly in Black men, while antihypertensive use was associated with lower fatal prostate cancer risk. This is important but observational; it may reflect both biology and healthcare access/treatment differences.

**Interpretation:** high blood pressure should be treated seriously for cardiovascular health. Its independent role in prostate cancer incidence remains uncertain, but it belongs in the patient’s risk and resilience discussion.

### 2. After diagnosis: recurrence, progression, and survivorship

Direct prostate-specific evidence linking hypertension to recurrence/progression is limited. A 2026 systematic review across adult solid-cancer survivors found no clear overall association between hypertension and recurrence, but cancer types and treatments varied, and prostate-specific certainty remains low.

In practice, the main postdiagnosis relevance is that blood pressure is a modifiable competing-risk factor: men with prostate cancer may live many years, and cardiovascular disease can be a major source of morbidity and mortality. Blood pressure also affects eligibility, monitoring, and safety for some systemic therapies.

### 3. During treatment: ADT and androgen-receptor pathway therapy

This is the strongest clinical link. ADT is associated with adverse metabolic changes, including metabolic syndrome and diabetes risk, and may worsen cardiovascular risk. Novel androgen-receptor pathway inhibitors can also raise cardiovascular risk.

Specific treatment signals:

- **Abiraterone** can cause mineralocorticoid-related toxicity, including hypertension, hypokalemia, and fluid retention; meta-analysis shows increased hypertension risk.
- **Enzalutamide** is associated with increased hypertension risk in randomized-trial meta-analysis.
- **Apalutamide/enzalutamide/darolutamide class in nmCRPC**: meta-analysis found increased cardiovascular events and grade 3–4 hypertension for novel hormonal agents plus ADT versus ADT/placebo.
- **Broader AR pathway inhibitor meta-analyses** show increased cardiovascular events, including hypertension and heart failure signals in some analyses.

**Interpretation:** BP should be assessed and optimized before and during ADT/AR pathway treatment. This is a treatment-safety issue, not just a general health issue.

### 4. After treatment has ended: long-term survivorship

After local treatment or systemic therapy, blood pressure remains relevant because cardiovascular disease is common and modifiable. Survivorship care should include BP measurement, lipid/glucose assessment, weight/body composition, physical activity, diet quality, medication review, and coordination between oncology/urology, primary care, and cardiology/cardio-oncology when risk is high.

## Selected papers

### Overview and clinical framing

#### The Intersection of Prostate Cancer and Hypertension: a Call to Action

- **Citation:** *Current Treatment Options in Oncology*. 2023.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1007/s11864-023-01094-z](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11864-023-01094-z); PMID [37191906](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37191906/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37191906/
- **Date:** 2023 Jul
- **Category:** Side Effects & Quality of Life; Advanced & Recurrent Disease
- **Evidence type:** Narrative review / clinical call to action.
- **Population:** Men with prostate cancer, especially those exposed to ADT, GnRH agonists/antagonists, enzalutamide, abiraterone, and related therapies.
- **Patient relevance:** Best high-level clinical overview of why BP matters in prostate cancer care.
- **Main finding:** Prostate cancer therapies can directly or indirectly increase hypertension risk; individualized BP goals and proactive cardiovascular risk management are recommended.
- **Limitations:** Review article, not a randomized trial; treatment decisions remain individualized.
- **Questions for clinician:** Should BP be checked before treatment starts, and how often during ADT or androgen-receptor pathway therapy?

### Incidence / before diagnosis

#### Blood Pressure, Hypertension, and Antihypertensive Medication Use and Risk of Total and Fatal Prostate Cancer in ARIC

- **Citation:** *Journal of Racial and Ethnic Health Disparities*. 2025.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1007/s40615-023-01885-y](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40615-023-01885-y); PMID [38099995](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38099995/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38099995/
- **Date:** 2025 Feb
- **Category:** Screening & Early Detection; Side Effects & Quality of Life
- **Evidence type:** Prospective cohort analysis.
- **Population:** 6,658 men in the Atherosclerosis Risk in Communities study; 889 total and 127 fatal prostate cancer cases.
- **Patient relevance:** Directly addresses BP, untreated hypertension, antihypertensive use, total prostate cancer, and fatal prostate cancer.
- **Main finding:** Untreated stage 1 hypertension was associated with higher fatal prostate cancer risk compared with untreated normal BP; the association was stronger in Black men. Antihypertensive use was associated with lower fatal prostate cancer risk compared with never use.
- **Limitations:** Observational; potential confounding by healthcare access, screening, treatment, adherence, and comorbidity.
- **Questions for clinician:** Does my BP status and cardiovascular care need review as part of overall prostate cancer risk management?

#### Effect of metabolic syndrome and its components on prostate cancer risk: meta-analysis

- **Citation:** Esposito K, et al. *Journal of Endocrinological Investigation*. 2013.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1007/BF03346748](https://doi.org/10.1007/BF03346748); PMID [23481613](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23481613/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23481613/
- **Date:** 2013 Feb
- **Category:** Nutrition; Side Effects & Quality of Life; Screening & Early Detection
- **Evidence type:** Meta-analysis.
- **Population:** 14 studies with 4,728 prostate cancer cases.
- **Patient relevance:** Places hypertension in the broader metabolic syndrome/cardiometabolic context.
- **Main finding:** Metabolic syndrome showed a modest, not statistically definitive overall association with prostate cancer risk; results differed by geography and study type.
- **Limitations:** Older evidence base, heterogeneous definitions, observational confounding.
- **Questions for clinician:** Should prostate cancer risk conversations include metabolic health, waist/weight, glucose, lipids, and BP together?

#### Meta-analysis of metabolic syndrome and prostate cancer

- **Citation:** *Prostate Cancer and Prostatic Diseases*. 2017.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1038/pcan.2017.1](https://doi.org/10.1038/pcan.2017.1); PMID [28220805](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28220805/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28220805/
- **Date:** 2017 Jun
- **Category:** Nutrition; Side Effects & Quality of Life; Risk Stratification & Staging
- **Evidence type:** Meta-analysis.
- **Population:** Studies of metabolic syndrome, prostate cancer incidence, high-grade disease, aggressive pathology, and biochemical recurrence.
- **Patient relevance:** Addresses whether cardiometabolic factors relate to more aggressive prostate cancer features.
- **Main finding:** Metabolic syndrome and some components may relate to aggressive prostate cancer features, but associations vary by endpoint and component.
- **Limitations:** Observational evidence; metabolic syndrome combines multiple risk factors, so hypertension-specific conclusions are limited.
- **Questions for clinician:** Are BP, diabetes, lipids, and obesity being managed as part of overall cancer survivorship health?

### After diagnosis / recurrence and survivorship

#### Hypertension and risk of cancer recurrence in adult survivors: systematic review and meta-analysis

- **Citation:** *Hypertension Research*. 2026.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1038/s41440-025-02431-5](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41440-025-02431-5); PMID [41203853](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41203853/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41203853/
- **Date:** 2026 Mar
- **Category:** Side Effects & Quality of Life
- **Evidence type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis of observational studies.
- **Population:** 13 observational studies, 15 cohorts, 50,549 adult solid-cancer survivors.
- **Patient relevance:** Addresses whether hypertension after cancer treatment is linked with recurrence risk.
- **Main finding:** Overall, hypertension was not clearly associated with increased solid tumor recurrence risk, but evidence varied and prostate-specific certainty is limited.
- **Limitations:** Multi-cancer evidence, observational studies, heterogeneous definitions and treatments.
- **Questions for clinician:** Is BP being managed mainly for cardiovascular survivorship, while recognizing cancer-recurrence evidence is uncertain?

#### Assessment and Management of Cardiovascular Risk Factors Among US Veterans With Prostate Cancer

- **Citation:** *JAMA Network Open*. 2021.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2021.0070](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2021.0070); PMID [33625512](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33625512/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33625512/
- **Date:** 2021 Feb 1
- **Category:** Side Effects & Quality of Life; Guidelines & Care Pathways
- **Evidence type:** Cross-sectional health-system analysis.
- **Population:** 90,494 US veterans with prostate cancer; includes men initiating ADT and men with atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease.
- **Patient relevance:** Shows real-world gaps/opportunities in BP, cholesterol, and glucose assessment and management around prostate cancer care.
- **Main finding:** Cardiovascular risk factor assessment and control are central survivorship issues, especially for men initiating ADT.
- **Limitations:** Veterans Health Administration population; observational health-system data.
- **Questions for clinician:** Have BP, LDL/cholesterol, glucose/HbA1c, smoking, weight, and exercise been explicitly reviewed before ADT?

### Treatment-related blood pressure and cardiovascular risk

#### Quantifying risk of metabolic syndrome and components following ADT: meta-analysis

- **Citation:** Bosco C, et al. *PLOS ONE*. 2015.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1371/journal.pone.0117344](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0117344); PMID [25794005](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25794005/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25794005/
- **Date:** 2015
- **Category:** Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Side Effects & Quality of Life
- **Evidence type:** Meta-analysis.
- **Population:** Nine studies of men receiving ADT.
- **Patient relevance:** ADT is common in advanced/high-risk prostate cancer and can worsen metabolic risk.
- **Main finding:** ADT was associated with increased metabolic syndrome risk; diabetes risk was also increased. Hypertension-specific evidence was less robust but part of the cardiometabolic risk frame.
- **Limitations:** Limited number of studies for individual components; observational designs included.
- **Questions for clinician:** What cardiometabolic monitoring schedule is needed when starting ADT?

#### Pharmacologic androgen deprivation and cardiovascular disease risk factors: systematic review

- **Citation:** *European Journal of Clinical Investigation*. 2015.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1111/eci.12431](https://doi.org/10.1111/eci.12431); PMID [25753698](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25753698/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25753698/
- **Date:** 2015 May
- **Category:** Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Side Effects & Quality of Life
- **Evidence type:** Systematic review of randomized controlled trials reporting cardiovascular risk factors.
- **Population:** Men with prostate cancer in RCTs comparing pharmacologic ADT with no ADT.
- **Patient relevance:** Looks at BP and other cardiovascular risk factors in a trial context.
- **Main finding:** ADT affects cardiovascular risk factors; BP reporting was less consistent than other metabolic outcomes.
- **Limitations:** Risk-factor outcomes were not always primary endpoints; trial populations may underrepresent frail patients or those with major comorbidities.
- **Questions for clinician:** Which risk factors are most likely to worsen for me: BP, weight, glucose, lipids, or body composition?

#### Association of ADT with metabolic disease in prostate cancer patients: updated meta-analysis

- **Citation:** *Clinical Genitourinary Cancer*. 2023.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.clgc.2022.12.006](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clgc.2022.12.006); PMID [36621463](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36621463/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36621463/
- **Date:** 2023 Jun
- **Category:** Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Side Effects & Quality of Life
- **Evidence type:** Updated meta-analysis.
- **Population:** 19 studies of ADT-associated metabolic outcomes.
- **Patient relevance:** Quantifies metabolic syndrome, diabetes, and hypertension domains in ADT-treated men.
- **Main finding:** ADT was associated with higher metabolic syndrome risk and diabetes risk; hypertension is part of the monitored metabolic disease burden.
- **Limitations:** Heterogeneous study designs, controls, and definitions.
- **Questions for clinician:** Should ADT care include baseline and follow-up BP, glucose/HbA1c, lipids, weight, and exercise/nutrition planning?

#### Cardiovascular Events and Androgen Receptor Signaling Inhibitors in Advanced Prostate Cancer

- **Citation:** *JAMA Oncology*. 2024.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1001/jamaoncol.2024.1549](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamaoncol.2024.1549); PMID [38842801](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38842801/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38842801/
- **Date:** 2024 Jul 1
- **Category:** Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Side Effects & Quality of Life
- **Evidence type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized clinical trials.
- **Population:** Men with locally advanced or metastatic prostate cancer treated with androgen receptor signaling inhibitors added to standard care.
- **Patient relevance:** Directly relevant when considering abiraterone, enzalutamide, apalutamide, darolutamide-class intensification.
- **Main finding:** AR signaling inhibitors improve prostate cancer outcomes in appropriate settings but add cardiovascular toxicity concerns, including hypertension-related monitoring needs.
- **Limitations:** Trial populations may exclude high-risk cardiovascular patients; adverse event reporting varies.
- **Questions for clinician:** What is the benefit of treatment intensification, and how will BP/cardiovascular toxicity be monitored and managed?

#### Cardiovascular events among men treated with androgen receptor pathway inhibitors

- **Citation:** *Prostate Cancer and Prostatic Diseases*. 2025.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1038/s41391-024-00886-0](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41391-024-00886-0); PMID [39237679](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39237679/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39237679/
- **Date:** 2025 Jun
- **Category:** Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Side Effects & Quality of Life
- **Evidence type:** Systematic review, meta-analysis, and network meta-analysis of RCTs.
- **Population:** 26 RCTs of abiraterone, apalutamide, darolutamide, and enzalutamide.
- **Patient relevance:** Helps compare cardiovascular toxicity across AR pathway inhibitors.
- **Main finding:** ARPIs were associated with increased cardiac disorders, heart failure, atrial fibrillation, and hypertension-related cardiovascular concern signals.
- **Limitations:** Indirect comparisons, heterogeneous disease states and treatment durations.
- **Questions for clinician:** If several ARPI options are reasonable, should cardiovascular history influence drug choice?

#### Risk of hypertension in cancer patients treated with abiraterone: meta-analysis

- **Citation:** *Clinical Hypertension*. 2019.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1186/s40885-019-0110-3](https://doi.org/10.1186/s40885-019-0110-3); PMID [30984412](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30984412/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30984412/
- **Date:** 2019
- **Category:** Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Side Effects & Quality of Life
- **Evidence type:** Meta-analysis of randomized clinical trials.
- **Population:** Five trials, 5,445 patients treated with abiraterone plus prednisone/prednisolone versus controls.
- **Patient relevance:** Specific to a common advanced prostate cancer therapy.
- **Main finding:** Abiraterone was associated with significantly increased all-grade and high-grade hypertension risk.
- **Limitations:** Trial selection and adverse-event reporting; BP management protocols vary.
- **Questions for clinician:** If abiraterone is considered, how will BP, potassium, edema/fluid retention, and heart failure risk be monitored?

#### Increased Risk of Hypertension with Enzalutamide in Prostate Cancer

- **Citation:** *Cancer Investigation*. 2019.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1080/07357907.2019.1670203](https://doi.org/10.1080/07357907.2019.1670203); PMID [31557062](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31557062/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31557062/
- **Date:** 2019
- **Category:** Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Side Effects & Quality of Life
- **Evidence type:** Meta-analysis of randomized clinical trials.
- **Population:** Seven studies, 7,347 patients treated with enzalutamide.
- **Patient relevance:** Specific to enzalutamide, used in several prostate cancer disease states.
- **Main finding:** Enzalutamide was associated with increased all-grade and high-grade hypertension risk.
- **Limitations:** Trial populations and adverse event definitions vary.
- **Questions for clinician:** If enzalutamide is considered, how often should BP be checked and what threshold triggers treatment adjustment?

#### Risk of cardiovascular toxicities and hypertension in nmCRPC treated with novel hormonal agents

- **Citation:** *Expert Opinion on Drug Metabolism & Toxicology*. 2021.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1080/17425255.2021.1970745](https://doi.org/10.1080/17425255.2021.1970745); PMID [34407702](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34407702/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34407702/
- **Date:** 2021 Oct
- **Category:** Advanced & Recurrent Disease; Side Effects & Quality of Life
- **Evidence type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis of RCTs.
- **Population:** 4,110 men with nonmetastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer treated with enzalutamide, apalutamide, or darolutamide plus ADT versus ADT/placebo.
- **Patient relevance:** Applies to a disease state where long-term treatment exposure and competing cardiovascular risk matter.
- **Main finding:** Novel hormonal agents increased cardiovascular events and grade 3–4 hypertension risk compared with ADT plus placebo.
- **Limitations:** Three RCTs; not all agents have identical risk profiles; trial selection may exclude high-risk patients.
- **Questions for clinician:** Should cardiovascular risk alter monitoring intensity or drug selection?

## Clinician discussion checklist

- What is my baseline BP, and is it confirmed with home or ambulatory measurements?
- What BP goal is appropriate for me, considering age, frailty, dizziness/falls, kidney disease, diabetes, and cardiovascular history?
- Before ADT or androgen-receptor pathway therapy, have BP, lipids, glucose/HbA1c, kidney function, weight, and cardiovascular history been reviewed?
- If abiraterone is used, how will BP, potassium, fluid retention/edema, and heart failure symptoms be monitored?
- If enzalutamide/apalutamide/darolutamide is used, what is the plan for BP checks and cardiovascular symptom monitoring?
- Should primary care, cardiology, or cardio-oncology be involved before starting systemic therapy?
- Are lifestyle measures—salt intake, alcohol, exercise, weight, sleep apnea assessment—being handled alongside medication decisions?
- Which symptoms require urgent contact: chest pain, shortness of breath, severe headache, neurologic symptoms, fainting, marked edema, or very high BP readings?

## Repository interpretation

This evidence adds a blood-pressure/cardiometabolic safety lens to the prostate cancer library:

1. **Incidence evidence** — hypertension may be associated with fatal prostate cancer in some cohorts, but causality is uncertain.
2. **After diagnosis** — prostate-specific recurrence evidence is limited; cardiovascular survivorship is the strongest reason to manage BP carefully.
3. **Treatment safety** — ADT and androgen-receptor pathway inhibitors create the clearest clinical need for BP monitoring and management.
4. **Shared decision-making** — treatment benefit should be weighed alongside cardiovascular history, BP control, frailty, and monitoring capacity.
