# Focal Therapy for Localized Prostate Cancer: Evidence Map

Retrieved: **2026-07-17**  
Category: **Localized Treatment; Therapies; Imaging & Biomarkers; Side Effects & Quality of Life**  
Scope: focal and partial-gland treatment approaches for localized prostate cancer, including HIFU, cryotherapy, irreversible electroporation, focal laser ablation, vascular-targeted photodynamic therapy, TULSA/MR-guided ultrasound, and focal radiation boost to dominant intraprostatic lesions.

!!! warning "Not medical advice"
    This page is for education and clinician discussion. It does **not** recommend focal therapy, ablation, radiation boost, active surveillance, surgery, or radiation for any individual. Focal therapy selection depends on MRI quality, biopsy mapping, grade group, lesion size/location, prostate anatomy, urinary/sexual priorities, treatment availability, trial/registry access, and willingness to undergo intensive follow-up biopsies. Major guidelines still generally regard focal ablative therapy as investigational or suitable only in trials/registries.

## Patient-facing bottom line

| Question | Practical answer | Strength of evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **What is focal therapy?** | Treatment aimed at the known cancer-bearing part of the prostate rather than the whole gland, attempting to preserve urinary and sexual function. | Strong concept; variable clinical validation by modality |
| **Who is the classic candidate?** | A man with localized, MRI-visible, biopsy-confirmed, clinically significant but limited-volume disease, often an index lesion that appears unilateral or regionally concentrated, with no clear extracapsular extension, seminal vesicle invasion, nodal disease, or metastases. | Consensus-based; not proven by large long-term randomized trials for most ablation modalities |
| **Who is usually a poor candidate?** | Multifocal high-grade disease, extensive bilateral disease, large or poorly localized tumour, MRI-invisible significant disease, suspected extracapsular extension, high-risk biology needing whole-gland/systemic control, inability to comply with MRI/biopsy surveillance, or expectation that focal therapy eliminates future monitoring. | Consensus/guideline-based |
| **Which modality has the strongest cancer-control evidence?** | For **radiation**, focal boost to an MRI-visible dominant intraprostatic lesion has randomized phase III evidence from FLAME. For **ablation**, HIFU, cryotherapy, and padeliporfin vascular-targeted photodynamic therapy have more published evidence than newer modalities, but long-term comparative evidence remains limited. | Moderate for focal boost RT; low-to-moderate for focal ablation depending on modality |
| **Is focal therapy mainstream?** | Focal radiation boost is moving into mainstream radiotherapy for selected intermediate/high-risk patients. Focal ablative therapies are increasingly available but are still commonly described by guidelines as investigational or best done in trials/registries. | Guideline-dependent; evolving |
| **What is the main trade-off?** | Lower urinary/sexual/rectal toxicity is the goal, but residual or out-of-field cancer can remain. Follow-up requires PSA, MRI, and usually repeat biopsy. | Supported by systematic reviews and consensus statements |

## Modalities covered

| Modality | How it works | Typical use-case logic | Evidence maturity | Key caveat |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **High-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU)** | Focused ultrasound heats/ablates tissue. | MRI-visible index lesion; often unilateral/hemi-gland or quadrant treatment; used for selected low/intermediate and some carefully selected higher-risk localized cases in registries. | One of the larger focal-ablation evidence bases, including prospective multicentre registry data. | Follow-up biopsy rates vary; repeat focal treatment may be needed; not equivalent to radical therapy evidence. |
| **Cryotherapy / focal cryoablation** | Freezing destroys targeted tissue. | Often MRI-visible lesions, including anterior or lateral lesions depending on access and ice-ball safety margins. | Registry and prospective development data; medium-term evidence. | Risk to urethra, sphincter, neurovascular bundles, rectum depends on lesion location and ice-ball margin. |
| **Irreversible electroporation (IRE / NanoKnife)** | Nonthermal electrical pulses disrupt cell membranes; theoretically preserves collagenous structures such as vessels/ducts. | Lesions close to structures where thermal spread is a concern, including selected apical/anterior/posterior locations, but selection is specialist-dependent. | Growing prospective evidence including phase II and pivotal single-arm data; still evolving. | Out-of-field clinically significant cancer and retreatment remain concerns; long-term comparative outcomes limited. |
| **Focal laser ablation (FLA)** | MRI-guided laser heating creates localized coagulative necrosis. | Small MRI-visible lesions accessible to in-bore or fusion-guided laser placement. | Early phase/single-arm studies; investigational. | Small numbers; variable local control; expertise and MRI thermometry matter. |
| **Vascular-targeted photodynamic therapy (VTP; padeliporfin/TOOKAD)** | Light-activated drug causes vascular occlusion and tissue necrosis in the treated zone. | Historically studied in low-risk localized disease compared with active surveillance. | One randomized phase III trial versus active surveillance in low-risk disease. | Low-risk disease may often be safely observed; availability/regulatory status varies. |
| **TULSA / MR-guided transurethral ultrasound ablation** | MRI-guided ultrasound from within urethra, often used for directional or near-whole-gland ablation. | Can be focal, subtotal, or near-whole-gland; useful conceptually for MRI-controlled thermal ablation. | Early prospective data; more whole-/near-whole-gland than classic focal in some studies. | Not the same evidence question as focal index-lesion ablation. |
| **Focal radiation boost / microboost** | Whole prostate receives radiotherapy, with an extra dose to the MRI-visible dominant lesion. | Intermediate/high-risk radiotherapy candidates with a clear dominant intraprostatic lesion and acceptable organ-at-risk constraints. | Stronger validation: FLAME randomized phase III trial and ESTRO implementation guidance. | This is not tissue-sparing ablation; the whole gland is still treated, so toxicity/benefit questions differ. |
| **Focal HDR brachytherapy boost** | Brachytherapy dose escalates the dominant intraprostatic lesion while treating/de-escalating the remaining gland. | Selected localized patients with MRI-defined dominant lesion; mostly specialist centres/trials. | Phase II and systematic review evidence. | Technique-sensitive; optimal dose/fractionation still evolving. |

## How case selection usually works

Focal therapy depends on the idea that the main clinically significant cancer focus — often called the **index lesion** or **dominant intraprostatic lesion** — can be identified, targeted, and safely ablated or dose-escalated while leaving other prostate tissue untreated or less intensely treated.

### Lesion pattern most compatible with focal therapy

Most favourable features usually include:

- **MRI-visible lesion** with clear boundaries;
- **concordant targeted and systematic/template biopsy** confirming grade and location;
- **localized disease** without extracapsular extension, seminal vesicle invasion, nodal disease, or metastases;
- **limited-volume clinically significant cancer**, commonly Grade Group 2 and selected Grade Group 3 depending on centre/trial;
- cancer **concentrated in one region**, such as one lobe, one quadrant, anterior zone, or a dominant lesion with no meaningful contralateral high-grade disease;
- willingness to accept **post-treatment MRI and repeat biopsy**, because PSA alone is not enough after focal therapy.

### Lesion pattern that weakens the case

Focal treatment is harder to justify when there is:

- multifocal or bilateral clinically significant cancer;
- high-volume Grade Group 3–5 disease;
- MRI-invisible significant cancer or discordance between MRI and biopsy;
- large lesions that would require near-whole-gland treatment;
- apical, urethral, sphincter-adjacent, neurovascular-bundle-adjacent, or rectal-adjacent disease where safe margins are difficult;
- suspected extracapsular extension or seminal vesicle invasion;
- inability or unwillingness to undergo follow-up biopsy;
- expectation that focal therapy is a “one-and-done cure” without surveillance.

### Location matters

| Lesion location / anatomy | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Anterior lesion** | May be missed by standard transrectal biopsy; often needs high-quality MRI plus targeted/transperineal sampling. Some focal technologies can access it well, but targeting accuracy matters. |
| **Posterior peripheral-zone lesion** | Common location; proximity to rectum and neurovascular bundles may affect thermal margins and rectal safety. |
| **Apical lesion** | Close to urinary sphincter; undertreatment risks recurrence, overtreatment risks continence. |
| **Base/bladder-neck lesion** | Urinary symptoms and bladder-neck/urethral safety constraints matter. |
| **Near urethra** | Thermal ablation may risk urethral injury/stricture; nonthermal or radiation approaches may be considered by specialists, but evidence varies. |
| **Bilateral disease** | Hemi-gland/subtotal approaches may still be discussed, but the more tissue treated, the less “focal” the functional advantage may become. |
| **Dominant lesion in intermediate/high-risk radiotherapy candidate** | Focal radiation boost is different: the whole prostate is treated, then the visible lesion receives extra dose if constraints permit. |

## Evidence synthesis by modality

### 1. Focal ablative therapy overall

The field has grown because MRI and targeted biopsy can identify many smaller, localized lesions. The argument is patient-centred: if the clinically important cancer is concentrated, perhaps the cancer-bearing region can be treated while reducing urinary, sexual, and bowel harms compared with whole-gland therapy.

The counterargument is also important: prostate cancer is often multifocal, MRI can miss clinically significant disease, definitions of “failure” vary, and many focal therapy studies are single-arm, retrospective, short-term, or use inconsistent follow-up biopsies. Therefore, focal ablation should be discussed as an evolving option rather than a proven equivalent to prostatectomy or radiotherapy.

### 2. HIFU

HIFU has one of the largest focal-ablation datasets. A multicentre study of focal HIFU reported 5-year outcomes in clinically significant nonmetastatic prostate cancer. It is useful because many men had intermediate- or high-risk features and were staged with mpMRI plus targeted/systematic or mapping biopsies.

**Patient interpretation:** HIFU is among the more mature focal ablation options, but its evidence is still not the same as decades of radical prostatectomy or radiotherapy outcome data. Repeat treatment and salvage therapy remain part of the pathway.

### 3. Cryotherapy

Focal cryotherapy has prospective registry evidence and may be considered for selected MRI-visible clinically significant lesions. It is not just “freezing the cancer”; the ice-ball must cover the target with a safety margin while protecting urethra, sphincter, rectum, and neurovascular structures.

**Patient interpretation:** cryotherapy may preserve function in selected men, but selection and follow-up are crucial. The evidence base is registry-heavy rather than definitive randomized comparison against surgery/radiation.

### 4. Irreversible electroporation

IRE is attractive because it is nonthermal and may spare some connective-tissue structures, vessels, and ducts. It is being studied for partial-gland ablation in intermediate-risk localized disease, including MRI/biopsy-defined index lesions.

**Patient interpretation:** IRE is promising, especially where thermal damage is a concern, but long-term comparative evidence is still developing. The PRESERVE trial provides important single-arm pivotal data, but it is not the same as randomized comparison with standard treatments.

### 5. Focal laser ablation

FLA is highly targeted and MRI-guided, usually for small MRI-visible lesions. The evidence is earlier phase and smaller than HIFU/cryo. It should be viewed as investigational unless offered in a protocol with robust follow-up.

### 6. Vascular-targeted photodynamic therapy

Padeliporfin VTP has unusually strong randomized evidence for a focal ablation modality because it was tested against active surveillance in low-risk prostate cancer. However, that also creates an interpretation problem: many low-risk men are already appropriate for active surveillance, so the clinical question is not merely whether VTP ablates tissue, but whether it improves outcomes enough to justify intervention.

### 7. Focal radiation boost

Focal radiation boost is the most validated “focal” concept on this page, but it is not focal ablation. The whole prostate is still treated; the MRI-visible dominant lesion receives a higher dose if rectal, bladder, urethral, and other constraints allow.

The FLAME randomized phase III trial showed improved biochemical disease-free survival with focal boost in intermediate/high-risk localized prostate cancer without significantly increasing toxicity in the trial context. ESTRO has since published clinical practice consensus recommendations on how to implement focal boosting.

**Patient interpretation:** for radiotherapy candidates with a clear dominant lesion, focal boost may be more mainstream than focal ablation. It should be discussed with a radiation oncologist as part of whole-gland radiotherapy planning.

## Selected papers and guidelines

### Overall evidence and guideline framing

#### A Systematic Review of Focal Ablative Therapy for Clinically Localised Prostate Cancer in Comparison with Standard Management Options

- **Citation:** van der Poel HG, et al. *European Urology Oncology*. 2021.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.euo.2020.12.008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.euo.2020.12.008); PMID [33423943](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33423943/)
- **Evidence type:** Systematic review comparing focal therapy with active surveillance, radical prostatectomy, or external beam radiotherapy.
- **Patient relevance:** Helps answer whether focal therapy has been validated against standard options.
- **Main finding:** Comparative evidence was limited; only a small number of comparative studies met criteria, with important risk of bias/confounding.
- **Limitations:** Insufficient high-quality comparative data; heterogeneity in modalities, selection, endpoints, and follow-up.
- **Questions for clinician:** Is this focal therapy being offered as standard care, trial/registry care, or private-market treatment with limited comparative evidence?

#### An Updated Systematic Review on Focal Therapy in Localized Prostate Cancer

- **Citation:** Tourinho-Barbosa R, et al. *European Urology*. 2022.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.eururo.2021.08.005](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eururo.2021.08.005); PMID [34489140](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34489140/)
- **Evidence type:** Updated systematic review of focal therapy outcomes.
- **Population:** 72 studies, eight energy sources, 5827 patients.
- **Patient relevance:** Broadest snapshot of focal modalities and maturity.
- **Main finding:** Functional outcomes are often favourable, but oncological effectiveness remains less definitively established.
- **Limitations:** Mostly nonrandomized studies; follow-up and biopsy protocols vary; outcome definitions inconsistent.
- **Questions for clinician:** What is the centre’s own rate of in-field recurrence, out-of-field recurrence, retreatment, and conversion to radical therapy?

#### EAU guideline position on focal/whole-gland ablation

- **Source:** EAU Prostate Cancer Guidelines, treatment chapter.
- **URL:** https://uroweb.org/guidelines/prostate-cancer/chapter/treatment
- **Guideline statement noted in current chapter:** “Only offer whole-gland ablative therapy … or focal ablative therapy within clinical trials or registries.”
- **Patient relevance:** Useful guardrail: focal ablation is not yet treated like routine prostatectomy or radiotherapy in major guideline framing.
- **Questions for clinician:** Is my proposed focal treatment part of a trial or registry, and what follow-up biopsy schedule is required?

#### AUA/ASTRO guideline position on ablation

- **Source:** AUA/ASTRO Clinically Localized Prostate Cancer Guideline 2022.
- **URL:** https://www.auanet.org/guidelines-and-quality/guidelines/clinically-localized-prostate-cancer-aua/astro-guideline-2022
- **Guideline statement:** Patients with low- or intermediate-risk prostate cancer should be told that whole-gland or focal ablation remains investigational without high-quality comparative data versus surgery, radiation, or active surveillance; whole-gland or focal ablation should not be recommended for high-risk prostate cancer outside a clinical trial.
- **Questions for clinician:** Am I low, favourable intermediate, unfavourable intermediate, or high risk — and does that change whether focal therapy is reasonable?

#### International consensus on focal therapy trial design

- **Citation:** van den Bos W, et al. *European Urology*. 2014.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.eururo.2014.01.001](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eururo.2014.01.001); PMID [24444476](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24444476/)
- **Evidence type:** International multidisciplinary Delphi consensus.
- **Patient relevance:** Explains why standardized selection, treatment definitions, outcomes, and follow-up matter.
- **Main finding:** Consensus was reached on patient selection, pre-treatment assessment, outcome evaluation, and follow-up for trials.
- **Limitations:** Consensus, not outcome proof.
- **Questions for clinician:** Does the proposed clinic follow consensus-style pre-treatment mapping and post-treatment surveillance?

#### Standardized nomenclature and surveillance after focal therapy

- **Citation:** Muller BG, et al. *European Urology*. 2020.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.eururo.2020.05.018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eururo.2020.05.018); PMID [32532513](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32532513/)
- **Evidence type:** International multidisciplinary consensus.
- **Patient relevance:** Clarifies that follow-up after focal therapy requires structured surveillance, not PSA alone.
- **Main finding:** Proposed standardized terminology and follow-up methodology after focal therapy/partial gland ablation.
- **Limitations:** Does not prove efficacy; helps standardize monitoring.
- **Questions for clinician:** What MRI and biopsy schedule will be used after treatment, and what counts as in-field versus out-of-field failure?

### HIFU

#### A Multicentre Study of 5-year Outcomes Following Focal HIFU

- **Citation:** Guillaumier S, et al. *European Urology*. 2018.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.eururo.2018.06.006](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eururo.2018.06.006); PMID [29960750](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29960750/)
- **Evidence type:** Prospective multicentre cohort/registry study.
- **Population:** 625 consecutive patients with clinically significant nonmetastatic prostate cancer treated with focal HIFU.
- **Main finding:** Reported 5-year cancer-control and functional outcomes after focal HIFU; many patients had intermediate- or high-risk disease.
- **Limitations:** Nonrandomized; follow-up biopsy not universal; failure-free survival definitions differ from standard radical therapy endpoints.
- **Questions for clinician:** What is the chance of needing repeat HIFU, radical salvage treatment, or later radiation/prostatectomy?

### Cryotherapy

#### Early-medium-term outcomes of primary focal cryotherapy

- **Citation:** Shah TT, et al. *European Urology*. 2019.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.eururo.2018.12.030](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eururo.2018.12.030); PMID [30638633](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30638633/)
- **Evidence type:** Prospective multicentre registry case series.
- **Population:** 122 men undergoing focal cryotherapy in five UK centres; mostly intermediate-risk with some high-risk disease.
- **Main finding:** Reported early-medium-term cancer control and functional outcomes using mpMRI and biopsy-based selection.
- **Limitations:** Registry, nonrandomized, short/medium follow-up, for-cause biopsies rather than uniform long-term mapping.
- **Questions for clinician:** Is my lesion location safely covered by the planned ice-ball margin without damaging sphincter, urethra, rectum, or neurovascular bundles?

### Vascular-targeted photodynamic therapy

#### Padeliporfin VTP versus active surveillance: phase 3 randomized trial

- **Citation:** Azzouzi AR, et al. *The Lancet Oncology*. 2017.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/S1470-2045(16)30661-1](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1470-2045(16)30661-1); PMID [28007457](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28007457/)
- **Evidence type:** Open-label phase 3 randomized controlled trial.
- **Population:** Men with low-risk localized prostate cancer randomized to padeliporfin VTP or active surveillance.
- **Main finding:** VTP reduced progression compared with active surveillance in this trial setting.
- **Limitations:** Low-risk population; many such men are suitable for active surveillance; applicability to modern MRI-era favourable-intermediate disease is uncertain.
- **Questions for clinician:** If my cancer is low risk, why intervene rather than continue active surveillance?

### Irreversible electroporation

#### Focal therapy of prostate cancer index lesion with IRE

- **Citation:** Blazevski A, et al. *Journal of Urology*. 2023.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1097/JU.0000000000002970](https://doi.org/10.1097/JU.0000000000002970); PMID [36073928](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36073928/)
- **Evidence type:** Single-centre phase II prospective study.
- **Population:** Men with localized Grade Group 1–2 prostate cancer, PSA ≤15 ng/mL, ≤cT2b, selected by mpMRI and transperineal systematic/targeted biopsy.
- **Main finding:** Evaluated 3-year oncologic, safety, and quality-of-life outcomes after IRE index-lesion ablation.
- **Limitations:** Single-centre, selected patients, not randomized against radical therapy or surveillance.
- **Questions for clinician:** What is the centre’s 12-month and 3-year in-field and out-of-field positive biopsy rate?

#### PRESERVE trial: IRE for intermediate-risk prostate cancer

- **Citation:** *European Urology*. 2026.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.eururo.2025.06.003](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eururo.2025.06.003); PMID [40685282](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40685282/)
- **Evidence type:** Prospective nonrandomized single-arm pivotal trial.
- **Population:** 121 patients with organ-confined Grade Group 2 or 3 prostate cancer, clinical stage ≤T2c, PSA ≤15 ng/mL or PSA density <0.15 ng/mL².
- **Main finding:** Reported 12-month in-field negative biopsy and safety endpoints after NanoKnife IRE.
- **Limitations:** Single-arm, 12-month primary endpoint, intermediate-risk selection, not long-term metastasis/survival validation.
- **Questions for clinician:** Does a negative in-field biopsy address out-of-field disease and long-term recurrence risk?

### Focal laser ablation

#### Focal laser ablation phase I trial

- **Citation:** Eggener SE, et al. *Journal of Urology*. 2016.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.juro.2015.12.083](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.juro.2015.12.083); PMID [26748164](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26748164/)
- **Evidence type:** Phase I clinical trial.
- **Population:** Eight men with intermediate-risk prostate cancer and MRI/US fusion diagnosis.
- **Main finding:** Assessed safety and exploratory 6-month cancer control after MRI-guided focal laser ablation.
- **Limitations:** Very small early-phase study; short follow-up; investigational.
- **Questions for clinician:** Is focal laser being offered in a trial or registry with mandatory biopsy follow-up?

#### MRI-guided focal laser ablation: 3-year single-arm trial

- **Citation:** *Diagnostic and Interventional Radiology*. 2021.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.jvir.2018.09.016](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jvir.2018.09.016); PMID [34003127](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34003127/)
- **Evidence type:** Prospective single-arm single-centre trial.
- **Population:** 15 men with Gleason 3+3 or 3+4 prostate cancer.
- **Main finding:** Reported feasibility, safety, quality-of-life, PSA, MRI, and biopsy outcomes over follow-up.
- **Limitations:** Small sample; no randomized comparator; local expertise may not generalize.
- **Questions for clinician:** What is the expected ablation margin and how will treatment success be confirmed?

### Focal radiation boost

#### FLAME randomized phase III trial

- **Citation:** Kerkmeijer LGW, et al. *Journal of Clinical Oncology*. 2021.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1200/JCO.20.02873](https://doi.org/10.1200/JCO.20.02873); PMID [33471548](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33471548/)
- **Evidence type:** Multicentre randomized phase III trial.
- **Population:** 571 patients with intermediate- and high-risk localized prostate cancer.
- **Main finding:** Adding a simultaneous focal boost up to 95 Gy to the MRI-visible intraprostatic lesion improved 5-year biochemical disease-free survival compared with standard whole-prostate EBRT, while prioritizing organ-at-risk constraints.
- **Limitations:** Biochemical endpoint; requires high-quality imaging/planning; not focal ablation; implementation depends on radiation expertise and anatomy.
- **Questions for radiation oncologist:** Is there a clear dominant lesion suitable for boost, and do rectal, bladder, urethral, and penile-bulb constraints allow safe escalation?

#### Patterns of failure after FLAME

- **Citation:** Groen VH, et al. *European Urology*. 2022.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.eururo.2021.12.012](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eururo.2021.12.012); PMID [34953603](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34953603/)
- **Evidence type:** Secondary analysis of randomized trial.
- **Patient relevance:** Helps answer whether focal boost changes local and metastatic failure patterns.
- **Main finding:** Analyzed local failure-free and regional/distant metastasis-free survival after focal boost versus standard EBRT.
- **Limitations:** Secondary analysis; imaging/failure definitions matter.
- **Questions for clinician:** Is the expected benefit mainly biochemical control, local control, or downstream metastasis reduction?

#### ESTRO focal boost consensus recommendations

- **Citation:** *Radiotherapy and Oncology*. 2026.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.radonc.2026.111642](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.radonc.2026.111642); PMID [42399065](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42399065/)
- **Evidence type:** Clinical practice consensus recommendations.
- **Patient relevance:** Practical guidance for centres implementing prostate focal boost.
- **Main finding:** Provides suggestions for focal boosting of intraprostatic tumour(s) after FLAME.
- **Limitations:** Consensus implementation guidance; not a new randomized trial.
- **Questions for clinician:** Does the centre follow ESTRO-style contouring, dose, margin, and organ-at-risk guidance?

#### HDR brachytherapy with focal dose escalation

- **Citation:** *Radiotherapy and Oncology*. 2021.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.radonc.2021.03.018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.radonc.2021.03.018); PMID [33766703](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33766703/)
- **Evidence type:** Phase II clinical trial with mature results.
- **Population:** 50 patients with localized prostate cancer receiving single-fraction HDR brachytherapy with focal dose escalation to the dominant lesion and dose de-escalation to remaining prostate.
- **Main finding:** Evaluated biochemical, local recurrence-free, metastasis-free, and toxicity outcomes.
- **Limitations:** Phase II, small sample, technique-specific, not broadly generalizable.
- **Questions for clinician:** Is focal HDR boost appropriate for my prostate size, urinary function, lesion location, and risk group?

## Practical clinician discussion checklist

### Disease mapping

- Is the lesion clearly visible on high-quality mpMRI?
- Has disease been confirmed with both targeted and systematic biopsy, preferably transperineal/template mapping if focal therapy is being seriously considered?
- Is clinically significant cancer unilateral, unifocal, or truly concentrated enough to target?
- Is there any contralateral Grade Group ≥2 disease?
- Is there extracapsular extension, seminal vesicle invasion, nodal disease, or metastasis?
- Would PSMA PET add anything in this risk group, or is MRI/biopsy the main decision tool?

### Treatment choice

- Is this being offered as standard care, clinical trial, registry, or private-market treatment?
- Why focal therapy instead of active surveillance, radical prostatectomy, EBRT, brachytherapy, or focal boost radiotherapy?
- What functional outcome is most important: erections, continence, urinary symptoms, bowel safety, recovery time?
- If the cancer is low risk, why treat rather than observe?
- If the cancer is unfavourable intermediate/high risk, why focal therapy rather than whole-gland curative therapy?

### Modality and anatomy

- Which modality is best for this lesion location: HIFU, cryo, IRE, laser, VTP, TULSA, focal boost RT, or brachy boost?
- What ablation or boost margin will be used around the MRI lesion?
- How close is the lesion to urethra, sphincter, rectum, bladder neck, capsule, neurovascular bundle, or apex?
- Is a hemi-gland or subtotal treatment actually being proposed, and how much functional advantage remains?

### Validation and follow-up

- What is the centre’s own positive in-field and out-of-field biopsy rate?
- What is the chance of needing repeat focal therapy?
- What is the chance of needing later prostatectomy, radiation, ADT, or systemic therapy?
- What follow-up MRI and biopsy schedule is mandatory?
- What PSA change is expected after focal therapy, and what PSA pattern triggers MRI/biopsy?
- If focal therapy fails, what salvage options remain and how toxic are they?

## Repository interpretation

This page fills a gap in the Localized Treatment section by separating:

1. **Focal ablation** — HIFU, cryotherapy, IRE, laser, VTP, TULSA and related partial-gland approaches, which aim to spare tissue but remain variably validated and often investigational/trial/registry-based.
2. **Focal radiation boost** — a different concept, where the whole prostate is treated and the dominant lesion receives extra dose; this has stronger randomized evidence from FLAME for selected intermediate/high-risk radiotherapy candidates.
3. **Patient selection** — focal treatment is most plausible when clinically significant disease is MRI-visible, biopsy-confirmed, localized, and regionally concentrated, and least plausible when disease is multifocal, bilateral, high-volume/high-grade, poorly mapped, or beyond the capsule.
4. **Follow-up discipline** — focal therapy does not eliminate surveillance; MRI and repeat biopsy are central because residual in-field or out-of-field cancer can remain.
