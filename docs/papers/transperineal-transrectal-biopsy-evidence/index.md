# Transperineal vs Transrectal Prostate Biopsy: Evidence Map

Retrieved: **2026-07-04**  
Categories: **Diagnostic Workup; Screening & Early Detection; Guidelines & Care Pathways**  
Scope: prostate biopsy route choice, infection/sepsis risk, cancer detection, pain/tolerability, anesthesia, antibiotic stewardship, circumstances where each route is used, and trends over time.

!!! warning "Not medical advice"
    This page summarizes evidence for education and clinician discussion. It does not decide whether a biopsy is needed or which route is best for an individual patient. Biopsy decisions depend on PSA pattern, MRI findings, DRE, prostate size/location of lesions, infection risk, anticoagulants, anatomy, available local expertise, anesthesia options, and patient preference.

## What the two approaches mean

| Approach | Needle path | Typical setting | Main advantages | Main trade-offs |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Transrectal biopsy (TR/TRUS)** | Needle passes through rectal wall into prostate | Historically office-based under local anesthesia; still common in many centres | Familiar, fast, widely available, simpler positioning/equipment in established practices | Higher infection/sepsis concern because rectal bacteria can be introduced; requires antibiotic prophylaxis and often rectal antisepsis/culture-based targeting |
| **Transperineal biopsy (TP)** | Needle passes through cleaned perineal skin between scrotum and anus | Previously often general anesthesia/template grid; now increasingly local-anesthetic office/freehand or MRI-fusion targeted | Lower infectious risk, better antibiotic stewardship, good anterior/apical access, avoids rectal wall | May require lithotomy positioning, perineal local anesthesia, different equipment/training; can be more immediately uncomfortable/embarrassing |

## Circumstances where each route may be used

### Situations that often favor transperineal biopsy

- Higher concern about infection or antimicrobial resistance.
- Prior post-biopsy infection, resistant rectal flora, recurrent UTIs, diabetes/immunosuppression, or other infection-risk considerations.
- Need to reduce or avoid antibiotic exposure where clinically appropriate.
- MRI lesions in anterior, apical, or transition-zone locations that may be easier to sample via a perineal route.
- Repeat biopsy after prior negative transrectal biopsy but persistent suspicion.
- Local centre has strong office-based local-anesthetic transperineal expertise.
- Patient preference to avoid rectal needle passage.

### Situations where transrectal biopsy may still be used

- Local transperineal service is unavailable or waiting time is substantially longer.
- Patient cannot tolerate lithotomy positioning or perineal access.
- The centre has a well-established transrectal pathway with targeted antibiotic prophylaxis, rectal preparation, and low documented infection rates.
- Posterior/peripheral-zone target is straightforward and operator expertise is strongest transrectally.
- Specific anatomy, equipment, sedation, or procedural constraints make transrectal approach more practical.

### Situations requiring individualized planning either way

- Anticoagulants/antiplatelets or bleeding risk.
- Prior rectal surgery, absent rectum, inflammatory bowel disease, anal/rectal pathology.
- Severe urinary symptoms or high urinary-retention risk.
- Large prostate, small/anterior MRI lesions, or prior negative biopsy.
- Need for systematic plus MRI-targeted sampling.
- Anxiety, pain tolerance, sedation preference, and local anesthesia plan.

## Trends over time

1. **Traditional era:** transrectal ultrasound-guided biopsy became the dominant office procedure because it was technically simple and widely available.
2. **Infection concern era:** increasing fluoroquinolone resistance and post-biopsy sepsis concerns led to targeted prophylaxis, augmented antibiotics, rectal povidone-iodine preparation, and renewed interest in transperineal biopsy.
3. **MRI-targeted era:** pre-biopsy MRI and fusion/cognitive targeting changed sampling strategy; route choice became tied to lesion location, targeted plus systematic cores, and local technology.
4. **Modern transition:** local-anesthetic, freehand, office-based transperineal biopsy reduced the historical need for general anesthesia/template-grid operating-theatre biopsy.
5. **Guideline direction:** European guidance now strongly favors transperineal biopsy because of low infectious risk and antibiotic stewardship. Some other systems still allow either route, especially where transrectal infection prevention is optimized.
6. **Current nuance:** recent RCTs show transperineal’s infection advantage may be smaller when transrectal biopsy uses targeted prophylaxis and rectal preparation. The direction of travel still favors transperineal adoption, but transrectal biopsy remains clinically viable in well-run pathways.

## Evidence synthesis

### Infection and sepsis

The central safety concern is that transrectal biopsy traverses rectal mucosa. EAU guideline text reports higher infectious complications after transrectal biopsy than transperineal biopsy in pooled randomized data, sepsis rates of 0.1% for transperineal versus 0.9% for transrectal biopsy in a large systematic review, and lower UK readmission rates for sepsis after transperineal versus transrectal biopsy.

However, modern RCTs complicate a simple “TP always safer” message. PREVENT found 0 infections in transperineal biopsy without antibiotics versus 1.4% after transrectal biopsy with rectal-swab-targeted prophylaxis; the difference did not reach conventional statistical significance. ProBE-PC found similar composite infectious complication rates between routes when performed in office settings.

**Interpretation:** transperineal biopsy is favored for infection prevention and antibiotic stewardship, but high-quality transrectal pathways can achieve low infection rates.

### Cancer detection

For clinically significant prostate cancer detection, route differences are less consistent. PREVENT reported similar clinically significant cancer detection: 53% transperineal vs 50% transrectal. A 2024 prospective-study meta-analysis found no significant difference in clinically significant cancer detection between MRI-targeted TP and TR biopsy.

TRANSLATE, a large UK randomized trial, found higher detection of Gleason Grade Group ≥2 cancer with local-anesthetic transperineal biopsy than transrectal biopsy. Earlier meta-analysis and some observational MRI-fusion studies suggested transperineal biopsy may better detect anterior prostate clinically significant cancer.

**Interpretation:** both routes can diagnose clinically significant cancer. Transperineal may be advantageous for anterior/apical access and in some modern pathways, but local technique, MRI quality, targeting, and sampling plan matter.

### Pain, tolerability, and anesthesia

Historically, transperineal biopsy was associated with template mapping and general/spinal anesthesia. Modern local-anesthetic transperineal biopsy is feasible. A systematic review of local-anesthetic transperineal biopsy found low procedural abandonment and low infection-related complications, with greatest pain during anesthetic administration.

PREVENT found slightly worse periprocedural pain with transperineal biopsy, but the effect was small and resolved by 7 days. TRANSLATE reported more immediate pain and embarrassment with local-anesthetic transperineal biopsy than transrectal biopsy, while longer-term urinary and sexual function outcomes were not meaningfully different.

**Interpretation:** transperineal biopsy is no longer necessarily an operating-room procedure, but patients should ask exactly how anesthesia and positioning are handled.

### Antibiotic stewardship

Transrectal biopsy generally requires antibiotic prophylaxis; in higher-risk settings, rectal swab-targeted antibiotics, augmented prophylaxis, and rectal povidone-iodine preparation may be used. Transperineal biopsy can often be performed with no or reduced antibiotics in low-risk patients, depending on local protocol and patient risk factors.

**Interpretation:** antibiotic stewardship is one of the strongest system-level reasons for the shift toward transperineal biopsy.

## Selected papers and guidance

### EAU Guidelines — Diagnostic Evaluation: prostate biopsy route and infection prevention

- **Source:** European Association of Urology prostate cancer guideline, diagnostic evaluation chapter.
- **URL:** https://uroweb.org/guidelines/prostate-cancer/chapter/diagnostic-evaluation
- **Category:** Guidelines & Care Pathways; Diagnostic Workup
- **Evidence type:** Clinical guideline synthesis.
- **Patient relevance:** Provides current guideline direction and infection-prevention recommendations.
- **Main finding:** EAU recommends performing prostate biopsy using the transperineal approach due to low infectious complication risk and better antibiotic stewardship; it also recommends rectal povidone-iodine preparation and targeted/augmented prophylaxis for transrectal biopsy.
- **Limitations:** Guidelines synthesize population-level evidence; local expertise and patient factors still matter.
- **Questions for clinician:** Does this centre follow a transperineal-first approach, and if not, what infection-prevention protocol is used for transrectal biopsy?

### PREVENT randomized trial — transperineal without antibiotics vs transrectal with targeted prophylaxis

- **Citation:** Hu JC, et al. *European Urology*. 2024; also summarized in *JAMA Oncology* 2024.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.eururo.2023.12.015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eururo.2023.12.015); PMID [38212178](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38212178/). JAMA Oncology PMID [39298143](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39298143/).
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38212178/
- **Evidence type:** Multicenter randomized trial.
- **Population:** 658 biopsy-naïve participants randomized to transperineal biopsy without antibiotic prophylaxis vs transrectal biopsy with rectal culture-targeted prophylaxis.
- **Main finding:** 0 infections in transperineal vs 4 infections (1.4%) in transrectal; clinically significant cancer detection was similar (53% vs 50%). Transperineal pain was slightly higher during the procedure but resolved by 7 days.
- **Patient relevance:** Strong modern evidence that office transperineal biopsy can avoid antibiotics without compromising detection.
- **Limitations:** Transrectal comparator used optimized targeted prophylaxis; results may differ where prophylaxis is less rigorous.
- **Questions for clinician:** If transrectal biopsy is offered, is rectal-swab-targeted prophylaxis used?

### ProBE-PC randomized clinical trial — complications after transrectal and transperineal biopsy

- **Citation:** *Journal of Urology*. 2024.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1097/JU.0000000000003788](https://doi.org/10.1097/JU.0000000000003788); PMID [37976319](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37976319/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37976319/
- **Evidence type:** Prospective pragmatic randomized clinical trial.
- **Population:** 718 men who underwent office transrectal or transperineal prostate biopsy under local anesthesia.
- **Main finding:** Composite infectious complications were similar: 2.6% transrectal and 2.7% transperineal; no sepsis occurred in either group; noninfectious complications were also similar.
- **Patient relevance:** Shows both approaches can be clinically viable and safe in controlled office settings.
- **Limitations:** Composite endpoint included relatively broad infection-related events; local protocols matter.
- **Questions for clinician:** What outcomes does this practice track—sepsis, hospitalization, antibiotics, fever, urinary retention, bleeding?

### TRANSLATE randomized trial — local-anesthetic transperineal vs transrectal biopsy

- **Citation:** *Lancet Oncology*. 2025.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/S1470-2045(25)00100-7](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1470-2045(25)00100-7); PMID [40139210](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40139210/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40139210/
- **Evidence type:** Multicenter randomized controlled trial.
- **Population:** 1,126 biopsy-naïve UK participants investigated for possible prostate cancer after pre-biopsy MRI.
- **Main finding:** Gleason Grade Group ≥2 cancer was detected in 60% assigned to local-anesthetic transperineal biopsy vs 54% assigned to transrectal biopsy. Hospital admission for infection was <1% in LATP vs 2% in TRUS. Immediate pain/embarrassment was more common with LATP.
- **Patient relevance:** Large modern RCT directly comparing local-anesthetic transperineal and transrectal biopsy.
- **Limitations:** UK pathway; mostly White British participants; excludes some high-risk infection/anatomy groups.
- **Questions for clinician:** Does local-anesthetic transperineal biopsy improve sampling for my MRI lesion, and how is comfort managed?

### Transperineal versus transrectal MRI-targeted prostate biopsy — systematic review and meta-analysis of prospective studies

- **Citation:** *European Urology Oncology*. 2024.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.euo.2024.07.009](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.euo.2024.07.009); PMID [39095298](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39095298/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39095298/
- **Evidence type:** Systematic review/meta-analysis of prospective randomized trials.
- **Population:** PREVENT, ProBE-PC, and PERFECT trials.
- **Main finding:** No significant difference in clinically significant cancer detection, infection, sepsis, urinary retention, or pain by 7 days; transperineal biopsy can omit antibiotics without increasing infection risk.
- **Patient relevance:** Balanced synthesis of the newest prospective evidence.
- **Limitations:** Only three RCTs; heterogeneous protocols and pain reporting.
- **Questions for clinician:** Is the main advantage in my case infection risk, lesion access, antibiotic avoidance, or all three?

### Local-anesthetic transperineal prostate biopsy — systematic review

- **Citation:** *BJU International*. 2023.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1111/bju.15906](https://doi.org/10.1111/bju.15906); PMID [36177521](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36177521/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36177521/
- **Evidence type:** Systematic review.
- **Population:** 35 publications, 113,944 men undergoing local-anesthetic transperineal biopsy.
- **Main finding:** Local-anesthetic transperineal biopsy had a low infection-related complication rate, low procedural abandonment, and viable diagnostic performance; greatest pain scores were during local anesthetic administration.
- **Patient relevance:** Addresses the practical question: “Can TP biopsy be done without general anesthesia?”
- **Limitations:** High heterogeneity and publication bias; limited formal cost analyses.
- **Questions for clinician:** Is this performed under local anesthetic, sedation, or general anesthesia at this centre?

### Transperineal MRI-targeted biopsy may perform better for anterior clinically significant cancer

- **Citation:** *Clinical Genitourinary Cancer*. 2019.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.clgc.2019.05.006](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clgc.2019.05.006); PMID [31281065](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31281065/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31281065/
- **Evidence type:** Systematic review/meta-analysis.
- **Population:** Men with positive mpMRI undergoing TP or TR MRI-targeted biopsy.
- **Main finding:** TP MRI-targeted biopsy detected more clinically significant cancer in the included studies, especially anterior-zone lesions.
- **Patient relevance:** Lesion location may influence route choice.
- **Limitations:** Older evidence, smaller datasets, nonrandomized comparisons; newer RCTs are more mixed.
- **Questions for clinician:** Is my MRI lesion anterior/apical or otherwise difficult to sample transrectally?

### Prostate cancer detection and complications of TP vs TR MRI-fusion guided biopsies

- **Citation:** *Urology*. 2023.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/j.urology.2023.04.003](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.urology.2023.04.003); PMID [37059232](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37059232/)
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37059232/
- **Evidence type:** Retrospective MRI-fusion biopsy comparison.
- **Population:** 361 patients undergoing TP or TR MRI-targeted biopsy with concurrent systematic biopsy.
- **Main finding:** Clinically significant cancer detection and complication rates did not differ significantly by route.
- **Patient relevance:** Supports the idea that both routes can work well when MRI-targeting and systematic sampling are performed properly.
- **Limitations:** Retrospective, single system/time period, possible selection bias.
- **Questions for clinician:** Is operator experience more important than route in this particular practice?

## Clinician discussion checklist

- Is biopsy needed now, or should PSA/MRI/risk calculation be repeated first?
- If biopsy is needed, why transperineal or why transrectal in my case?
- What is the centre’s infection/sepsis/hospitalization rate for each route?
- Will the biopsy be MRI-targeted, systematic, or both?
- Where is the MRI lesion: anterior, apical, posterior, transition zone, or peripheral zone?
- What anesthesia is used: local anesthetic, sedation, spinal/general anesthesia?
- What pain, bleeding, urinary retention, infection, and sexual/urinary side-effect rates should I expect?
- If transrectal, will there be rectal swab-targeted antibiotics, augmented prophylaxis, and/or povidone-iodine rectal preparation?
- If transperineal, are antibiotics omitted or used, and what infection-risk factors would change that?
- How are anticoagulants/antiplatelets managed?
- How many cores will be taken and how will samples be labeled for pathology?
- What symptoms after biopsy require urgent contact?

## Repository interpretation

This evidence strengthens the **Diagnostic Workup** category by adding biopsy-route decision context:

1. **Transperineal is increasingly favored** because it avoids rectal mucosa, reduces infection concern, and improves antibiotic stewardship.
2. **Transrectal remains viable** where local expertise is strong and infection-prevention protocols are rigorous.
3. **Cancer detection is broadly similar in many modern studies**, though transperineal may help with anterior/apical sampling and TRANSLATE found higher Grade Group ≥2 detection.
4. **The best route is contextual**: MRI lesion location, patient anatomy, infection risk, anticoagulation, anesthesia tolerance, local equipment, and operator experience all matter.
