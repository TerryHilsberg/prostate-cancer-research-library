# Hormone Levels and Prostate Cancer Incidence: Evidence Map

**Retrieval date:** 2026-07-07  
**Primary category:** Screening & Early Detection; Imaging & Biomarkers  
**Scope:** Evidence on circulating sex hormones and related biomarkers — testosterone, free testosterone, dihydrotestosterone, sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG), luteinising hormone (LH), and insulin-like growth factor-I (IGF-I) — in relation to prostate cancer incidence, aggressive disease, and cancer mortality in men.

!!! warning "Educational research support — not medical advice"
    This page summarizes evidence for clinician discussion. Hormone measurements are **not** presented here as a stand-alone prostate cancer screening test, a diagnostic test, or a reason to start/stop testosterone or other hormone-related therapy without medical advice. Prostate cancer assessment still depends on the clinical context, PSA trend, DRE where used, MRI, biopsy/pathology, risk grouping, and specialist interpretation.

## Patient-facing bottom line

| Question | Current evidence signal | Patient interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Do male hormone levels appear biologically relevant to prostate cancer risk? | Yes, but the pattern is complex. Recent large evidence links SHBG and LH with incident prostate cancer risk, while testosterone itself is not consistently associated across all designs. | Hormone biology is relevant, but no single hormone result should be interpreted as “causing” or “excluding” prostate cancer. |
| Does the 2026 Lancet Healthy Longevity paper matter? | Yes. It used individual participant data meta-analysis, mass-spectrometry testosterone measurement, long follow-up, and adjustment for major baseline factors. It found lower SHBG and lower LH were associated with higher incident prostate cancer risk; testosterone was **not** associated with incident prostate cancer in that analysis. | This is important evidence for a biomarker/risk-context page, especially because it is recent and methodologically strong. |
| Does higher testosterone increase prostate cancer incidence? | Mixed. Observational analyses in UK Biobank and large consortia have linked higher **free testosterone** with higher prostate cancer risk; Mendelian-randomization studies also suggest genetically higher testosterone/free testosterone may increase risk. The 2026 IPD meta-analysis did **not** find total testosterone associated with incident prostate cancer. | A useful clinician question is whether total testosterone, free testosterone, SHBG, and LH mean different things biologically and clinically. |
| Are hormone levels ready for routine risk stratification? | Not yet. Evidence supports further investigation as risk biomarkers, but routine use for prostate cancer incidence prediction is not established. | Worth discussing if hormone tests are already being done, but not a substitute for guideline-based prostate assessment. |
| What is the biggest caution? | Detection bias, confounding, age/body-composition effects, assay differences, and reverse causality can all affect interpretation. | Avoid using a single hormone result to make cancer decisions without specialist context. |

## Why the Lancet paper is significant

The user-supplied paper is:

> Marriott RJ, Murray K, Antonio L, Adams RJ, Ballantyne CM, Bauer DC, Bhasin S, Biggs ML, et al. **Associations of testosterone, sex hormone-binding globulin, and related hormones with risks of cancer death, incident cancer, and incident prostate cancer in men: individual participant data meta-analyses.** *The Lancet Healthy Longevity*. 2026; article 100857. DOI: [10.1016/j.lanhl.2026.100857](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lanhl.2026.100857). PMID: [42320510](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42320510/).

What makes it notable:

- **Study type:** systematic review plus individual participant data (IPD) meta-analyses.
- **Population:** community-dwelling men in prospective cohort studies.
- **Scale:** 10 IPD studies with 24,510 men and 276,931 participant-years for cancer death analyses; five IPD studies with 12,280 men and 918 incident prostate cancer events.
- **Measurement quality:** included studies required testosterone measurement by mass spectrometry and at least 5 years' follow-up.
- **Adjustment:** analyses controlled for baseline age, previous cancer, BMI, marital status, alcohol, smoking, physical activity, hypertension, and diabetes.
- **Key prostate-cancer finding:** lower SHBG and lower LH were associated with higher incident prostate cancer risk; total testosterone was not associated with incident prostate cancer in this analysis.
- **Cancer-mortality finding:** lower total testosterone and lower dihydrotestosterone were associated with higher cancer death risk, and mid-range SHBG/LH were associated with lower cancer death risk.
- **Interpretation from the authors:** sex hormones could serve as biomarkers for cancer risk and warrant further investigation.

## Evidence by clinical question

### 1) Before diagnosis / incidence risk

The main question is whether hormone levels help identify men at higher probability of eventually being diagnosed with prostate cancer.

Current pattern:

- **SHBG and LH:** the 2026 IPD meta-analysis found lower SHBG and lower LH associated with higher incident prostate cancer risk.
- **Free testosterone:** 2021 and 2022 consortium/UK Biobank analyses found higher free testosterone associated with prostate cancer risk, including signals for aggressive disease in Mendelian-randomization analyses.
- **Total testosterone:** results are less consistent; the 2026 IPD meta-analysis did not find total testosterone associated with incident prostate cancer, and the 2021 UK Biobank analysis did not find total testosterone associated with prostate cancer.
- **IGF-I:** evidence from UK Biobank supports an association between higher IGF-I and prostate cancer diagnosis and mortality, making it a related but distinct hormone/growth-factor pathway.

Patient-facing implication:

- Hormone levels may eventually contribute to risk models, but they are not currently a replacement for PSA interpretation, MRI decisions, biopsy decisions, family-history/genetic risk assessment, or clinician judgment.

### 2) Aggressive or early-onset disease

Some evidence suggests free testosterone may matter more for aggressive prostate cancer than total testosterone:

- A 2022 International Journal of Cancer analysis reported free testosterone associated with aggressive disease in Mendelian-randomization analyses and with overall prostate cancer in both Mendelian-randomization and blood-based analyses.
- However, observational blood-based analyses did not show a simple overall association with aggressive disease, and the authors reported age-related heterogeneity.

Patient-facing implication:

- The strongest practical interpretation is not “high testosterone causes aggressive cancer,” but rather that **free testosterone and binding proteins deserve separate attention** in research and risk discussions.

### 3) Cancer mortality and broader health trade-offs

The 2026 Lancet Healthy Longevity paper found lower total testosterone and lower dihydrotestosterone associated with higher cancer death risk. This does not mean low testosterone causes cancer death; it could reflect frailty, illness, body composition, inflammation, comorbidity, or other biological pathways.

Patient-facing implication:

- A low testosterone result can be clinically meaningful for general health, symptoms, frailty, sexual function, bone, metabolic health, and quality of life — but the prostate cancer relevance should be interpreted cautiously.

### 4) Testosterone therapy or supplementation

This evidence map is **not** a testosterone-therapy recommendation page. The prostate-cancer safety of testosterone treatment depends on indication, baseline prostate assessment, prior prostate cancer history, PSA monitoring, formulation/dose, age, comorbidities, and specialist guidance.

Patient-facing implication:

- Men considering testosterone therapy should ask how baseline PSA/risk assessment, follow-up PSA monitoring, urinary symptoms, family history, and prior biopsy/MRI history affect the decision.
- Men already diagnosed with prostate cancer should discuss testosterone, androgen deprivation therapy, or other hormone-related questions with the treating urologist, radiation oncologist, or medical oncologist.

## Selected papers

### 1) Marriott et al., 2026 — IPD meta-analysis of testosterone, SHBG, related hormones, cancer death, and incident prostate cancer

- **Citation:** Marriott RJ, Murray K, Antonio L, Adams RJ, Ballantyne CM, Bauer DC, Bhasin S, Biggs ML, et al. *The Lancet Healthy Longevity*. 2026; article 100857.
- **DOI/PMID/URL:** DOI [10.1016/j.lanhl.2026.100857](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lanhl.2026.100857); PMID [42320510](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42320510/); Lancet page supplied by user: <https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanhl/article/PIIS2666-7568(26)00041-3/fulltext>.
- **Evidence type:** Systematic review and individual participant data meta-analyses of prospective cohorts.
- **Population:** Community-dwelling men; cancer-death analyses included 24,510 men; incident prostate cancer analyses included 12,280 men and 918 events.
- **Patient relevance:** Strong recent evidence that hormone-binding and pituitary-axis markers may be relevant to prostate cancer incidence risk.
- **Main finding:** Lower SHBG and lower LH were associated with higher incident prostate cancer risk; testosterone was not associated with incident prostate cancer in this analysis. Lower total testosterone and lower dihydrotestosterone were associated with higher cancer death risk.
- **Limitations:** Observational cohort evidence; hormone levels can reflect health status, age, body composition, comorbidity, and assay/timing factors; not proof that modifying hormone levels changes prostate cancer risk.
- **Clinician questions:** Could SHBG/LH/free testosterone add risk-context when hormone tests already exist? Are these findings relevant to my age, symptoms, BMI/metabolic health, PSA trend, and family history?

### 2) Watts et al., 2022 — Free testosterone and aggressive prostate cancer in international consortia

- **Citation:** Watts EL, Perez-Cornago A, Fensom GK, Smith-Byrne K, Noor U, Andrews CD, Gunter MJ, Holmes MV, et al. *International Journal of Cancer*. 2022.
- **DOI/PMID/URL:** DOI [10.1002/ijc.34116](https://doi.org/10.1002/ijc.34116); PMID [35579976](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35579976/).
- **Evidence type:** Prospective biomarker analyses plus Mendelian-randomization analyses.
- **Population:** Up to 25 studies with 14,944 cases and 36,752 controls for blood-based analyses; PRACTICAL/UK Biobank genetic analyses included up to 79,148 cases and 61,106 controls.
- **Patient relevance:** Addresses whether free testosterone, not just total testosterone, may be relevant to prostate cancer development and aggressive subgroups.
- **Main finding:** Free testosterone was associated with aggressive prostate cancer in Mendelian-randomization analyses and with overall prostate cancer in both MR and blood-based analyses.
- **Limitations:** MR estimates depend on genetic instruments and assumptions; blood-based results varied by age and outcome; not a clinical screening algorithm.
- **Clinician questions:** If testosterone is measured, should free testosterone and SHBG be interpreted separately from total testosterone?

### 3) Watts et al., 2021 — IGF-I, testosterone, SHBG, and prostate cancer risk in UK Biobank

- **Citation:** Watts EL, Fensom GK, Smith Byrne K, Perez-Cornago A, Allen NE, Knuppel A, Gunter MJ, Holmes MV, et al. *International Journal of Cancer*. 2021.
- **DOI/PMID/URL:** DOI [10.1002/ijc.33416](https://doi.org/10.1002/ijc.33416); PMID [33252839](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33252839/).
- **Evidence type:** Large prospective cohort plus Mendelian randomization for IGF-I.
- **Population:** 199,698 male UK Biobank participants; 5,402 prostate cancer diagnoses and 295 prostate cancer deaths.
- **Patient relevance:** Places testosterone biology alongside IGF-I and SHBG in a large cohort with standardized assays.
- **Main finding:** Higher IGF-I was associated with prostate cancer diagnosis and mortality; higher free testosterone was associated with higher prostate cancer risk; higher SHBG was associated with lower risk; total testosterone was not associated with prostate cancer.
- **Limitations:** UK Biobank population and follow-up characteristics; diagnosis risk may be affected by PSA testing patterns and detection; observational hormone associations do not prove benefit from modifying hormone levels.
- **Clinician questions:** Are metabolic, endocrine, or growth-factor issues relevant to my risk profile, and are they being managed for general health as well as cancer-risk context?

### 4) Ruth et al., 2020 — Genetic evidence on testosterone and disease risk

- **Citation:** Ruth KS, Day FR, Tyrrell J, Thompson DJ, Wood AR, Mahajan A, Beaumont RN, Wittemans L, et al. *Nature Medicine*. 2020.
- **DOI/PMID/URL:** DOI [10.1038/s41591-020-0751-5](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-020-0751-5); PMID [32042192](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32042192/).
- **Evidence type:** Genome-wide association and Mendelian-randomization analysis using UK Biobank and disease outcomes.
- **Population:** 425,097 UK Biobank participants for genetic determinants of testosterone and related traits.
- **Patient relevance:** Supports a causal-inference signal that genetically higher testosterone may have disease trade-offs, including prostate cancer risk in men.
- **Main finding:** The authors reported adverse effects of higher testosterone on prostate cancer in men, while also finding sex-specific effects on metabolic traits.
- **Limitations:** Genetic instruments reflect lifelong tendency rather than short-term measured hormone levels or prescribed therapy; MR assumptions and ancestry/population issues matter.
- **Clinician questions:** How should prostate monitoring be handled if testosterone therapy is being considered for symptoms or confirmed hypogonadism?

## Clinician discussion checklist

If hormone levels are part of the discussion, consider asking:

- Was the testosterone test **morning**, repeated, and interpreted with symptoms and SHBG/free testosterone where appropriate?
- Were **total testosterone**, **free testosterone**, **SHBG**, and possibly **LH** interpreted as different signals rather than one interchangeable “testosterone level”?
- Could low testosterone be a marker of general health, obesity/metabolic syndrome, chronic illness, medications, or frailty rather than a direct cancer signal?
- Does my PSA trend, family history, ancestry, MRI findings, prostate volume/PSA density, or prior biopsy matter more than hormone levels for near-term prostate cancer decisions?
- If testosterone therapy is being considered, what baseline prostate assessment and PSA monitoring plan is appropriate?
- If prostate cancer is already diagnosed, which specialist should address testosterone or androgen-axis questions: urologist, radiation oncologist, medical oncologist, or endocrinologist?

## Repository interpretation / what this changes

This page adds a new **hormone-levels and prostate cancer incidence** evidence map because the 2026 Lancet Healthy Longevity IPD meta-analysis is important enough to track as a biomarker/risk-context topic.

Practical interpretation for the repository:

- Hormone levels are a **risk-context and biomarker research topic**, not a stand-alone screening or treatment decision tool.
- The most consistent patient-facing lesson is to distinguish **total testosterone**, **free testosterone**, **SHBG**, and **LH**.
- The topic belongs alongside screening/early detection and biomarker evidence because it may eventually influence risk prediction, but current clinical decisions should still be anchored in guideline-based assessment and specialist interpretation.
