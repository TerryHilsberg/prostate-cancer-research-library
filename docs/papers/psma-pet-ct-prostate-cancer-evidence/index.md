# PSMA PET/CT in Prostate Cancer Diagnosis, Staging, Recurrence, and Treatment: Evidence Map

_Retrieved: 2026-07-04_

**Category:** Imaging & biomarkers; risk stratification & staging; advanced & recurrent disease  
**Scope:** Patient-facing synthesis of where prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA) PET/CT fits in the prostate-cancer journey: initial diagnostic work-up, staging before curative-intent treatment, biochemical recurrence after treatment, selection for PSMA-targeted radioligand therapy, and selected issues in treatment monitoring.

!!! warning "Not medical advice"
    This page is for evidence review and clinician discussion. PSMA PET/CT can change staging and treatment plans, but whether to order it, how to interpret it, and what treatment should follow depends on the full clinical context, local availability, tracer used, prior treatments, pathology, PSA kinetics, symptoms, kidney function, radiation exposure considerations, and specialist judgement.

## Patient-facing bottom line

| Patient question | Evidence-supported answer | Clinician discussion question |
|---|---|---|
| **Can PSMA PET/CT diagnose prostate cancer by itself?** | Not usually. MRI and biopsy remain the core diagnostic pathway. PSMA PET/CT can show suspicious intraprostatic uptake and may help target biopsy in selected settings, but specificity is imperfect and a tissue diagnosis is still central. | "Would PSMA PET add information beyond mpMRI and biopsy in my case, or would it risk confusing the diagnostic pathway?" |
| **When is PSMA PET/CT strongest before treatment?** | Strongest evidence is for **staging higher-risk localized or locally advanced prostate cancer** before curative-intent surgery or radiotherapy. The randomized proPSMA trial found PSMA PET/CT more accurate than CT plus bone scan. | "Given my PSA, Grade Group, MRI stage, and risk category, am I in the group where PSMA PET/CT is likely to change staging or the treatment field?" |
| **What if PSA rises after surgery or radiation?** | PSMA PET/CT is a major tool for localizing biochemical recurrence, often detecting disease when conventional imaging is negative. Detection increases with PSA level, but useful findings can occur at relatively low PSA. | "At my current PSA and PSA doubling time, would a PSMA PET result change salvage radiation, metastasis-directed therapy, systemic therapy, or surveillance?" |
| **Can a negative PSMA PET/CT rule out disease?** | No. Microscopic disease can be below PET resolution or have low PSMA expression. A negative scan may still support treatment such as early salvage radiotherapy if other risk factors indicate it. | "If my scan is negative, what treatment or monitoring would still be recommended based on pathology and PSA kinetics?" |
| **Why does PSMA PET matter for advanced disease?** | It can identify whether tumors express enough PSMA to consider PSMA-targeted radioligand therapy such as lutetium-177 PSMA-617 in selected metastatic castration-resistant settings. | "Does my disease meet imaging and clinical eligibility for PSMA-targeted therapy, and do we need FDG PET to look for PSMA-negative aggressive disease?" |
| **Can PSMA PET/CT be used to monitor ongoing treatment?** | It may help clarify response or progression in some advanced-disease settings, but routine monitoring schedules are not as standardized as PSA, symptoms, CT/bone imaging, and treatment-specific assessments. | "What result on PSMA PET would actually change my current treatment plan, and what are the alternatives for monitoring?" |

The 24-hour rule is well established for **FDG PET/CT**, where exercised muscles can take up more glucose tracer. ## Where PSMA PET/CT fits in the care pathway

### 1. Initial diagnosis: promising adjunct, not a biopsy replacement

PSMA PET/CT detects cellular expression of PSMA, which is commonly high in prostate cancer, but uptake is not perfectly cancer-specific and not all clinically important cancer is equally PSMA-avid. Current diagnostic pathways still rely on PSA context, digital rectal examination where relevant, mpMRI, and prostate biopsy.

The EAU diagnostic-evaluation guideline notes that radiolabelled PSMA PET imaging is mainly used for staging, while it may also help indicate precise locations for targeted biopsies or follow selected active-surveillance candidates. It reports pooled performance for clinically significant prostate cancer detection and describes trials combining PSMA PET with MRI, but this remains an adjunctive role rather than routine first-line diagnosis for every patient.[^eau-diagnostic]

**Patient interpretation:** PSMA PET can sharpen the question "where is disease?" but it does not usually answer "do I have prostate cancer?" without pathology.

### 2. Initial staging before curative-intent treatment: strongest diagnostic evidence

The strongest randomized evidence is in men with **high-risk localized prostate cancer** being staged before curative-intent surgery or radiotherapy.

The Australian **proPSMA** randomized trial compared first-line gallium-68 PSMA-11 PET/CT with conventional CT plus bone scan in high-risk localized prostate cancer. PSMA PET/CT had substantially better accuracy for pelvic nodal or distant metastatic disease:

- accuracy: **92% vs 65%** for conventional imaging;
- sensitivity: **85% vs 38%**;
- specificity: **98% vs 91%**;
- fewer equivocal findings: **7% vs 23%**;
- more management changes as first-line imaging: **28% vs 15%**.[^propsma]

The **OSPREY** phase 2/3 trial of fluorine-18 DCFPyL PET/CT found very high specificity for pelvic nodal disease in high-risk patients undergoing prostatectomy and lymphadenectomy, but sensitivity was limited:

- median specificity: **97.9%**;
- median sensitivity: **40.3%**;
- median positive predictive value: **86.7%**.[^osprey]

**Patient interpretation:** a positive PSMA-avid lymph node or distant lesion is often meaningful and may change the treatment plan, but a negative scan does **not** exclude microscopic nodal disease. Surgical pathology, radiation-field decisions, and systemic-treatment decisions may still depend on risk factors beyond the scan.

### 3. Biochemical recurrence after surgery or radiation: high clinical utility, but not perfect

When PSA rises after definitive treatment, the key practical question is whether disease is local, nodal, bone/visceral metastatic, or not yet visible. PSMA PET/CT is now one of the most important imaging tools for this situation.

The **CONDOR** phase 3 trial studied fluorine-18 DCFPyL PET/CT in men with biochemical recurrence and uninformative standard imaging. Median baseline PSA was 0.8 ng/mL. It found:

- correct localization rate: **84.8%–87.0%** across readers;
- disease detection rate: **59%–66%**;
- intended management changed in **63.9%** of evaluable patients.[^condor]

A histopathology-validated meta-analysis of gallium-68 PSMA-11 PET reported high positive predictive value in recurrence and detection that rose with PSA:

- detection rate **63%** when PSA was less than 2.0 ng/mL;
- detection rate **94%** when PSA was more than 2.0 ng/mL.[^hope-meta]

A prospective Australian multicenter study found PSMA PET/CT changed intended management in **51%** overall and **62%** among patients with biochemical failure after surgery or radiation.[^roach]

**Patient interpretation:** PSMA PET/CT is especially useful when the result will change a salvage plan: prostate-bed radiation alone vs pelvic nodal treatment, metastasis-directed therapy, systemic therapy, clinical trial, or observation. A negative scan can still be compatible with microscopic recurrence.

### 4. Relationship to ongoing treatment and treatment monitoring

PSMA PET/CT has several treatment-related roles, but these should be separated:

1. **Planning curative-intent local treatment:** before surgery or radiotherapy in higher-risk disease, it can reveal nodal or distant disease that changes local vs systemic strategy.
2. **Planning salvage treatment:** after PSA recurrence, it can localize recurrence and help shape salvage radiation fields or metastasis-directed therapy.
3. **Selecting patients for PSMA-targeted radioligand therapy:** in metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer, PSMA PET can identify whether lesions are sufficiently PSMA-positive for therapy such as lutetium-177 PSMA-617.
4. **Monitoring response/progression:** quantitative PSMA PET measures are being studied and may help in selected advanced-disease decisions, but routine monitoring is less standardized than its staging and recurrence-localization roles.

The key patient question is not simply "can we scan?" but **"what decision would change if the scan is positive, negative, mixed, or shows new sites?"**

### 5. Advanced disease and theranostics: imaging linked to treatment eligibility

PSMA PET/CT becomes part of a theranostic pathway when the imaging target can also be treated. In metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer, trials of lutetium-177 PSMA-617 required PSMA-positive imaging, and some protocols also used FDG PET to exclude aggressive PSMA-negative/FDG-positive discordant disease.

The phase 3 **VISION** trial enrolled patients with PSMA-positive metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer previously treated with androgen-receptor pathway inhibition and taxane chemotherapy. Adding lutetium-177 PSMA-617 to standard care improved:

- imaging-based progression-free survival: **8.7 vs 3.4 months**;
- overall survival: **15.3 vs 11.3 months**.[^vision]

The randomized phase 2 **TheraP** trial compared lutetium-177 PSMA-617 with cabazitaxel in men with metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer after docetaxel, using PSMA PET and FDG PET selection. It found:

- PSA50 responses: **66% vs 37%** with cabazitaxel;
- grade 3–4 adverse events: **33% vs 53%** with cabazitaxel.[^therap]

With longer follow-up, TheraP did **not** show an overall-survival difference between randomized groups, supporting lutetium-177 PSMA-617 as an alternative rather than proving superiority over cabazitaxel for survival in that trial.[^therap-os]

The later **PSMAfore** trial, in taxane-naive PSMA-positive metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer after progression on one androgen-receptor pathway inhibitor, reported improved radiographic progression-free survival in earlier publications; a 2025 patient-reported-outcomes analysis found delayed worsening of quality of life, pain, and symptomatic skeletal events with lutetium-177 PSMA-617 compared with ARPI change.[^psmafore]

**Patient interpretation:** PSMA PET is not just a map in advanced disease; it can be a treatment-selection test. But eligibility depends on cancer stage, prior treatments, blood counts, kidney function, symptoms, whole-body disease pattern, PSMA expression, possible PSMA-negative lesions, and drug availability/reimbursement.

## Practical limitations and pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Stage migration** | PSMA PET finds disease that older imaging misses. This can improve targeting, but it also makes outcomes hard to compare with older studies staged by CT/bone scan. |
| **False positives** | Benign ganglia, fractures, inflammation, healing bone lesions, and non-prostate malignancies can sometimes show uptake. Correlation with CT/MRI, pattern, history, and sometimes biopsy is needed. |
| **False negatives** | Small-volume disease may be below resolution; some aggressive or dedifferentiated prostate cancer may be less PSMA-avid. |
| **Tracer differences** | Gallium-68 PSMA-11, fluorine-18 DCFPyL, fluorine-18 PSMA-1007, and other tracers differ in urinary excretion, biodistribution, and false-positive patterns. |
| **Access and reimbursement** | Availability and indication rules differ by country, scanner access, tracer supply, and insurer/public-system criteria. |
| **A scan is not a treatment plan** | The best use of PSMA PET is when the care team has pre-specified what would change based on each possible result. |

## Practical preparation question: exercise before PSMA PET/CT

Some imaging centres tell patients not to exercise for a day before a PET/CT scan. The evidence basis depends on the tracer:

- For **FDG PET/CT**, exercise restriction is guideline-supported. FDG is a glucose analogue, and skeletal muscle activated by strenuous exercise can take up more tracer. The EANM FDG PET/CT tumour-imaging guideline says patients should avoid strenuous exercise for at least 6 hours before FDG PET/CT, and preferably for 24 hours.[^fdg-eanm]
- For **PSMA PET/CT**, I found no direct study showing that strenuous exercise the day before materially degrades scan accuracy, and the joint EANM/SNMMI PSMA PET/CT procedure guideline does **not** list 24-hour exercise avoidance as a standard patient-preparation requirement. Its preparation advice emphasizes no fasting requirement, hydration, medication continuity, bladder/urinary activity management, and timing around new ADT when possible.[^psma-guideline]
- Therefore, a "no exercise for 24 hours" instruction before PSMA PET/CT may reflect a local department's cautious general PET protocol or a rule carried over from FDG PET/CT rather than PSMA-specific evidence.

**Practical patient approach:** follow the imaging centre's written instructions, because local protocols vary and the centre is responsible for image quality. But if the instruction matters—for example, because exercise is part of daily cancer-supportive care—ask whether ordinary walking is acceptable and whether the centre mainly wants to avoid strenuous resistance training, running, cycling, heavy physical work, or unusual exertion in the 24 hours before the scan.

**Clinician/nuclear-medicine question:** "Is the exercise restriction specific to my PSMA tracer and protocol, or is it a general PET rule derived from FDG PET/CT?"

## Selected evidence notes

### Joint EANM/SNMMI PSMA PET/CT procedure guideline — patient preparation

- **Citation:** Fendler WP, Eiber M, Beheshti M, et al. *European Journal of Nuclear Medicine and Molecular Imaging*. 2023.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.2967/jnumed.120.252114](https://doi.org/10.2967/jnumed.120.252114); PMID [36604326](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36604326/)
- **Evidence type:** Joint professional procedure guideline / procedure standard for PSMA PET/CT.
- **Patient relevance:** Best source for standard PSMA PET/CT preparation expectations.
- **Main finding for preparation:** Patients do not need to fast and may take medications; hydration and bladder/urinary activity management are emphasized. The guideline does not give a standard 24-hour exercise-avoidance requirement.
- **Limitations:** Local departments may still issue centre-specific instructions, and protocols may differ by tracer and scanner workflow.
- **Questions for clinician:** "Does your department require exercise restriction for PSMA PET/CT, and if so is this tracer-specific or a general PET precaution?"

### EANM FDG PET/CT tumour-imaging guideline — source of the 24-hour exercise rule

- **Citation:** Boellaard R, Delgado-Bolton R, Oyen WJG, et al. *European Journal of Nuclear Medicine and Molecular Imaging*. 2015.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1007/s00259-014-2961-x](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00259-014-2961-x); PMID [25452219](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25452219/)
- **Evidence type:** Professional procedure guideline for FDG PET/CT tumour imaging.
- **Patient relevance:** Explains why many PET departments ask patients to avoid strenuous exercise before PET.
- **Main finding for exercise:** Patients should avoid strenuous exercise for at least 6 hours before FDG PET/CT, and preferably for 24 hours, to reduce skeletal-muscle and brown-fat FDG uptake.
- **Limitations:** This applies to FDG PET/CT, not directly to PSMA PET/CT.
- **Questions for clinician:** "Is my centre applying the FDG exercise rule to PSMA PET/CT as a precaution?"

### Soffientini et al. 2012 — high muscular FDG uptake can affect lesion quantification

- **Citation:** Soffientini A, et al. *European Journal of Nuclear Medicine and Molecular Imaging*. 2012.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1007/s00259-012-2202-0](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00259-012-2202-0); PMID [22890803](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22890803/)
- **Evidence type:** Retrospective repeat FDG PET/CT analysis.
- **Patient relevance:** Supports the biological rationale for avoiding unnecessary muscle activation before FDG PET.
- **Main finding:** In patients recalled for high muscular FDG uptake, reducing muscular uptake on repeat imaging changed lesion SUV quantification.
- **Limitations:** FDG-specific; not a PSMA PET study.
- **Questions for clinician:** "Does this FDG rationale apply to my PSMA tracer, or is the concern different?"

### Hofman et al. 2020 — proPSMA randomized trial

- **Citation:** Hofman MS, et al. *Lancet*. 2020.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/S0140-6736(20)30314-7](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(20)30314-7); PMID [32209449](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32209449/)
- **Evidence type:** Prospective randomized multicenter diagnostic trial.
- **Population:** Men with high-risk localized prostate cancer before curative-intent surgery or radiotherapy.
- **Patient relevance:** Best evidence that PSMA PET/CT outperforms CT plus bone scan for initial staging in higher-risk disease.
- **Main finding:** Accuracy 92% vs 65%; management change 28% vs 15%; equivocal findings 7% vs 23%.
- **Limitations:** Staging accuracy and management changes do not automatically prove improved long-term survival; results apply mainly to high-risk staging.
- **Questions for clinician:** "Would this result alter surgery, lymph-node dissection, radiation fields, ADT/systemic therapy, or trial eligibility?"

### Pienta et al. 2021 — OSPREY phase 2/3 trial

- **Citation:** Pienta KJ, et al. *Journal of Urology*. 2021.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1097/JU.0000000000001698](https://doi.org/10.1097/JU.0000000000001698); PMID [33634707](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33634707/)
- **Evidence type:** Prospective multicenter diagnostic trial of fluorine-18 DCFPyL PET/CT.
- **Population:** High-risk primary staging cohort plus suspected recurrent/metastatic cohort.
- **Patient relevance:** Supports high specificity/positive predictive value of PSMA-avid lesions, but highlights limited sensitivity for pelvic nodal micrometastases.
- **Main finding:** Pelvic nodal specificity 97.9%; sensitivity 40.3%; PPV 86.7%.
- **Limitations:** A negative scan cannot reliably exclude microscopic pelvic nodal disease.
- **Questions for clinician:** "If my PSMA PET is negative, how will you still estimate microscopic nodal risk?"

### Morris et al. 2021 — CONDOR phase 3 trial

- **Citation:** Morris MJ, et al. *Clinical Cancer Research*. 2021.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1158/1078-0432.CCR-20-4573](https://doi.org/10.1158/1078-0432.CCR-20-4573); PMID [33622706](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33622706/)
- **Evidence type:** Prospective phase 3 multicenter diagnostic trial.
- **Population:** Biochemical recurrence with uninformative standard imaging.
- **Patient relevance:** Directly relevant to a rising PSA after treatment.
- **Main finding:** Correct localization 84.8%–87.0%; disease detection 59%–66%; intended management changed in 63.9%.
- **Limitations:** Management change is not the same as proven improvement in survival or quality of life.
- **Questions for clinician:** "What salvage or systemic treatment decision depends on localizing recurrence now?"

### Hope et al. 2019 — histopathology-validated meta-analysis

- **Citation:** Hope TA, et al. *Journal of Nuclear Medicine*. 2019.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.2967/jnumed.118.219501](https://doi.org/10.2967/jnumed.118.219501); PMID [30530831](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30530831/)
- **Evidence type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis of gallium-68 PSMA-11 PET accuracy.
- **Population:** Initial staging and biochemical recurrence studies with pathology correlation where available.
- **Patient relevance:** Helps explain why PSA level affects the chance of seeing recurrent disease.
- **Main finding:** Initial-staging pelvic nodal sensitivity 0.74 and specificity 0.96 in pooled pathology-correlated studies; biochemical-recurrence detection 63% when PSA <2.0 and 94% when PSA >2.0.
- **Limitations:** Heterogeneity across studies, tracers, scanners, patient groups, and reference standards.
- **Questions for clinician:** "At my PSA, what is the realistic chance of a positive scan and what would we do with each result?"

### Roach et al. 2018 — management-intent study

- **Citation:** Roach PJ, et al. *Journal of Nuclear Medicine*. 2018.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.2967/jnumed.117.197160](https://doi.org/10.2967/jnumed.117.197160); PMID [28646014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28646014/)
- **Evidence type:** Prospective multicenter Australian management-impact study.
- **Population:** Primary and recurrent prostate cancer patients referred for PSMA PET/CT.
- **Patient relevance:** Shows how often clinicians changed intended management after PSMA PET/CT.
- **Main finding:** Management intent changed in 51% overall and 62% after biochemical failure.
- **Limitations:** Change in planned management does not by itself establish better outcomes.
- **Questions for clinician:** "Would this scan change management in a way that is evidence-based and acceptable to me?"

### Sartor et al. 2021 — VISION trial

- **Citation:** Sartor O, et al. *New England Journal of Medicine*. 2021.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1056/NEJMoa2107322](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2107322); PMID [34161051](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34161051/)
- **Evidence type:** Phase 3 randomized treatment trial.
- **Population:** PSMA-positive metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer after androgen-receptor pathway inhibitor and taxane treatment.
- **Patient relevance:** Shows how PSMA imaging can select patients for PSMA-targeted radioligand therapy.
- **Main finding:** Lutetium-177 PSMA-617 plus standard care improved imaging-based progression-free survival and overall survival.
- **Limitations:** Applies to selected advanced disease, not early localized prostate cancer; treatment has hematologic, renal, salivary, fatigue, nausea, and other safety considerations.
- **Questions for clinician:** "Do my scan pattern, prior treatments, symptoms, blood counts, and kidney function make me eligible?"

### Hofman et al. 2021 / 2024 — TheraP trial

- **Citation:** Hofman MS, et al. *Lancet*. 2021; mature OS update in *Lancet Oncology*. 2024.
- **DOI/PMID:** DOI [10.1016/S0140-6736(21)00237-3](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(21)00237-3), PMID [33581798](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33581798/); DOI [10.1016/S1470-2045(23)00529-6](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1470-2045(23)00529-6), PMID [38043558](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38043558/)
- **Evidence type:** Randomized phase 2 treatment trial.
- **Population:** Metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer after docetaxel, eligible for cabazitaxel; selected with PSMA PET and FDG PET.
- **Patient relevance:** Illustrates why PSMA positivity and absence of discordant FDG-positive/PSMA-negative disease matter.
- **Main finding:** Higher PSA50 response and fewer grade 3–4 adverse events than cabazitaxel; no overall-survival difference on mature follow-up.
- **Limitations:** Phase 2 design; selection criteria matter; cross-over and subsequent therapies complicate OS interpretation.
- **Questions for clinician:** "Would FDG PET be useful to identify aggressive PSMA-negative disease before PSMA-targeted therapy?"

### EAU guideline diagnostic-evaluation section

- **Source:** European Association of Urology prostate cancer guideline, diagnostic evaluation chapter.
- **URL:** <https://uroweb.org/guidelines/prostate-cancer/chapter/diagnostic-evaluation>
- **Evidence type:** Guideline synthesis.
- **Patient relevance:** Places PSMA PET/CT in context: mainly staging, with selected adjunctive diagnostic roles.
- **Main finding:** PSMA PET is mainly used for staging; mpMRI remains central for primary diagnosis; PSMA PET + MRI may improve sensitivity/NPV in selected diagnostic studies but can reduce specificity.
- **Limitations:** Guideline recommendations change over time and vary by region; local availability and reimbursement matter.
- **Questions for clinician:** "Which guideline or local policy are you using for PSMA PET in my situation?"

## Clinician discussion checklist

Bring these questions to the treating urologist, radiation oncologist, medical oncologist, or nuclear medicine physician:

- **Indication:** Is the scan for initial staging, recurrence localization, treatment selection, or monitoring?
- **Decision impact:** What exact decision would change if the scan is positive, negative, equivocal, or shows distant disease?
- **Risk group:** Does my Grade Group, PSA, PSA density, MRI stage, clinical stage, or PSA doubling time place me in a group where PSMA PET is evidence-supported?
- **Tracer:** Which tracer is available locally, and what are its common pitfalls?
- **Preparation:** Does your centre require avoiding strenuous exercise before PSMA PET/CT? If yes, is this PSMA-specific or a general FDG-PET-derived precaution, and is ordinary walking acceptable?
- **Negative scan:** If no disease is seen, will that change treatment or only indicate disease is below detection?
- **Equivocal lesion:** Would follow-up MRI/CT, repeat imaging, biopsy, or multidisciplinary review be needed?
- **Radiation planning:** If recurrence is found, would the scan alter prostate-bed, pelvic-node, or metastasis-directed radiation fields?
- **Advanced disease:** If considering lutetium-177 PSMA therapy, do we need FDG PET or other imaging to detect PSMA-negative disease?
- **Monitoring:** How often would imaging be repeated, and would PSA/symptoms/CT/bone scan be more appropriate for routine follow-up?
- **Access/cost:** Is this scan reimbursed for my indication, and how quickly can it be performed without delaying necessary treatment?

## Repository interpretation

For this dashboard, PSMA PET/CT should be treated as a **decision-changing imaging tool**, not as a stand-alone diagnostic answer. The evidence is strongest for:

1. staging higher-risk disease before curative-intent treatment;
2. localizing biochemical recurrence when the result will guide salvage or systemic treatment;
3. selecting eligible advanced-disease patients for PSMA-targeted radioligand therapy.

The main uncertainty is not whether PSMA PET/CT sees more disease than older imaging—it often does—but whether each scan-driven management change improves outcomes for the individual patient. That makes the pre-scan clinical question essential.
