# Localized Treatment

## Scope

Research notes and filed articles for **localized prostate cancer treatment**, including active surveillance decision points, surgery, radiotherapy, brachytherapy, focal/partial-gland therapies, focal radiation boost, post-treatment recurrence strategy, treatment side effects, and patient-selection questions.

This category is for evidence that helps patients discuss which localized treatment strategy fits their risk group, tumour location, imaging/biopsy findings, life expectancy, urinary/sexual/bowel priorities, and tolerance for follow-up.

## Key patient questions

- Is my disease low, favourable intermediate, unfavourable intermediate, high risk, or locally advanced?
- Is the cancer unifocal/unilateral and MRI-visible, or multifocal/bilateral and better suited to whole-gland treatment?
- What are the benefits, harms, and uncertainties of surgery, external-beam radiotherapy, brachytherapy, focal radiation boost, or focal ablation?
- Is a treatment option mainstream standard care, guideline-supported, trial/registry-based, or experimental/private-market?
- What follow-up is required after localized treatment: PSA, MRI, biopsy, PSMA PET, salvage therapy planning?
- What should be discussed with a urologist, radiation oncologist, medical oncologist, radiologist, or focal-therapy centre?

## Evidence to prioritize

- Randomized trials and mature prospective studies of localized treatment outcomes
- Guideline statements from EAU, AUA/ASTRO, NCCN-equivalent bodies, ESTRO and specialist societies
- Comparative studies of surgery, radiation, active surveillance, focal therapy, and salvage treatment
- MRI/biopsy mapping studies relevant to focal therapy patient selection
- Functional-outcome studies: continence, erectile function, urinary symptoms, bowel toxicity, quality of life

## Cautions

!!! warning "Not medical advice"
    Localized-treatment notes are educational. They should not be used to select or delay prostate cancer treatment without specialist advice. Focal therapy in particular depends on high-quality MRI, targeted plus systematic or template biopsy, careful lesion mapping, risk group, lesion location, and willingness to undergo intensive follow-up.

Be especially cautious with:

- treating low-risk disease when active surveillance may be sufficient;
- undertreating unfavourable intermediate/high-risk or multifocal disease with a focal approach;
- relying on PSA alone after focal therapy;
- assuming a focal therapy preserves function without considering lesion location and ablation margins;
- comparing nonrandomized focal-therapy registry outcomes directly with radical prostatectomy or radiotherapy trials.

## Current evidence summary

| Topic | Current interpretation |
|---|---|
| Standard localized treatments | Radical prostatectomy, external-beam radiotherapy, brachytherapy, and active surveillance have the most established guideline frameworks. |
| Focal ablative therapy | HIFU, cryotherapy, IRE, laser, VTP and TULSA aim to treat the index lesion/region while preserving function. The 2026 European Urology HEAT/ICE update adds important 3477-patient UK registry evidence for focal HIFU/cryo, but major guidelines still generally frame focal ablation as investigational or best performed in trials/registries rather than proven equivalent to radical therapy. |
| Best focal-ablation candidates | Most plausible when clinically significant disease is MRI-visible, biopsy-confirmed, localized, and concentrated in a treatable region, with no meaningful contralateral high-grade disease. |
| Weak focal-ablation candidates | Multifocal/bilateral significant disease, high-volume/high-grade disease, MRI-invisible significant disease, suspected extracapsular extension, or unwillingness to undergo repeat MRI/biopsy surveillance. |
| Focal radiation boost | Different from focal ablation: the whole prostate is treated and the dominant lesion receives extra dose. It has stronger randomized evidence from FLAME for selected intermediate/high-risk radiotherapy candidates. |
| Follow-up after focal therapy | PSA is not enough. MRI and repeat biopsy are central because in-field residual cancer and out-of-field clinically significant cancer can occur. |
