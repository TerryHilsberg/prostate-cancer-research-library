    Weekly Prostate Cancer Literature & Guideline Scan — 2026-07-06

    Educational research support — not medical advice

    This scan is for patient-perspective education and research organization only. It is not a diagnosis, treatment recommendation, or substitute for care from a qualified clinician. Screening, biopsy, imaging, surgery, radiation, systemic therapy, and medication decisions can have important contraindications, harms, trade-offs, and monitoring needs; discuss applicability with a clinician who knows the clinical details.

    Scan scope

    Sources checked included PubMed/NCBI, EAU, NCCN, AUA, NICE, and configured repository sources for items published or newly visible in approximately the last 7–14 days. Items below were selected using the repository selection rubric; all scored ≥7.5 or were included as a guideline/safety watchlist item.

    Accepted items

    1) 2026 EAU Guidelines on Prostate Cancer — limited update

    • Citation/source: European Association of Urology. EAU Guidelines on Prostate Cancer. 2026 guideline page. https://uroweb.org/guidelines/prostate-cancer
    • Evidence type and population: International clinical practice guideline for prostate cancer screening/diagnosis, staging, localized treatment, recurrence, metastatic hormone-sensitive disease, and castration-resistant disease.
    • Category/categories: Guidelines & care pathways; screening/early detection; diagnostic workup; risk stratification/staging; active surveillance; localized treatment; advanced/recurrent disease; imaging & biomarkers.
    • Selection score: 9.5/10 — high source credibility and very high patient-decision relevance; guideline details require clinician interpretation.
    • Summary: The EAU page identifies the 2026 prostate cancer guideline as a limited update of the 2025 publication. Listed changes include revised recommendations or evidence summaries for MRI in biopsy indication/strategy, staging, active surveillance strategy, imaging in biochemical recurrence, second-line therapy after curative-intent treatment, first-line hormone-sensitive metastatic disease, and systemic treatment of castration-resistant disease. It also notes updates to EAU localized/locally advanced risk groups, a new table of externally validated risk calculators for clinically significant prostate cancer, changes to biopsy decision flow diagrams, and new sections on rare aggressive subtypes, micro-ultrasound biopsy indication, MRI-targeted biopsy in younger men, treatment-emergent neuroendocrine prostate cancer, and newer combination approaches in metastatic hormone-sensitive disease.
    • Main finding: This is a broad guideline-watchlist update rather than a single trial result; it signals that several patient decision points—MRI/biopsy decisions, staging, surveillance, recurrence imaging, and advanced-disease systemic therapy sequencing—should be checked against the current guideline text.
    • Patient relevance: Patients preparing for PSA/MRI/biopsy decisions, active surveillance, post-treatment recurrence workup, or metastatic treatment discussions may want to ask whether the clinician is using the most current guideline version and how the updated sections apply to their risk group.
    • Limitations: The public guideline is extensive and not a personalized treatment algorithm. Some recommendations may vary by country, access, imaging availability, reimbursement, and individual clinical risk.
    • Safety/clinical cautions: Do not change screening, biopsy, imaging, surveillance, radiation, surgery, or systemic therapy plans based only on a guideline summary. Use it to frame clinician questions.
    • Repository impact: Changes category pages by adding a guideline-watch note under Guidelines & Care Pathways, Diagnostic Workup, Imaging & Biomarkers, Localized Treatment, and Advanced & Recurrent Disease. No direct patient decision map rewrite in this run.

    2) ESTRO consensus recommendations on focal boost radiotherapy

    • Citation/source: Willigenburg T, Draulans C, Pos FJ, Seibert TM, Syndikus I, Henry A, et al. How to focal boost in prostate cancer radiotherapy: ESTRO clinical practice consensus recommendations. Radiotherapy and Oncology. 2026 Aug. DOI: 10.1016/j.radonc.2026.111642. PMID: 42399065.
    • Evidence type and population: Clinical practice consensus/review drawing on focal dose-escalation evidence, including FLAME trial context; relevant mainly to intermediate- and high-risk localized prostate cancer where definitive radiotherapy is being considered.
    • Category/categories: Localized treatment; therapies; side effects & quality of life; imaging & biomarkers.
    • Selection score: 8.6/10 — consensus recommendations from a major radiation oncology society with direct treatment-planning relevance.
    • Summary: ESTRO provides practical “how-to” recommendations for focal boosting of intraprostatic tumor areas using external beam radiotherapy or brachytherapy. The abstract notes that whole-gland dose escalation can increase side effects, while isotolerated focal boosting tested in FLAME improved biochemical progression-free survival without a significant side-effect increase.
    • Main finding: Focal boosting is moving from trial evidence toward practical implementation guidance for selected patients receiving definitive radiotherapy.
    • Patient relevance: A patient comparing radiation approaches may ask whether a visible dominant lesion on MRI/other imaging makes focal boost relevant, what evidence supports it for the patient’s risk group, and how urinary, bowel, and sexual side-effect risks are handled in planning.
    • Limitations: Consensus guidance is not the same as individualized treatment proof for every patient. Applicability depends on tumor visibility, imaging quality, planning expertise, anatomy, baseline urinary/bowel/sexual function, and local technology.
    • Safety/clinical cautions: Radiation dose escalation can affect urinary, bowel, and sexual outcomes. Discuss expected benefits, uncertainties, toxicity constraints, alternatives, and center experience with a radiation oncologist.
    • Repository impact: Changes Localized Treatment category page with a note to consider focal boost as a clinician-discussion topic for selected radiotherapy candidates; no decision map rewrite.

    3) PSMA PET/CT-targeted biopsy after negative or equivocal MRI — FUPERMAN study

    • Citation/source: Farolfi A, Sgro CMP, Brusa I, Dall'Olio D, Romei F, Presutti M, et al. PSMA PET/CT-Targeted Biopsy in Men with Negative or Equivocal Multiparametric MRI and Exploratory Dynamic Total-Body PET: The FUPERMAN Study. Journal of Nuclear Medicine. 2026 Jul 02. DOI: 10.2967/jnumed.126.272108. PMID: 42392696.
    • Evidence type and population: Prospective single-center diagnostic study; 63 men with PI-RADS 2–3 mpMRI underwent [68Ga]Ga-PSMA-11 PET/CT followed by transperineal biopsy; small dynamic total-body PET subset.
    • Category/categories: Diagnostic workup; imaging & biomarkers; biopsy; risk stratification/staging.
    • Selection score: 8.0/10 — prospective and highly relevant to MRI-negative/equivocal diagnostic uncertainty, but small and single-center.
    • Summary: In men with negative or equivocal mpMRI, PSMA PET/CT-targeted biopsy detected clinically significant prostate cancer more often than systematic biopsy in the abstract (37% vs 21%; P=0.021). Combined PET-targeted plus systematic biopsy detected clinically significant cancer in 41%. Reported PET-targeted biopsy sensitivity was 96% and negative predictive value was 95%, but specificity was 49%.
    • Main finding: PSMA PET/CT may help identify clinically significant cancers missed or under-characterized by mpMRI in selected men, but the approach needs larger multicenter validation before it should be treated as a routine first-line substitute for established MRI-biopsy pathways.
    • Patient relevance: For a patient with persistently concerning PSA/risk features but PI-RADS 2–3 MRI, this study may support asking whether additional imaging, repeat MRI, targeted transperineal biopsy, or standard systematic biopsy is most appropriate.
    • Limitations: Small sample; single center; specialist PET availability; uncertain reimbursement and generalizability; high sensitivity but modest specificity may produce false positives or additional procedures.
    • Safety/clinical cautions: PET findings should not be treated as a diagnosis without pathology. Biopsy carries infection, bleeding, urinary retention, pain, and false-negative/false-positive trade-offs; decisions need clinician review.
    • Repository impact: Changes Diagnostic Workup and Imaging & Biomarkers category pages with a watch note; no decision map rewrite yet.

    4) Hypokalemia risk with abiraterone/enzalutamide-class therapy

    • Citation/source: Lou Y, Fan D, Chen T, Xiong K, et al. The impact of abiraterone and enzalutamide on hypokalemia incidence in patients with prostate cancer: a systematic review and meta-analysis. BMC Cancer. 2026 Jul 02. DOI: 10.1007/s11864-024-01215-2. PMID: 42387420.
    • Evidence type and population: Systematic review and meta-analysis of 8 randomized controlled trials / 9 reports including 9,298 patients with advanced prostate cancer receiving androgen receptor axis-targeted therapies.
    • Category/categories: Advanced/recurrent disease; therapies; side effects & quality of life; safety monitoring.
    • Selection score: 8.7/10 — meta-analysis of randomized trials with direct medication safety relevance.
    • Summary: Compared with controls, androgen receptor axis-targeted therapy was associated with higher all-grade hypokalemia risk (RR 3.11, 95% CI 1.88–5.15). The signal was elevated with abiraterone (RR 3.25) and abiraterone plus enzalutamide (RR 4.62), but not clearly with enzalutamide alone (RR 1.16). Severe grade ≥3 hypokalemia was also higher overall (RR 4.54), with a stronger but less certain signal in combination regimens.
    • Main finding: Potassium monitoring and proactive management are especially important when abiraterone-containing treatment is used.
    • Patient relevance: Patients considering or receiving abiraterone-based therapy can ask how often potassium, blood pressure, liver tests, edema/fluid retention, and steroid-related risks will be monitored, and what symptoms should trigger urgent contact.
    • Limitations: High heterogeneity for all-grade hypokalemia; regimen and population differences; limited data for some combinations; the DOI metadata should be verified at source because PubMed-reported DOI/journal metadata can occasionally lag or contain indexing errors.
    • Safety/clinical cautions: Hypokalemia can be clinically important, particularly with heart disease, arrhythmia risk, diuretics, kidney disease, vomiting/diarrhea, or interacting medications. Do not start/stop abiraterone, prednisone, enzalutamide, supplements, or potassium replacement without clinician direction.
    • Repository impact: Changes Advanced & Recurrent Disease and Side Effects & Quality of Life category pages by highlighting potassium monitoring in abiraterone-containing therapy.

    5) Metastasis-directed ablative radiotherapy in oligometastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer

    • Citation/source: Hölscher T, Lohaus F, Koi L, Kotzerke J, Hoberück S, Thomas C, et al. Ablative radiotherapy in castration-resistant prostate cancer. BJU International. 2026 Jul 02. DOI: 10.1111/bju.70368. PMID: 42389925. ClinicalTrials.gov: NCT04141709.
    • Evidence type and population: Planned interim analysis of a small single-center randomized phase II trial; 30 patients with up to five PSMA-positive bone or lymph-node metastases and PSA progression during ADT or ADT plus androgen-receptor targeted therapy.
    • Category/categories: Advanced/recurrent disease; PSMA PET/imaging; localized/metastasis-directed radiotherapy; shared decision-making.
    • Selection score: 7.8/10 — randomized signal and patient-relevant question, but interim, underpowered, and single-center.
    • Summary: Patients were randomized 2:1 to metastasis-directed ablative radiotherapy or observation without immediate systemic therapy change. PSA progression within 1 year occurred in 44% of the metastasis-directed therapy group versus 75% of observation, which was not statistically significant (P=0.14). Median time to PSA progression was longer with metastasis-directed therapy (12.4 vs 2.9 months; P=0.03). The study did not meet criteria to stop early and plans multicenter continuation.
    • Main finding: Metastasis-directed radiotherapy in oligometastatic CRPC remains promising but not practice-settling; it may delay PSA progression in selected patients but needs larger validation.
    • Patient relevance: A patient with a small number of PSMA-PET-positive lesions after castration resistance develops may ask whether local treatment to metastases is appropriate, whether it could delay systemic therapy change, and whether trial participation is available.
    • Limitations: Very small interim sample; single center; PSA progression rather than survival or quality-of-life endpoint; not statistically significant for the primary 1-year progression proportion.
    • Safety/clinical cautions: Ablative radiotherapy can cause site-specific toxicity and should be balanced against systemic therapy options, symptoms, disease tempo, lesion location, prior radiation, and goals of care.
    • Repository impact: Adds an Advanced & Recurrent Disease watch note; no decision map rewrite.

    6) Post-prostatectomy adjuvant radiation vs initial observation practice pattern

    • Citation/source: Demus T, Ginsburg KB, Meah S, Daignault-Newton S, Labardee C, Johnson A, et al. Adjuvant Radiation Therapy Versus Initial Observation for Patients After Prostatectomy with Adverse Pathology: Have Findings from 3 Randomized Clinical Trials Influenced Clinical Practice? Urology Practice. 2026 Jul 02. DOI: 10.1097/UPJ.0000000000001055. PMID: 42391301.
    • Evidence type and population: Retrospective registry analysis from the Michigan Urological Surgery Improvement Collaborative; 9,144 post-prostatectomy patients with adverse pathology and postoperative PSA ≤0.1, approximating prior RCT inclusion criteria.
    • Category/categories: Localized treatment; recurrence; shared decision-making; quality of life.
    • Selection score: 7.7/10 — large real-world registry linked to prior randomized evidence; observational and mainly practice-pattern evidence.
    • Summary: Use of adjuvant radiation decreased after publication of randomized trials supporting initial observation with early salvage radiotherapy for many patients with adverse pathology after prostatectomy. Adjuvant radiation use fell from 8.2% before 2020 to 2.7% after 2020; adjusted analyses found lower use after 2020.
    • Main finding: Practice appears to be moving toward observation with PSA monitoring and early salvage radiation rather than automatic adjuvant radiation for many post-prostatectomy patients who have adverse pathology but undetectable/very low PSA.
    • Patient relevance: Patients with positive margins or pT3/pT4 features after surgery can ask about PSA monitoring frequency, thresholds for early salvage radiation, and whether immediate adjuvant radiation offers enough benefit to justify urinary/bowel/sexual side-effect risks in their specific case.
    • Limitations: Observational practice-pattern analysis; does not itself re-randomize patients or prove the best option for every adverse-pathology scenario.
    • Safety/clinical cautions: Observation is not “no care”; it requires planned PSA monitoring and a clear salvage-radiation trigger. Discuss individualized pathology, PSA kinetics, genomic classifiers if used, and radiation side-effect trade-offs.
    • Repository impact: Adds a Localized Treatment / Shared Decision-Making note; no decision map rewrite because the finding reinforces existing RCT-based practice rather than changing it.

    Overall decision-map implications for this week

    • No immediate patient decision-map rewrite was made. The main practical change is a set of category watch notes: updated EAU guideline sections should be checked during clinician conversations; focal boost radiotherapy is an increasingly structured option for selected radiotherapy candidates; PSMA PET-targeted biopsy after equivocal/negative MRI remains investigational but relevant to diagnostic uncertainty; abiraterone-containing therapy requires careful potassium monitoring; metastasis-directed therapy in oligometastatic CRPC remains promising but not definitive.
